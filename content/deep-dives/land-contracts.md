# The Land Contract

### When the Seller Is the Bank — What You're Actually Signing, What It Actually Costs, and How to Protect Yourself

---

## What a Land Contract Actually Is

Someone offers you a house. You can't get a mortgage — maybe your credit is wrecked, maybe you're self-employed and can't prove income the way banks want, maybe the property is unconventional and no lender will touch it. But this seller says: "I'll be the bank. You pay me monthly, and when you've paid it all off, I'll give you the deed."

That's a land contract. Also called a contract for deed, installment land contract, or bond for deed depending on which state you're in. The name changes. The structure doesn't.

Here's what's actually happening:

1. **You agree to buy the property at a set price.** Just like a mortgage.
2. **You make a down payment.** Usually 5-20% of the purchase price.
3. **You make monthly payments to the seller.** Principal and interest, just like a mortgage payment — except the seller gets the check, not a bank.
4. **The seller keeps the deed.** This is the part that changes everything. You don't own the property until you've made every payment. You have "equitable interest," which is a legal term that sounds more protective than it is.
5. **When you've paid in full — or refinanced into a traditional mortgage — the seller transfers the deed to you.** That's when you actually own it.

On the surface, it sounds like a mortgage with different paperwork. It is not a mortgage with different paperwork. It is a fundamentally different legal arrangement that shifts almost all the risk to you.

### How It Differs From a Mortgage

With a traditional mortgage:
- **You get the deed at closing.** You own the property from day one. The bank has a lien on it (they can foreclose if you don't pay), but you're the legal owner.
- **The bank is regulated.** Federal and state laws govern what banks can charge, how they can foreclose, and what disclosures they must make.
- **You get title insurance.** This protects you if someone else has a claim on the property.
- **The property is appraised.** A professional confirms the home is worth what you're paying.
- **There's an inspection contingency.** You can walk away if the property has serious problems.
- **Foreclosure takes time.** If you can't pay, the bank has to go through a legal process that can take 6-18 months. During that time, you can negotiate, sell, or find alternatives.

With a land contract:
- **You don't get the deed.** The seller keeps it until you've paid in full.
- **The seller is not regulated like a bank.** In most states, land contracts exist in a regulatory gray area. Fewer disclosure requirements. Fewer consumer protections.
- **There's no title insurance by default.** Unless you insist on it and pay for it.
- **There's no required appraisal.** You might be paying $150,000 for a house worth $90,000.
- **There's no required inspection.** The roof might be failing. The foundation might be cracked. You won't know unless you pay for your own inspection.
- **Forfeiture, not foreclosure.** In many states, if you miss payments on a land contract, the seller can reclaim the property through forfeiture — a process that can take as little as 30-60 days. You lose the property and every dollar you've put into it.

Read that last bullet again. Under a mortgage, if you default after paying $50,000 in principal, you still have equity. You can sell the house, pay off the lender, and keep what's left. Under many land contracts, if you default after paying $50,000, you get nothing. The seller keeps the property, keeps your down payment, keeps every monthly payment you made, and can sell it to the next buyer on another land contract.

That's not a technicality. That's the whole game.

### Why Land Contracts Exist

Land contracts aren't new. They've been around for over a century, and they fill a real gap:

**Buyers who can't qualify for traditional financing.** This includes people with credit scores below 580, self-employed borrowers who can't document income to bank standards, people buying in rural areas where banks won't lend, and buyers of unconventional properties (mobile homes on land, properties needing significant work, mixed-use buildings).

**Sellers who want a higher price and steady income.** A seller who finances the purchase can often get a higher sale price, charge above-market interest, and receive steady monthly income. If the buyer defaults, they get the property back and can sell it again.

**Properties that banks won't touch.** Some properties — those with title issues, needed repairs, unusual zoning, or in areas with declining values — can't get traditional financing. Land contracts become the only way to transact.

**How common are they?** Hard to pin down exact numbers because land contracts aren't tracked the way mortgages are (no centralized reporting). But they're concentrated in:
- The Midwest (Michigan, Ohio, Indiana, Minnesota, Wisconsin)
- Rural areas nationwide
- Lower-priced properties (under $200,000)
- Urban areas with distressed housing stock (Detroit, Cleveland, St. Louis)

Studies have estimated that hundreds of thousands of families are living in homes purchased through land contracts at any given time. In some neighborhoods, land contracts outnumber traditional mortgages.

## How the Money Works

This is where land contracts stop being an "alternative financing option" and start being an expensive one. The math is not in your favor, and the structure is designed so that you don't realize it until you're years in.

### Typical Terms

Here's what a land contract usually looks like compared to a traditional mortgage:

```
TYPICAL LAND CONTRACT vs. TRADITIONAL MORTGAGE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                      LAND CONTRACT    MORTGAGE (FHA)
Down payment:         10-20%           3.5%
Interest rate:        8-12%            6-7%
Term (until payoff):  5-10 years       30 years
Amortization:         20-30 years      30 years
Balloon payment:      YES              No
Title insurance:      Rarely           Always
Buyer gets deed:      At payoff        At closing
Default process:      Forfeiture       Foreclosure
Consumer protections: Minimal          Extensive
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

Notice the mismatch between "term" and "amortization." This is the trap most buyers don't see.

### The Amortization Trick

Your monthly payment is calculated based on a 20-30 year schedule — so it looks affordable. If you're buying a $150,000 house with $15,000 down and a 9% interest rate amortized over 30 years, your monthly payment is about **$1,086**.

That sounds manageable. Maybe tight, but manageable.

But the contract term is only 5 years. After 5 years of making those $1,086 payments, you don't own the house. You owe a **balloon payment** — the entire remaining balance. On this example, that balloon is roughly **$129,000**.

Let that number land. You've been paying $1,086 every month for five years. That's $65,160 in total payments. Of that, roughly $60,000 went to interest and only about $6,000 went to principal. After five years of payments, you still owe $129,000, and it's all due at once.

Your options at that point:
1. **Refinance into a traditional mortgage.** This only works if your credit has improved enough to qualify, the property appraises high enough, and you can afford closing costs.
2. **Negotiate an extension with the seller.** The seller has zero obligation to extend. If the property has appreciated, they may prefer to take it back and sell it for more.
3. **Lose the house.** If you can't pay the balloon or refinance, the contract ends. The seller keeps the property and everything you've paid.

### The Cost Comparison: Real Numbers

Let's run the actual math on a $150,000 property:

```
$150,000 PROPERTY — LAND CONTRACT vs. MORTGAGE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                         LAND CONTRACT    30-YR MORTGAGE
Purchase price:          $150,000         $150,000
Down payment:            $15,000 (10%)    $15,000 (10%)
Amount financed:         $135,000         $135,000
Interest rate:           9%               7%
Monthly payment:         $1,086           $898
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

AFTER 5 YEARS:
Total payments made:     $65,160          $53,880
Interest paid:           $59,199          $45,195
Principal paid:          $5,961           $8,685
Balance remaining:       $129,039         $126,315
Equity built (w/ down):  $20,961          $23,685

EXTRA COST OF LAND CONTRACT OVER 5 YEARS:
Extra payments:          $11,280
Extra interest:          $14,004
Less equity built:       $2,724
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The land contract costs you **$11,280 more** in payments over five years. You build **$2,724 less** in equity. And at the end, you face a $129,000 balloon that the mortgage buyer never has to deal with.

That $11,280 is the premium you're paying for seller financing. It's not a small premium. On a $135,000 loan, that's an 8.4% surcharge over five years, paid entirely in extra interest.

And this is at 9%. Some land contracts charge 10%, 11%, 12%. At 12%, your monthly payment jumps to $1,389, your total payments over 5 years hit $83,340, and you've paid $48,000 more than the mortgage buyer — on the same house.

### Property Taxes and Insurance: The Hidden Risk

Here's a question most land contract buyers don't think to ask: **Who's paying the property taxes and insurance?**

The contract will specify this, but the answer creates risk either way:

**If you pay taxes and insurance directly:** You're spending $200-$600/month on top of your contract payment for costs that protect a property you don't legally own yet. If the seller defaults on their mortgage or goes bankrupt, your tax and insurance payments don't protect you.

**If the seller pays them from your payment:** You have no way to verify they're actually making those payments. If the seller pockets your tax money and doesn't pay, the county puts a tax lien on the property. Tax liens take priority over everything — including your land contract. The property can be sold at a tax sale, and you lose everything.

**If nobody's paying insurance:** The property is uninsured. If it burns down, you're still obligated to make payments on a pile of ashes you don't own.

The correct arrangement is an **escrow account** managed by a neutral third party (a title company or attorney). Your payment goes into escrow, which distributes the correct amounts to taxes, insurance, and the seller. This costs a small monthly fee and is worth every cent.

## What Can Go Wrong (Buyer's Risk List)

Land contracts concentrate nearly all the risk on the buyer. Here's the complete list of what can go wrong, because you need to know every single one before you sign anything.

### 1. No Deed = No Ownership Rights

Until you've paid in full and the deed is transferred, you are not the legal owner. You're occupying someone else's property under a contract. This means:

- You can't sell the property without the seller's cooperation
- You can't take out a home equity loan
- You can't make improvements and guarantee you'll benefit from them
- In some states, you have fewer rights than a tenant
- Your "ownership" exists only as long as you make every payment on time

### 2. The Seller's Existing Mortgage (Due-on-Sale Risk)

This is the land contract risk that most buyers have never heard of, and it can destroy the entire deal overnight.

Many sellers still have a mortgage on the property they're selling you via land contract. They're collecting your payments and using that money to make their mortgage payments. Sounds fine — until you learn about the **due-on-sale clause**.

Almost every mortgage contains a due-on-sale clause. It says: if the borrower sells or transfers the property, the full mortgage balance becomes due immediately. A land contract is a sale. If the seller's bank discovers the land contract (and they often do, through property tax records or insurance changes), they can demand the full mortgage balance immediately.

What happens next:
1. The seller can't pay the full balance (that's why they did a land contract instead of selling normally)
2. The bank forecloses on the seller's mortgage
3. The bank takes the property
4. You lose the house and every dollar you've put into it
5. You have no recourse against the bank — you weren't party to that mortgage

This isn't theoretical. It happens. The seller's bank doesn't care about your land contract. They have a first-position lien and the legal right to enforce it.

### 3. Forfeiture: Faster and Harsher Than Foreclosure

If you miss payments on a mortgage, the bank has to foreclose. Foreclosure is a legal process with timelines, notices, rights to cure, and (in many states) a redemption period. It typically takes 6-18 months. During that time, you can catch up on payments, negotiate a modification, sell the property, or find alternatives.

Land contract forfeiture is different. In many states, forfeiture requires only:
- Written notice that you're in default
- A cure period as short as 30 days (some states don't require any cure period)
- If you don't cure within the period, the contract terminates
- The seller keeps the property, your down payment, and all payments made

No auction. No equity recovery. No redemption period. You're out, and everything you paid is gone.

Some states have modernized their land contract laws to provide more protections — Michigan, for instance, requires judicial foreclosure for land contracts where the buyer has paid 50% or more of the purchase price, or has been on the contract for 5+ years. But many states haven't. And even in states with better protections, the burden is on you to know and assert your rights.

### 4. Seller Dies or Goes Bankrupt

What happens to your land contract if the seller dies? The contract passes to the seller's estate and heirs. The heirs may:
- Not know about the land contract
- Not want to honor it
- Demand full payment
- Contest the contract in probate court

What if the seller files for bankruptcy? The property becomes part of the bankruptcy estate. A bankruptcy trustee may:
- Reject the land contract as an executory contract
- Sell the property to satisfy the seller's creditors
- Force a sale that extinguishes your interest

In both cases, your only protection is having the contract **recorded with the county** (more on this below). An unrecorded land contract may not survive the seller's death or bankruptcy.

### 5. Hidden Liens and Title Problems

When you buy with a mortgage, the lender requires a title search and title insurance. This reveals any liens, judgments, easements, or claims against the property. If there's a $30,000 IRS lien on the property, you find out before closing.

With a land contract, there's no required title search. The property you're buying could have:
- Tax liens from unpaid property taxes
- Mechanic's liens from unpaid contractors
- Judgment liens from the seller's lawsuits
- Child support liens
- IRS or state tax liens
- An existing mortgage you didn't know about
- Easements that affect how you can use the property
- Boundary disputes

Any of these can make the title unmarketable, meaning even if you pay off the land contract, the seller can't deliver a clean deed. And if there are liens that take priority over your contract, those creditors can force a sale of the property.

### 6. No Required Inspections or Appraisals

Banks require inspections and appraisals to protect their investment. Without a bank, nobody requires anything. This means:

**You could overpay dramatically.** The seller says it's worth $150,000. Maybe it appraised at $90,000 last year. Without an appraisal, you'll never know. And when you try to refinance in 5 years, the appraiser says it's worth $95,000, and no bank will lend you $129,000 on a $95,000 house.

**The property could have serious defects.** Foundation issues, mold, faulty wiring, failing septic system, roof at end of life, water damage, termites. Without an inspection, you discover these after you've signed and started paying. You're now paying $1,086/month for a house that needs $40,000 in repairs — on a property you don't own.

### 7. State-by-State Protections Vary Wildly

There is no federal land contract law. Your protections depend entirely on your state, and the variation is enormous:

- **Minnesota, Michigan, Ohio:** Relatively strong buyer protections, including mandatory cure periods and sometimes judicial process for forfeiture
- **Indiana, Iowa:** Moderate protections with defined cure periods
- **Texas:** Specific disclosure requirements and protections under Property Code Chapter 5
- **Many other states:** Minimal statutory protections — the contract terms govern, and the contract was written by the seller

If you're considering a land contract, you need to know your state's specific laws. Not what the seller tells you. Not what you read in a general article. Your state's actual statutes. An attorney can tell you in one consultation.

## Protecting Yourself: The Non-Negotiables

If you're going to do a land contract — and there are situations where it makes sense — these aren't suggestions. These are requirements. Skip any one of them and you're gambling with the largest purchase of your life.

### 1. Get a Title Search ($200-$400)

Before you sign anything, pay a title company to search the property's title. This will reveal:
- Whether the seller actually owns the property
- Any existing mortgages on the property
- Any liens, judgments, or encumbrances
- Any easements or restrictions
- Whether the property taxes are current

If the title search reveals an existing mortgage, you need to know the balance, the payment status, and whether the mortgage has a due-on-sale clause. If the seller refuses to disclose this information, walk away.

If you can afford it, get **title insurance** as well. This protects you if a title defect emerges later that the search missed. Cost: $500-$1,500 depending on the property value. Some title companies won't insure land contract interests — if yours will, take it.

### 2. Record the Contract With the County ($50-$100)

This is the single most important protection you can get, and it costs almost nothing.

When you record the land contract with the county recorder's office, it creates a public record of your interest in the property. This means:
- Future buyers or lenders can see that you have a claim
- The seller can't sell the property to someone else without dealing with your interest
- If the seller dies, the estate can't claim they didn't know about the contract
- If the seller goes bankrupt, the trustee can see your recorded interest
- It provides evidence of the contract terms if there's ever a dispute

**If the seller refuses to let you record the contract, do not sign.** There is no legitimate reason for a seller to oppose recording. The only reason to keep a land contract unrecorded is to preserve the ability to screw you later.

### 3. Verify the Seller's Mortgage Situation

If the seller has an existing mortgage on the property:
- Get written proof of the current mortgage balance
- Get copies of recent mortgage statements showing payments are current
- Include a contract clause requiring the seller to provide proof of ongoing mortgage payments (quarterly or annually)
- Consider requiring the seller to use an escrow agent who pays the seller's mortgage directly from your payments

If the seller's mortgage has a due-on-sale clause (it almost certainly does), you need to understand the risk. The seller's lender could call the loan at any time. You're betting that the lender won't notice or won't enforce. That's a bet, not a plan.

### 4. Get the Property Inspected ($300-$500)

Hire a licensed home inspector. Get the full inspection — foundation, roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, water heater, attic, crawlspace, everything. If the property has a well and septic system, those need separate inspections.

If the inspection reveals major issues, you have three options:
1. Negotiate a lower price that accounts for repair costs
2. Require the seller to make repairs before you sign
3. Walk away

Option 3 is often the right answer. If you're buying on a land contract because you can't qualify for a mortgage, you probably can't afford $15,000 in unexpected repairs either.

### 5. Get an Attorney to Review the Contract ($500-$1,000)

Not the seller's attorney. Your attorney. One who knows real estate law in your state and has reviewed land contracts before.

The attorney will:
- Explain your rights and the seller's rights under your state's law
- Identify unfavorable clauses (and there will be some)
- Negotiate better terms on your behalf
- Ensure the contract includes all necessary protections
- Verify that the contract complies with state law

"I can't afford a lawyer" is the most dangerous sentence in land contract transactions. You're about to commit to paying $60,000-$200,000 for a property. Spending $500-$1,000 to have a professional review the contract isn't optional. It's the minimum.

### 6. Required Contract Clauses

Your contract should include all of the following. If the seller won't agree to these, you need to seriously reconsider the deal:

**Warranty deed at payoff.** The contract must specify that the seller will deliver a warranty deed (not a quitclaim deed) when you've paid in full. A warranty deed guarantees the seller has clear title. A quitclaim deed transfers only whatever interest the seller has — which might be nothing if there are title problems.

**Right to cure period.** If you miss a payment, you get a defined period (at least 30 days, preferably 60) to catch up before the seller can take any action. The contract should specify that forfeiture cannot begin until the cure period expires.

**Notice requirements.** The seller must provide written notice of any default, sent to your current address by certified mail. No "we left a note on the door" or "we sent a text."

**Escrow for taxes and insurance.** A neutral third party collects your payment and distributes funds to taxes, insurance, and the seller. This prevents the seller from pocketing tax money.

**Right to prepay without penalty.** You should be able to pay off the contract early — or make extra principal payments — without a prepayment penalty. This is essential for your refinance exit strategy.

**Seller's obligation to maintain clear title.** The seller must not place any new liens or encumbrances on the property during the contract period.

**Right to assign or sell your interest.** If you need to get out of the contract, you should be able to sell your interest to another buyer (with reasonable conditions).

**Maintenance and improvement provisions.** Clarify who's responsible for maintenance and who benefits from improvements. If you put a new roof on a house you don't own, the contract should protect that investment.

### 7. The Escrow Arrangement

Set up an escrow account through a title company or attorney. Here's how it works:

1. You make your monthly payment to the escrow agent
2. The escrow agent pays property taxes to the county
3. The escrow agent pays homeowner's insurance premiums
4. If the seller has a mortgage, the escrow agent pays the seller's mortgage
5. The remaining amount goes to the seller
6. The escrow agent keeps records of all payments

Cost: typically $25-$50/month or a flat annual fee. This is non-negotiable protection.

```

──────────────────────────────────────────

Recording the contract:          $50-$100

──────────────────────────────────────────

──────────────────────────────────────────
```

On a $150,000 purchase, that's 0.7-1.3% of the purchase price. This is the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy. If the seller objects to any of these costs, that tells you something important about the seller.

## When a Land Contract Makes Sense (and When It Doesn't)

Land contracts aren't inherently predatory. They're a tool. Like any tool, they can be used well or used badly. Here's how to tell the difference.

### When It Can Make Sense

**You're rebuilding credit with a clear refinance path.** Your credit score is 550 today, but you have a stable income, you're paying down debt, and you have a realistic plan to reach 620-640 within 2-3 years. A land contract gives you a place to live while you rebuild, and you refinance into an FHA mortgage when your score qualifies. The key word is "plan" — a specific, measurable, time-bound plan, not a vague hope.

**Rural property with no bank interest.** Some properties in rural areas can't get traditional financing — the values are too low, the properties are too unconventional, or there simply aren't lenders active in the area. USDA loans are an option for some of these (see below), but for properties that don't qualify for any program, a land contract from a reasonable seller may be the only path.

**Family sale.** Buying from a parent, grandparent, or other family member who's offering fair terms — market-rate price, reasonable interest, willingness to record the contract and transfer a warranty deed. Family land contracts can work well because the seller genuinely wants you to succeed, not just to collect payments until you default.

**Bridge financing.** You're 6-12 months away from qualifying for a mortgage (closing on a sale of another property, waiting for tax returns to show self-employment income, finishing a bankruptcy waiting period). A short-term land contract bridges that gap. The key: it's short-term with a defined exit.

### When It Doesn't Make Sense

**You have no realistic plan to refinance.** If your credit score is 480, you have no stable income, and you have no plan to change either of those facts within the contract term, a land contract isn't a stepping stone — it's a ticking clock. You'll make payments for 5 years, build minimal equity, and lose everything when the balloon comes due.

**The interest rate is above 10-12%.** At rates above 10%, the cost premium over a traditional mortgage becomes extreme. At 12% on $135,000, you're paying $1,389/month and almost none of it goes to principal. In the first year, you pay $16,668 and only $534 goes to principal. The rest — $16,134 — is pure interest income for the seller. Some states cap land contract interest rates. Many don't.

**The seller won't allow a title search.** "Trust me, the title is clean." No. If the seller objects to a $300 title search, the title isn't clean. Walk away.

**The seller won't record the contract.** There is no legitimate reason for this. An unrecorded land contract leaves you with almost no legal protection. Walk away.

**The property needs major repairs.** If the house needs a new roof ($8,000-$15,000), foundation work ($5,000-$25,000), or major systems replacement — and you're already stretching to make the land contract payment — you're buying a money pit you don't even own. The seller is transferring their repair liability to you while keeping the deed.

**The purchase price is inflated.** Without an appraisal, you won't know. Get one anyway ($300-$500). If the seller is charging $150,000 for a house that appraises at $100,000, you're paying a 50% premium. When you try to refinance, no bank will lend $129,000 on a $100,000 house. You'll need to bring the difference in cash — which you probably don't have.

**You'd qualify for an FHA, USDA, or VA loan.** If you can qualify for any of these programs, there is no reason to do a land contract. None. These programs exist specifically for buyers with limited credit or down payment capacity, and they provide vastly better protections.

### The Refinance Exit: Your Actual Plan

If you sign a land contract, your goal from day one is to get out of it. Not at some vague future date — within a specific timeframe, using a specific plan.

**Your refinance checklist:**

```
REFINANCE READINESS CHECKLIST
──────────────────────────────────────────────
□ Target credit score: 620+ (FHA) or 640+ (conventional)
□ Current credit score: ____
□ Monthly credit score improvement plan:
  □ All current bills paid on time
  □ Credit card utilization below 30%
  □ No new credit applications
  □ Dispute any errors on credit reports
□ Debt-to-income ratio below 43%
□ 2 years of documented income (for self-employed)
□ Property will appraise at or above loan amount
□ Savings for refinance closing costs ($3,000-$8,000)
□ Target refinance date: _______________
──────────────────────────────────────────────
```

If you can't fill in every line of this checklist, you're not ready for a land contract. You're ready to keep renting while you build the foundation for a mortgage.

### Credit Rebuilding Paths

For a deeper dive on how credit scores actually work and how to build yours deliberately, see [The Credit Score Contract](/deep-dives/credit-scores/).

Here are the loan programs you're working toward:

**FHA Loans**
- Minimum credit score: 580 (3.5% down) or 500 (10% down)
- Down payment: 3.5-10%
- Interest rate: market rate (currently 6-7%)
- Mortgage insurance required (MIP): 0.55%/year for the life of the loan
- Available for most property types

**USDA Loans**
- Minimum credit score: 640 (flexible with manual underwriting)
- Down payment: 0%
- Interest rate: market rate, often slightly below conventional
- Property must be in USDA-eligible area (more areas qualify than you'd think — check the USDA eligibility map)
- Income limits apply (varies by county)
- Guarantee fee: 1% upfront + 0.35%/year

**VA Loans (for eligible veterans)**
- No minimum credit score (most lenders want 620+)
- Down payment: 0%
- No mortgage insurance
- Funding fee: 1.25-3.3% (can be rolled into loan)
- See [Veterans Transition](/deep-dives/veterans/) for the full picture

**Conventional Loans**
- Minimum credit score: 620
- Down payment: 3-20%
- PMI required below 20% down (drops off at 80% LTV)
- Best rates for 740+ credit scores

### Rent-to-Own: The Land Contract's Cousin

Rent-to-own (lease-option) arrangements are similar to land contracts but with key differences:

- You're a **tenant**, not an equitable owner
- A portion of your rent is credited toward a future purchase (typically 15-25%)
- You have the **option** to buy at a preset price at the end of the lease
- If you don't buy, you lose the rent credits but you're not in forfeiture — you just move out
- Tenant protections apply (eviction process, habitability requirements)

Rent-to-own can be less risky than a land contract because you maintain tenant rights and don't face forfeiture of accumulated equity. But rent-to-own has its own problems — above-market rent, non-refundable option fees, and the risk that you won't qualify to buy at the end.

Neither is a substitute for a real mortgage. Both are bridges. The question is whether the bridge leads somewhere.

For a comprehensive look at all the costs involved in buying a home through any method, see [The Homeownership Contract](/deep-dives/homeownership/).

### Red Flags: Walk Away If Any of These

These aren't yellow flags. These aren't "proceed with caution." If you encounter any of the following, the deal is bad and the seller knows it:

- **Seller refuses to allow a title search.** The title isn't clean.
- **Seller refuses to record the contract.** They want the ability to sell the property out from under you.
- **Seller refuses to disclose their existing mortgage.** They're hiding a due-on-sale risk.
- **Interest rate above 12%.** This is extraction, not financing.
- **No cure period for missed payments.** The seller wants the right to take the property back the moment you stumble.
- **Quitclaim deed instead of warranty deed.** The seller isn't willing to guarantee clear title.
- **Seller is a company that buys and resells properties on land contracts.** These operations buy distressed properties at auction for $20,000-$40,000 and sell them on land contracts for $80,000-$120,000. The terms are predatory by design. The business model depends on buyer default.
- **"No lawyers needed" or "simple agreement between us."** When someone tells you not to get a lawyer, you need a lawyer.
- **Pressure to sign quickly.** "Someone else is interested" or "this deal won't last." Good deals survive a week of due diligence. Scams don't.
- **Purchase price is more than 10% above recent comparable sales.** The seller is selling you a house at an inflated price because they know you can't get an appraisal through a traditional lender.

### Negotiation Points

If the deal passes the red flag test, here are specific terms to negotiate:

**Interest rate:** Start by offering 2 percentage points below what the seller asks. If they're at 10%, counter at 8%. The seller's cost of funds is zero (they own the property outright) or their mortgage rate (typically 3-5%). Anything above 8% is generous to the seller.

**Term length:** Push for the longest term possible — 7-10 years instead of 3-5. More time means more time to rebuild credit and refinance. If the seller insists on 5 years, negotiate a clause that allows a one-time extension of 2-3 years if you're current on payments.

**Down payment:** Lower is better for you, but a reasonable down payment (5-10%) gives the seller security and may get you a better interest rate. Don't put more than 10% down unless you're getting exceptionally good terms — you're putting equity into a property you don't own.

**Cure period:** Push for 60 days. The seller will counter with 30. Either is acceptable. Zero is a dealbreaker.

**Prepayment:** No penalty, no restrictions. You should be able to pay $500 extra per month toward principal if you want to. This accelerates equity building and reduces your balloon.

## The Bottom Line

A land contract is not homeownership. It's a path toward homeownership that costs more, provides fewer protections, and carries significant risk of losing everything you've invested. For some people in specific situations, it's a reasonable bridge. For many people, it's a trap that looks like a door.

The difference between the two comes down to three things:

**1. Your exit plan.** If you have a specific, realistic plan to refinance into a traditional mortgage within the contract term — with the credit score trajectory, income stability, and savings to make it happen — a land contract can work. If your plan is "I hope things get better," it won't.

**2. Your protections.** If you get every protection on the checklist above — title search, recorded contract, attorney review, inspection, escrow — you've minimized the risks that destroy most land contract buyers. If you skip any of them to save $300, you've just made a $300 bet on a $150,000 outcome.

**3. The seller's terms.** A fair seller offers reasonable interest (under 9%), a meaningful term (7+ years), a warranty deed, a right to cure, and no objection to recording or inspection. An unfair seller offers "easy" terms that are designed to extract maximum payments before default. The fair seller wants you to succeed because they get paid. The unfair seller wants you to almost succeed because they get paid and keep the house.

Know which one you're dealing with. And if you don't know, that's what the checklist is for.

The contract you sign with yourself — the one from [Module 06](/methodology/the-contract/) — says you make decisions based on math, not desperation. A land contract entered with full information, full protection, and a clear exit plan is math. A land contract entered because someone said "don't worry about the details" is desperation wearing a house-shaped costume.

Do the math. Get the protections. Have the plan. Or keep renting until you do.

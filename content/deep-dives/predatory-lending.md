# The Predatory Lending Contract

### The Math They're Counting On You Not Doing

---

## The Industry That Profits From Desperation

There's a $15 billion industry in America built on one business model: lend money to people who are broke, charge fees so high that they can never pay it back, and collect those fees over and over until the borrower has paid far more than they ever borrowed.

It's not a secret. It's not illegal in most states. It's payday lending, title lending, rent-to-own, and buy-here-pay-here car lots. And the people who run these businesses understand one thing perfectly: when you're desperate for $400 to keep the lights on, you don't read the fine print. You sign.

This module is the fine print — translated into math.

---

## Payday Loans: The $15 That Costs $400

### How They Work

You walk into a storefront (or open an app). You need $400. The lender says: "We'll give you $400 today. On your next payday — in two weeks — you pay us back $460. The fee is $15 per $100 borrowed."

$60 fee on a $400 loan. That's 15%. For two weeks. Sounds expensive but manageable — it's $60, not $600.

Here's what they're not explaining:

**That 15% is for two weeks, not a year.** Annualized, a $15-per-$100 fee on a 14-day loan is an APR of **391%**. Not a typo. Three hundred ninety-one percent.

For comparison:
- Credit card: 18-25% APR
- Personal loan from a credit union: 8-12% APR
- Mortgage: 6-7% APR
- Payday loan: 300-500% APR

But APR is an abstraction. Let's talk about what actually happens.

### The Rollover Trap

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau found that **80% of payday loans are rolled over or followed by another loan within 14 days**. Eighty percent. The industry's business model doesn't work if you pay the loan off on time. The industry's business model depends on you not being able to.

Here's why: if you couldn't cover a $400 expense two weeks ago, what's changed about your financial situation in those two weeks? Nothing. You still earn the same amount. You still have the same bills. But now you also owe $460.

So you roll it over. You pay the $60 fee — and the loan resets for another two weeks. Now you owe $460 again in two more weeks. Same problem. Same outcome.

**A $400 payday loan rolled over 4 times:**

```
THE $400 PAYDAY LOAN — ROLLED OVER 4 TIMES
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
         FEE PAID     STILL OWED     TOTAL SPENT
Initial   —            $400           $0
Week 2    $60          $400           $60
Week 4    $60          $400           $120
Week 6    $60          $400           $180
Week 8    $60          $400           $240
Payoff    $400         $0             $640
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL FEES PAID:       $240
TOTAL REPAID:          $640
COST OF BORROWING $400: $240 (60% of the loan)
TIME IN DEBT:          56 days
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

You paid $640 to borrow $400. The lender earned $240 — sixty percent of your original loan — in less than two months.

Run your own numbers with the [Predatory Loan Cost Calculator](/tools/predatory-loan-cost/).

### What That Same $400 Would Cost Elsewhere

```
$400 BORROWED FOR 56 DAYS — BY SOURCE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
                     INTEREST/FEES    TOTAL REPAID
Payday loan (4x):   $240.00          $640.00
Credit card (25%):  $15.34           $415.34
Personal loan (12%):$7.37            $407.37
Credit union (8%):  $4.91            $404.91
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The payday loan costs **49 times** what a credit card costs and **49 times** what a personal loan costs for the same money over the same period. The interest on a credit card for 56 days is $15. The payday lender charges $240.

"But I can't get a credit card," you say. Maybe. But have you tried? Have you applied to a credit union? Have you asked about a secured credit card with a $300 limit? Have you looked into employer paycheck advances? Have you called 211 for emergency assistance? The payday loan industry exists because people don't exhaust their alternatives first. We'll get to every alternative below.

### The Average Payday Borrower

The typical payday borrower:
- Takes out 8 loans per year
- Pays $520 in fees per year on an average loan of $375
- Spends 5 months of the year in debt to payday lenders
- Has an annual household income of $30,000-$40,000

That $520 in annual fees on a $30,000 income is 1.7% of gross income — spent on nothing but the privilege of borrowing your own future paycheck. Over 5 years, that's $2,600. Invested at 7%, it would be $3,075. That's a starter emergency fund, created from money that instead went to a storefront with neon signs.

## Title Loans: Bet Your Car

### How They Work

A title loan is a payday loan with collateral — your car. You hand over your car title, the lender gives you a loan (typically 25-50% of your car's value), and you make payments. If you can't pay, they take your car.

**Typical terms:**
- Loan amount: 25-50% of vehicle value
- Fee: $25 per $100 (25% per month)
- Term: 30 days
- APR: 300%
- Collateral: your car title

If your car is worth $8,000, you might get a $3,000 loan. The monthly fee is $750. After 30 days, you owe $3,750.

### The Math

```
$3,000 TITLE LOAN — $25 PER $100, 30-DAY TERM
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
                FEE PAID    BALANCE     TOTAL COST
Month 1         $750        $3,000      $750
Month 2         $750        $3,000      $1,500
Month 3         $750        $3,000      $2,250
Month 4         $750        $3,000      $3,000
Payoff          $3,000      $0          $6,000
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL REPAID: $6,000 (200% of loan amount)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

After 4 months of payments, you've paid $3,000 in fees — the full amount of the original loan — and you still owe $3,000. To fully repay, you spend $6,000 on a $3,000 loan.

### What You Actually Lose

The CFPB found that **1 in 5 title loan borrowers have their car repossessed**. One in five.

Think about what losing your car means:
- You can't get to work → you lose your job
- You can't get groceries → you pay more for delivery or nearby convenience stores
- You can't get to medical appointments → health problems compound
- You can't get your kids to school → cascading family disruption

You borrowed $3,000 to solve a short-term problem. Now you've paid $3,000-$6,000 in fees, lost your $8,000 car, lost your job, and the short-term problem is still there. The title lender resells your car and makes the loan to someone else.

The title lending industry generates roughly $3 billion in fees per year. The cars they repossess are resold or re-lent on. It's a recycling machine, and the raw material is your desperation.

## Buy-Here-Pay-Here Car Lots: The Markup Machine

### How They Work

BHPH dealers target buyers with bad credit — people who can't get approved at a traditional dealership. The dealer is both the seller and the lender. They don't run your credit through a bank. They finance the car themselves.

This sounds like a solution. Here's what it actually is:

**Step 1: The car is overpriced.** A car with a fair market value of $8,000 is listed at $12,000-$15,000. You don't know this because you're comparing it to the sticker prices at the BHPH lot, not to market value.

**Step 2: The interest rate is extreme.** BHPH interest rates typically run 18-30%. Some states have no cap.

**Step 3: The terms are structured for repossession.** Short payment cycles (weekly or biweekly), GPS tracking on the vehicle, and immediate repossession for missed payments. Many BHPH dealers install GPS kill switches — miss a payment, and the car won't start.

**Step 4: When you default, they take the car back and sell it again.** The average BHPH car is sold 3-4 times. Each time, the dealer collects a down payment and weeks or months of payments before repossessing and reselling.

### The Math

```
BUY-HERE-PAY-HERE vs. FAIR MARKET PURCHASE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                          BHPH LOT        FAIR MARKET
Car fair market value:    $8,000          $8,000
Sale price:               $14,000         $8,000
Down payment:             $2,000          $2,000
Amount financed:          $12,000         $6,000
Interest rate:            24%             8%
Term:                     48 months       48 months
Monthly payment:          $394            $147
Total payments:           $18,912         $7,056
Total cost (w/ down):     $20,912         $9,056
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EXTRA COST OF BHPH:       $11,856
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The BHPH car costs **$11,856 more** — 132% extra — for the same vehicle. And if you default at month 18 after paying $2,000 down plus $7,092 in payments ($9,092 total), you lose the car and every dollar. The dealer puts the car on the lot again.

### The GPS Kill Switch

Many BHPH dealers install GPS tracking and remote-disable devices in their vehicles. Miss a payment — even by a day — and the car won't start. You're stranded wherever you are: at work, at the grocery store, picking up your kids.

Some states have started regulating these devices. Most haven't. The dealer's argument is that it's their collateral and they have a right to protect it. The reality is that it's a tool of coercion. You make every payment on time because the alternative is being stranded.

## Rent-to-Own: Paying Triple for a Couch

### How It Works

Rent-to-own stores (Aaron's, Rent-A-Center, and local equivalents) let you take home furniture, appliances, and electronics today with no credit check. You make weekly or monthly payments. When you've made all the payments, you own the item.

What they don't make obvious: the total payment amount is 2-3 times the retail price.

### The Math

```
RENT-TO-OWN vs. RETAIL PURCHASE
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ITEM                RTO TOTAL     RETAIL      MARKUP
Washer/dryer set    $2,340        $900        160%
Laptop              $1,560        $500        212%
Living room set     $3,900        $1,400      179%
55" TV              $1,820        $550        231%
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
RTO terms: typically $25-$40/week for 12-18 months
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

That $550 TV at $30/week for 60 weeks costs $1,800. You paid $1,250 in extra fees for the privilege of not having $550 right now.

And if you miss a payment, the store takes the item back. You've paid $900 toward a $550 TV, and you have nothing.

### The Alternative You Already Have

A used washer/dryer on Facebook Marketplace or Craigslist: $150-$300. A used laptop at a refurbisher: $150-$250. A used couch: $50-$200.

"But I don't have $150." If you can afford $30/week in rent-to-own payments, you have $150 in five weeks. Buy a used item after five weeks of saving and you keep the other $1,650 the rent-to-own store would have extracted.

Or: many communities have free furniture banks, Salvation Army stores, Habitat for Humanity ReStores, and Buy Nothing groups. The item isn't new. It works. And it doesn't cost you $1,800.

## Why This Industry Exists (And Why It Works)

Predatory lending isn't a market failure. The market is working exactly as designed. Here's why it persists:

### 1. Banking Deserts

Approximately 5.9 million US households are "unbanked" — they have no checking or savings account. Another 18.7 million are "underbanked" — they have an account but still use alternative financial services. When there's no bank branch within 10 miles and no credit union that will serve you, the payday lender on the corner is the only option you see.

### 2. Credit Invisibility

About 26 million Americans are "credit invisible" — they have no credit file at all. Another 19 million have files too thin to generate a score. Without a credit score, you can't get a credit card, a personal loan, or a mortgage. Payday and title lenders don't check credit. That's the selling point. It's also the trap.

### 3. Paycheck Timing

Most Americans are paid biweekly or semi-monthly. Bills don't follow that schedule. When rent is due on the 1st and payday is the 7th, you need $1,200 for six days. A payday loan covers those six days for "only" $180. The structural mismatch between pay cycles and bill due dates creates constant demand for short-term credit.

### 4. Emergency Costs

The Federal Reserve found that 37% of Americans couldn't cover a $400 unexpected expense with cash or savings. When the car breaks down, when the kid gets sick, when the furnace dies in January — there's no buffer. The payday lender offers immediate cash. The consequences are deferred. In a crisis, deferred consequences feel like no consequences.

### 5. The Shame Cycle

People who use predatory loans often feel ashamed about it. That shame prevents them from talking about it — with family, friends, or financial counselors who might offer alternatives. The isolation keeps them in the system. The industry counts on your silence.

## Every Alternative to Predatory Lending

Before you walk into a payday lender, a title loan shop, a rent-to-own store, or a buy-here-pay-here lot, exhaust this list. Every single option on it is cheaper. Most of them are free.

### Immediate Cash Needs ($100-$500)

**1. Employer paycheck advance.** Many employers offer early access to earned wages at no cost. Apps like Earnin, DailyPay, and PayActiv partner with employers. Ask HR. The fee is $0-$5 instead of $60-$100.

**2. Credit union payday alternative loans (PALs).** Federal credit unions offer PALs: $200-$1,000 loans with a maximum 28% APR and a maximum $20 application fee. A $400 PAL costs approximately $9 in interest over 30 days. A payday loan costs $60. Joining a credit union usually requires a $5-$25 minimum deposit.

**3. Payment plan with the creditor.** Call whoever you owe. Utilities, medical offices, landlords — most will work out a payment plan before sending you to collections. The fee for asking is $0.

**4. 211 / Community assistance.** Dial 211 or visit 211.org. This connects you to local emergency assistance programs: utility assistance (LIHEAP), rent assistance, food assistance, and emergency cash grants from nonprofits. These are free. They exist for exactly this situation. Most people don't know about them.

**5. Negotiate the bill.** Medical bills are negotiable. Always. Ask for an itemized bill (errors are common), then ask for the "self-pay" or "uninsured" discount (typically 20-50% off). Many hospitals have financial assistance programs that reduce or eliminate bills entirely.

**6. Family or friends.** Uncomfortable? Yes. Cheaper than a payday loan? By $240 on a $400 loan. Write up a simple agreement, set a repayment date, and pay them back. The social cost of asking is real but finite. The financial cost of a payday loan is ongoing.

### Short-Term Credit ($500-$5,000)

**7. Secured credit card.** Put down $200-$500 as a deposit, get a credit card with that limit. Use it for emergencies, pay the balance each month, and you're building credit simultaneously. APR is 20-25% — not great, but 15-20x cheaper than payday lending.

**8. Credit-builder loan.** Credit unions and online lenders (Self, MoneyLion) offer small loans designed to build credit. You make payments into a savings account; when the loan term is up, you get the money. You've built credit and savings simultaneously.

**9. Personal loan from a credit union.** Even with bad credit, many credit unions will make small personal loans at 12-18% APR. That's a fraction of payday loan costs.

**10. Local nonprofit emergency loan programs.** Many communities have CDFIs (Community Development Financial Institutions) that make small emergency loans at fair rates. Google "[your city] CDFI" or "[your city] emergency loan nonprofit."

### Structural Solutions (Preventing the Next Crisis)

**11. Build a $500 buffer.** That's it. Not $10,000. Not six months of expenses. $500. The Federal Reserve data shows that having $500 in accessible savings reduces the likelihood of using predatory lending by over 50%. Save $20/week for 25 weeks. Put it in a separate account. Don't touch it unless something breaks.

**12. Shift your bill due dates.** Most creditors will move your due date to align with your paycheck. Call each one and ask. If you get paid on the 1st and 15th, cluster bills on the 5th and 20th. The paycheck timing problem disappears.

**13. Use SNAP and food benefits to free up cash.** If your household income qualifies, SNAP benefits cover groceries — freeing up $200-$500/month in cash for bills. Apply at your state's benefits portal or in person at your local DHS office. There's no shame in using a program funded by your taxes.

**14. Free tax preparation (VITA).** The IRS Volunteer Income Tax Assistance program does your taxes for free if your household income is under $67,000. Paid tax preparers charge $200-$400 and sometimes push refund advance loans (another predatory product). VITA is free and often gets you a larger refund because they know the credits you qualify for. Visit irs.gov/vita to find a location.

For building credit from zero or rebuilding after damage, see [The Credit Score Contract](/deep-dives/credit-scores/).

## How to Get Out If You're Already In

If you're currently in a payday loan cycle, a title loan, or underwater on a BHPH car, here's the extraction plan.

### Payday Loan Exit Strategy

**Step 1: Stop the rollover.** This is the hardest step. The next time the loan comes due, let it default rather than rolling it over. Yes, you'll get collection calls. But every rollover costs another $60-$100 in fees. Stopping the bleed is step one.

**Step 2: Set up a payment plan.** Many states require payday lenders to offer Extended Payment Plans (EPPs) — usually 4 installments at no additional fee. Ask for the EPP. If the lender refuses, file a complaint with your state attorney general's office and the CFPB.

**Step 3: Revoke ACH authorization.** If the lender has automatic withdrawal access to your bank account, revoke it. Call your bank and request a stop payment on the lender's withdrawals. Put the revocation in writing. The lender cannot debit your account after you've revoked authorization — it's a federal violation.

**Step 4: Know your state's protections.** Some states cap rollovers. Some require cooling-off periods between loans. Some ban payday lending entirely. Check your state attorney general's website.

**Step 5: Don't take out a new loan to pay the old one.** This is the trap. Getting a second payday loan to cover the first doubles the fees and doubles the hole. Find any alternative from the list above instead.

### Title Loan Exit Strategy

**Step 1: Refinance with a credit union.** Some credit unions will refinance title loans into personal loans at dramatically lower rates. Call every credit union in your area and ask.

**Step 2: Negotiate with the lender.** Title lenders would rather keep collecting payments than repossess your car (repossession costs them money). Ask for a reduced payoff amount or a lower interest rate. They may say no. Ask anyway.

**Step 3: If repossession is imminent, know your rights.** In most states, the lender must give you written notice before repossessing. They cannot breach the peace (break into your garage, threaten you). If they repossess and sell the car for more than you owe, they must return the surplus. Some states provide a right-to-cure period.

**Step 4: Surplus rights.** If the car is repossessed and sold at auction for more than your remaining loan balance, you're entitled to the difference. Many title lenders don't tell you this. Ask in writing for an accounting of the sale proceeds.

### BHPH Car Exit Strategy

**Step 1: Check the value of the car.** Look up the fair market value on Kelley Blue Book or NADA Guides. If you owe $10,000 on a car worth $4,000, you're deeply underwater.

**Step 2: Consider voluntary surrender.** If the car is worth far less than you owe and the payments are crushing you, voluntarily surrendering the car stops the bleeding. You'll still owe the deficiency (the difference between what the car sells for and what you owe), but the weekly payments stop and you can negotiate the deficiency.

**Step 3: Buy a cheap car with cash.** A $2,000-$3,000 car that runs is better than a $14,000 BHPH car that's draining your income. Yes, it's not pretty. It moves. That's the requirement.

### Rent-to-Own Exit

**Step 1: Return the item.** In most states, you can return a rent-to-own item at any time with no further obligation. You lose everything you've paid, but you stop the bleeding.

**Step 2: Replace it cheaper.** Used appliances, furniture, and electronics are available at a fraction of rent-to-own cost. Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, Habitat ReStore, Salvation Army, Buy Nothing groups. See the list above.

## State-by-State: Where You Have Protection and Where You Don't

Payday lending is banned or severely restricted in these states:
- **Banned entirely:** Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado (reformed with rate caps), Connecticut, Georgia, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, North Carolina, Pennsylvania, South Dakota, Vermont, West Virginia, District of Columbia
- **Rate caps that effectively ban payday lending:** Some states cap APR at 36%, which makes the payday model unprofitable and eliminates storefronts

In states where payday lending is legal, protections vary from significant to almost none. In Texas, Ohio, and several other states, lenders have found loopholes around even modest regulations by structuring loans as "credit access businesses" or "credit service organizations."

**Check your state:** Visit your state attorney general's website or the CFPB's state-by-state guide. Know the rules before you need them.

The contract doesn't tell you what to do. The contract tells you to do the math. And the math on predatory lending is the simplest math in this entire methodology: it costs too much, there are cheaper alternatives, and the industry's profit depends on you not knowing that.

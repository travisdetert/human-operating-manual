# The Inflation Contract

### Because Your Dollar Lost 20% of Its Value Since 2020 and Nobody Sent You a Bill

---

## The Invisible Tax

There is a tax you pay every single day that never shows up on your pay stub, never appears on a receipt, and never gets debated at the dinner table. It's called inflation, and it is the single most effective wealth-destruction mechanism in modern economics -- not because it's dramatic, but because it's invisible.

Here's the simplest way to understand it: **$100 in the year 2000 buys you about $57 worth of stuff today.** That's it. Same bill. Same number printed on it. But it buys 43% less. Nobody stole the other $43. It just evaporated through the slow, silent erosion of purchasing power.

**The historical average:** From 1926 to 2023, inflation in the United States has averaged about **3.2% per year**. That sounds small. It is not small. At 3.2% annual inflation, prices double roughly every 22 years. Everything your parents bought for $1 costs you $2. Everything you buy for $1 will cost your kids $2.

**The recent reality:** In June 2022, the Consumer Price Index hit **9.1% year-over-year** -- the highest inflation rate in 40 years. Groceries were up 12%. Energy was up 41%. Used cars were up 7.1% on top of the 37% they'd already jumped the year before. In the span of about three years, the average American household saw their cost of living increase by 20-25%. Nobody got a 20-25% raise to match.

Now let's talk about your savings account.

If you're in a **high-yield savings account paying 4.5%**, you're roughly keeping pace with long-term average inflation. Congratulations -- you're running on a treadmill. Your money isn't growing; it's treading water. And if inflation ticks above 4.5%, even your high-yield account is underwater.

If you're in a **standard savings account paying 0.5%** -- which is where most Americans keep their money -- you are losing approximately **2.5% or more** of your purchasing power every single year. Not hypothetically. Actually.

Here's what that looks like with real numbers:

```

(Standard savings at 0.5%, inflation at 3%)

              Nominal Balance    Real Purchasing Power    Lost
              (what the bank     (what it actually
               statement says)    buys)

Year 0:       $50,000            $50,000                  $0
Year 5:       $51,264            $43,130                  $6,870
Year 10:      $52,557            $37,205                  $12,795
Year 15:      $53,881            $32,107                  $17,893

```

Read that table again. In 30 years, your $50,000 can buy what $20,645 buys today. You lost almost 60% of your money's value while it sat "safely" in the bank. The bank statement never showed a withdrawal. No one took a dollar from your account. But the dollars themselves shrank.

**"Saving money" in a 0.5% account is mathematically identical to losing 2.5% per year.** You are paying a 2.5% annual fee for the privilege of watching your wealth disappear in slow motion. The bank loves this arrangement. You should not.

## Real Returns vs. Nominal Returns

This is the single most important concept in personal finance that almost nobody understands, and it changes every financial decision you make.

**Nominal return** is the number you see. It's the percentage your investment went up. It's the interest rate on your savings account. It's the number on the screen. It feels real. It is not the full picture.

**Real return** is the nominal return minus inflation. It's what your money actually earned in terms of purchasing power. It's the only number that matters.

When someone tells you the stock market returned 10% last year, they're giving you the nominal number. If inflation was 3%, your real return was 7%. You didn't earn 10%. You earned 7% in actual, spend-it-on-things purchasing power. **Inflation ate 30% of your gains.**

Let's compare asset classes honestly:

**S&P 500 (stocks):**
- Nominal return: ~10% per year (historical average)
- Real return: ~7% per year
- Verdict: **Builds wealth.** The only broadly accessible asset class that consistently outpaces inflation by a wide margin over long periods.

**High-yield savings account:**
- Nominal return: 4.5% (current rates, which will change)
- Real return: ~1.5% (assuming 3% inflation)
- Verdict: **Barely positive.** Better than losing money. Not building wealth. Appropriate for emergency funds and short-term savings, not long-term wealth building.

**Standard savings/checking account:**
- Nominal return: 0-0.5%
- Real return: **-2.5% to -3%**
- Verdict: **You are losing money every day.** This is not a safe place for your money. It feels safe because the number doesn't go down. But the number is lying to you.

**Bonds (Treasury, investment-grade):**
- Nominal return: 4-5% (current)
- Real return: 1-2%
- Verdict: **Preservation, not growth.** Useful for stability and near-term goals. Will not make you wealthy.

**Cash under your mattress:**
- Nominal return: 0%
- Real return: **-3%**
- Verdict: **The worst possible choice.** You are guaranteed to lose money at the full rate of inflation.

Now here's why this matters over a lifetime:

```
$10,000 INVESTED AT AGE 25, LEFT FOR 30 YEARS
(All returns nominal, then adjusted for 3% inflation)

Asset Class          Nominal     Real         Real Value
                     Return      Return       at Age 55

Stocks (S&P 500)     10%         7%           $76,123
High-Yield Savings    4.5%       1.5%         $15,631
Bonds                 4%         1%           $13,478
Standard Savings      0.5%      -2.5%          $4,689
Checking/Cash         0%        -3%            $4,120

Stocks turn $10,000 into $76,123 of real purchasing power.
Cash turns $10,000 into $4,120 of real purchasing power.
The difference: $71,000+ on a single $10,000 investment.
Multiply that by every dollar you have.
```

**"Keeping cash safe" is mathematically a decision to lose purchasing power.** The stock market is volatile. Cash is not. But over 30 years, cash is guaranteed to lose real value, while stocks are overwhelmingly likely to gain it. The thing that feels safe is the thing that's actually dangerous. The thing that feels dangerous is the thing that's actually safe -- over time.

This does not mean you should invest your emergency fund in stocks. It means you need to understand what "safe" actually means. Safe from volatility? That's cash. Safe from purchasing-power erosion? That's investments. You need both kinds of safety. Most people only think about the first kind.

## Lifestyle Inflation: The Other Inflation Nobody Talks About

The government measures consumer price inflation. Nobody measures the other inflation -- the one that's arguably more destructive to your financial life. **Lifestyle inflation** is when your spending rises to match (or exceed) your income every time your income goes up.

And almost everybody does it.

You get a $10,000 raise. What happens? You upgrade the apartment. You eat out more. You buy the nicer car. You start shopping at the places you used to just browse. Within 6 months, you've absorbed the entire raise into your lifestyle. Your bank balance looks exactly the same as it did before. You make more money and have nothing to show for it.

**The math of lifestyle inflation:**

You earn $50,000. You spend $45,000. You save $5,000/year.

You get promoted. You now earn $75,000. If you kept spending $45,000, you'd save $30,000/year -- a 6x increase in savings on a 50% raise. That's transformational.

But you don't. You move to a nicer apartment (+$400/month). You lease a new car (+$250/month). You eat out twice a week instead of twice a month (+$300/month). You upgrade your wardrobe, your gym, your subscriptions, your vacations. Your spending inflates to $70,000.

**New savings: $5,000/year.** Exactly where you started.

A 50% raise. Zero financial progress. This is the hedonic treadmill applied to money, and it catches almost everyone.

**Why it happens:** Your brain adjusts to your new income within about 3 months. Behavioral economists call this **hedonic adaptation**. The new salary feels normal. The new apartment feels like baseline. The nicer car feels like what you deserve. And "deserve" is the most expensive word in personal finance.

**The raise allocation rule:**

Here's the countermeasure. It's simple, it's mechanical, and it works: **Decide what percentage of every raise goes to savings before you receive the raise.** Not after. Not once you "see how things go." Before. While the number is still abstract.

The rule: **Save at least 50% of every raise.** You can spend the other 50%. Your lifestyle still improves. You still feel the bump. But half of every raise goes straight to your future self, automatically, before your brain adjusts to the new normal.

**The compounding effect of saving half your raises:**

```
SAVING 50% OF RAISES, INVESTED AT 7% REAL RETURN

Starting salary: $50K, saving $5K/year
Raises: $5K every 2 years (typical trajectory)
50% of each raise → additional annual savings

Year 0:  Save $5,000/yr  |  Portfolio: $0
Year 2:  Save $7,500/yr  |  Portfolio: $10,700
Year 4:  Save $10,000/yr |  Portfolio: $28,100
Year 6:  Save $12,500/yr |  Portfolio: $54,200
Year 8:  Save $15,000/yr |  Portfolio: $91,500
Year 10: Save $17,500/yr |  Portfolio: $143,200
Year 15: Save $22,500/yr |  Portfolio: $340,800
Year 20: Save $27,500/yr |  Portfolio: $651,400

By Year 20: $651K+ invested. Your salary rose from $50K to $100K.
Your lifestyle spending went from $45K to $72.5K (still a 61% increase).
You live MUCH better than you did at 25. And you have $651K.

Without the rule: you saved $5K/year for 20 years.
Portfolio at 7%: ~$219K. You spent the rest.
Difference: $432,000.
```

That's $432,000 -- the cost of lifestyle inflation over 20 years. Not in some theoretical model. In your actual life. Every dollar your lifestyle absorbs from a raise is a dollar that never compounds. And the compounding is where the life-changing money comes from.

## What Inflation Means for Every Financial Plan

Inflation isn't an abstract economic concept. It's a concrete force that changes the math on every single financial plan you have. If you're not adjusting for it, your plans are wrong.

**Retirement:**

You want to retire in 30 years with "a million dollars." Sounds great. But $1,000,000 in 30 years, at 3% average inflation, has the purchasing power of about **$412,000 in today's dollars**. That's not wealthy. That's modest. That might be tight.

If you need $50,000 per year to live on in today's dollars, you need to plan for **$121,363 per year** in 30 years (at 3% inflation). Using the 4% withdrawal rule, that requires a portfolio of roughly **$3,034,000** -- not $1,250,000, which is what you'd calculate if you ignored inflation.

Inflation more than doubles your retirement number. Most retirement calculators don't show you this clearly. The contract demands you do the math in real terms.

**Debt:**

Here's the one area where inflation is your friend. If you have a **fixed-rate mortgage at 3.5%** and inflation averages 3%, your real interest rate is only **0.5%**. Your monthly payment stays the same in nominal terms, but it gets cheaper in real terms every year because your income (hopefully) grows while your payment doesn't.

A $300,000 mortgage with a $1,500/month payment feels heavy at a $60,000 salary (30% of gross income). In 15 years, if your salary has grown to $90,000, that same $1,500 payment is now 20% of gross income. Inflation effectively shrank your debt. This is why financial advisors say "don't rush to pay off a low-rate mortgage." The math favors investing the difference.

**Savings Goals:**

You're saving for a $20,000 car you plan to buy in 10 years. But at 3% inflation, that car costs **$26,878** in 10 years. Your target isn't $20,000. It's $27,000. If you saved exactly $20,000, you're $7,000 short and wondering what happened.

The same logic applies to everything: college savings, a house down payment, a wedding, a sabbatical fund. Every goal more than 5 years out needs an inflation adjustment, or you'll undershoot.

**Your Salary:**

This is where inflation gets personal. If your annual raise is **2%** and inflation is **4%**, you didn't get a raise. You got a **2% pay cut**. Your number went up. Your purchasing power went down. You can buy less with your new salary than you could with your old one.

```
DID YOU ACTUALLY GET A RAISE?

Last year's salary:     $_______
This year's salary:     $_______

Nominal raise:          $_______ (new - old)
Nominal raise %:        _______% (raise / old salary x 100)

Current inflation rate:  _______% (check BLS.gov for latest CPI)

Real raise %:           _______% (nominal raise % - inflation %)

If the number above is NEGATIVE, you got a pay cut.
If the number above is ZERO, you broke even.
If the number above is POSITIVE, you actually got a raise.

Example:
Salary went from $65,000 to $67,600 (4% nominal raise)
Inflation: 3.2%
Real raise: 0.8%

Your $2,600 raise is really worth $520 in purchasing power.
The other $2,080 just kept you in the same place.
```

Every year you don't get a raise that matches inflation, you are functionally making less money. Over a career, this compounds. If you go 10 years getting 2% raises in a 3.5% inflation environment, you've taken a cumulative **14% real pay cut**. You make more on paper and can afford less. This is how the middle class slowly slides backward without anyone noticing.

**Negotiate your salary with inflation in mind.** A "cost of living adjustment" isn't a raise. It's just not a pay cut. An actual raise starts above the inflation rate.

## Assets That Beat Inflation vs. Assets That Don't

Not all money is parked in the same place, and where you park it determines whether inflation helps you, hurts you, or slowly destroys you.

**Assets that beat inflation (historically):**

**Stocks (broad index funds):** 7% real return over long periods. The single most reliable long-term inflation hedge available to regular people. An S&P 500 index fund has beaten inflation in every rolling 20-year period in history. Not most of them. All of them. Short-term? Volatile. Scary. Gut-wrenching. Long-term? Undefeated.

**Real estate:** 2-4% real appreciation historically, plus rental income. Housing tends to rise with inflation because replacement costs (labor, materials) rise with inflation. If you own a home with a fixed-rate mortgage, you benefit twice: your asset appreciates with inflation while your debt shrinks in real terms.

**TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities):** These are government bonds designed specifically to keep up with inflation. The principal adjusts based on CPI. If inflation is 3%, your principal grows 3%. You get a small real yield on top. Not exciting, but guaranteed to match inflation. Useful for the conservative portion of a portfolio.

**I-Bonds:** The unsung hero of inflation protection. These are savings bonds issued by the US Treasury with an interest rate that adjusts every 6 months based on the CPI. The current composite rate has ranged from 5-7% during high-inflation periods. The catch: **$10,000 annual purchase limit per person** ($20K per couple), and you must hold them for at least one year (with a small penalty if redeemed before 5 years).

I-Bonds are essentially **risk-free, inflation-adjusted savings**. For the portion of your emergency fund you won't need in the next 12 months, I-Bonds are one of the smartest places to park money. You'll never lose to inflation. You'll never lose principal. The government guarantees both.

**Assets that lose to inflation:**

**Cash.** Loses 3%+ per year, every year, forever. The most popular place to store money is the worst possible place to store money long-term.

**Checking accounts.** Paying 0-0.01%. Guaranteed real loss.

**Standard savings accounts.** Paying 0.3-0.5%. Guaranteed real loss, just slightly less guaranteed.

**CDs below the inflation rate.** If your 2-year CD pays 3% and inflation is 3.5%, you locked up your money to lose purchasing power. And you can't even access it.

**Most bonds in high-inflation environments.** When inflation spikes, existing bonds with fixed rates lose real value. A bond paying 3% during 6% inflation is losing 3% per year in real terms.

**The simple, practical strategy:**

1. **Keep 3-6 months of expenses in a high-yield savings account.** This is your emergency fund. Accept that it roughly treads water with inflation. That's fine. Liquidity is the priority here, not growth.
2. **Consider I-Bonds for a portion of your emergency fund or short-term savings.** Up to $10K per year, inflation-adjusted, government-backed.
3. **Invest everything else.** Broad index funds (total stock market, S&P 500) for money you won't need for 5+ years. This is how you beat inflation. This is how you build wealth. Everything sitting in cash beyond your emergency fund is losing value every single day.

The goal is simple: **no idle money.** Every dollar should have a job. Emergency dollars sit in high-yield savings. Near-term dollars (1-5 years) go into I-Bonds or short-term bond funds. Long-term dollars go into equities. Nothing sits in a checking account gathering dust and losing purchasing power.

## The Wage-Price Spiral and Why Your Grocery Bill Doubled

Between 2020 and 2023, something happened that most Americans hadn't experienced in their adult lives: real, visible, painful inflation. Not the quiet 2-3% annual erosion you don't notice. The kind where you look at a receipt and say, "How is this possible?"

**Why it happened:**

A perfect storm of forces, and no single explanation is complete without all of them:

**Supply chain disruption.** COVID shut down factories, ports, and logistics networks worldwide. When demand came back, supply couldn't keep up. Basic economics: same demand plus less supply equals higher prices. This was the initial spark.

**Monetary policy.** The Federal Reserve pumped roughly $4.8 trillion into the economy between 2020 and 2022 through quantitative easing and near-zero interest rates. The government sent trillions more in stimulus checks, PPP loans, and expanded unemployment. This was arguably necessary to prevent economic collapse, but the side effect was a massive increase in the money supply chasing the same amount of goods.

**Corporate pricing power.** Here's the part that gets less attention: many companies raised prices because they could. When consumers expect inflation, companies can raise prices above their actual cost increases and blame "the economy." Corporate profit margins hit record highs during the inflation surge. Not all price increases were driven by costs. Some were driven by opportunity.

**The wage-price feedback loop.** Workers demanded higher wages to keep up with prices. Companies raised prices to cover higher wages. Workers demanded higher wages to keep up with prices. This cycle, once started, is difficult to stop.

**What it looked like in your life:**

- **Eggs:** $1.47/dozen in January 2020 to $4.82/dozen at peak (January 2023). A 228% increase.
- **Housing (median rent):** $1,100/month in 2020 to $1,370/month by 2023. Up 25%.
- **Used cars (average price):** $22,000 in 2020 to $31,000 at peak. Up 41%.
- **Ground beef:** $3.85/lb to $5.20/lb. Up 35%.
- **Healthcare premiums:** Up 7% in 2023 alone, with out-of-pocket maximums rising even faster.
- **Childcare:** Up 32% since 2019 in many metro areas.

And then there's **shrinkflation** -- the inflation you're not supposed to notice. Same package, same price, less product inside. The family-size chip bag went from 16 oz to 13 oz. The ice cream "half gallon" became 1.5 quarts (it already had -- they did it again). The paper towels have fewer sheets. The candy bar is smaller. The laundry detergent does "40 loads" instead of "50 loads" at the same price.

This is real inflation disguised as nothing. The price per unit goes up, but the sticker price stays the same, so your brain files it as "normal." It is not normal. It is a price increase wearing a costume.

**The hard truth: prices almost never go back down.**

If you're waiting for eggs to go back to $1.47 or rent to go back to $1,100, you will wait forever. That's not how inflation works. Inflation isn't a wave that rises and falls. It's a ratchet. It goes up. It stays up. Sometimes it goes up slower. Sometimes it goes up faster. But it doesn't come back down. Deflation -- sustained, broad-based price decreases -- is extraordinarily rare and usually only happens during severe economic depressions. You do not want deflation. It's worse than inflation. But you also shouldn't plan your financial life around prices returning to 2019 levels. They won't.

**The only real hedge against rising prices is increasing your earning power faster than prices increase.** Negotiate your salary. Build skills that are in demand. Create additional income streams. You cannot control the price of eggs. You can control how much value you create and capture in the labor market. The contract is about what you can control. This is what you can control.

## Scenario: Ben and Sarah

**The Setup:**

Ben (34) and Sarah (32) are married, living in a mid-sized Midwestern city. Combined household income: **$95,000** (Ben earns $55K as a warehouse operations manager, Sarah earns $40K as an office administrator). No kids yet, but they're planning.

**Their financial snapshot:**

- **Checking account:** $3,200 (where paychecks land)
- **Savings account (standard, 0.5% APY):** $40,000
- **Investments:** $0
- **Retirement accounts:** Ben has $8,000 in an old 401(k) he hasn't contributed to in 3 years. Sarah has nothing.
- **Debt:** $6,500 remaining on Ben's car loan at 4.9%. No credit card debt. No student loans (both worked through community college).
- **Monthly expenses:** ~$5,800/month (rent, car, insurance, food, utilities, subscriptions, misc)
- **Monthly take-home (combined):** ~$6,400
- **Monthly surplus:** ~$600, which mostly accumulates in checking or gets spent on irregular expenses

They feel responsible. They have $40,000 in savings. No credit card debt. They're doing "fine." They came across the contract framework because Sarah read an article about retirement planning and panicked when she ran the numbers.

**The inflation audit:**

Let's look at what their money is actually doing.

That $40,000 in a 0.5% savings account earns **$200/year** in interest. Inflation at 3% costs them **$1,200/year** in purchasing power. **Net loss: $1,000/year.** Every year they leave that money where it is, they lose the equivalent of $1,000 in real value.

In 10 years at this rate, their $40,000 will have the purchasing power of roughly **$30,000 in today's dollars.** They will have "saved" $40,000 and lost $10,000 of it to inflation without a single withdrawal.

Ben's old 401(k) with $8,000 is sitting in a target-date fund that's earning roughly 7-8% per year. That's actually growing. But he stopped contributing 3 years ago, and $8,000 alone isn't going to fund retirement.

Their $600/month surplus is sitting in checking, earning literally nothing, and getting nibbled away by random spending because there's no plan for it.

**The diagnosis:** Ben and Sarah aren't broke. They're bleeding slowly. They have the income and discipline to build real wealth, but their money is parked in the wrong places, and time is burning.

**The plan:**

**Step 1: Restructure their savings.**
- Move $15,000 from standard savings to a **high-yield savings account** (4.5% APY). This is their liquid emergency fund -- roughly 2.5 months of expenses.
- Purchase **$10,000 in I-Bonds** (each of them buys $5K, or $10K total for the household). This becomes the second tier of their emergency fund -- accessible after 12 months, inflation-adjusted.
- Move the remaining **$15,000** into a **brokerage account** invested in a total stock market index fund. This money they won't need for 5+ years.

**Step 2: Automate investing.**
- Set up automatic monthly investment of **$500/month** into the brokerage account (total stock market index fund). This comes from their $600/month surplus.
- Remaining $100/month stays as buffer in checking.

**Step 3: Restart retirement contributions.**
- Ben re-enrolls in his company's 401(k) at **6% of salary** ($275/month). His employer matches 3% -- that's $137.50/month in free money he's been leaving on the table for 3 years.
- Sarah opens a **Roth IRA** and contributes **$200/month** to start.

**Step 4: Address the car loan.**
- $6,500 at 4.9% isn't an emergency, but it's not great. They pay the normal payment and put any windfalls (tax refund, bonuses) toward it. Should be paid off within a year.

**The trajectory:**

```
BEN AND SARAH: CURRENT PATH VS. INFLATION-AWARE PATH
(20-year projection, 3% inflation, 7% real stock returns)

=== CURRENT PATH (do nothing) ===

Savings account (0.5%):
  Year 0:  $40,000 real value
  Year 10: $30,200 real value
  Year 20: $22,800 real value

Ben's old 401(k) (no new contributions, 7% real):
  Year 0:  $8,000
  Year 10: $15,700
  Year 20: $30,900

Total real wealth at Year 20: ~$53,700
They lost purchasing power on their savings.
They barely built retirement.

=== INFLATION-AWARE PATH ===

High-yield savings (emergency fund, ~1.5% real):
  Maintained at $15,000 real value (replenished as needed)

I-Bonds ($10,000, keeps pace with inflation):
  Maintained at $10,000 real value

Brokerage ($15K initial + $500/month, 7% real):
  Year 5:   $57,100
  Year 10:  $117,800
  Year 20:  $310,200

Ben's 401(k) ($8K + $275/mo + $137.50 match, 7% real):
  Year 5:   $38,600
  Year 10:  $84,700
  Year 20:  $234,100

Sarah's Roth IRA ($200/month, 7% real):
  Year 5:   $14,600
  Year 10:  $35,400
  Year 20:  $106,500

Total real wealth at Year 20: ~$675,800

DIFFERENCE: $622,100 in real purchasing power.
Same income. Same discipline. Different allocation.
```

Let that sink in. **$622,100 in additional real wealth.** Not from earning more. Not from cutting lattes. From understanding what inflation does and putting their existing money in the right places.

**Ben and Sarah's contract terms:**

1. **No more than $15,000 in standard savings.** Everything above that gets invested or placed in inflation-protected assets. Non-negotiable.
2. **Automatic investing of $500/month.** Set it and forget it. The money moves before they can spend it.
3. **Both contribute to retirement accounts every paycheck.** Ben at 6% to 401(k), Sarah at $200/month to Roth IRA. They increase these by 1% of salary every year.
4. **Annual inflation audit.** Every January, they review: Are our savings losing real value? Did our raises beat inflation? Are we on track for our real (inflation-adjusted) retirement number?
5. **50% raise rule.** When either of them gets a raise, 50% goes to increased savings/investing before lifestyle adjusts.

**Sprint 1 focus:** Open the high-yield savings account, purchase I-Bonds, open the brokerage account. Three tasks in 30 days. Everything else can wait until Sprint 2.

## Your Inflation Contract

Inflation is not something that happens to other people. It is not an abstract macroeconomic force. It is happening to you, right now, today, to every dollar you have. The question isn't whether inflation will erode your wealth. It will. The question is whether you'll build a system that outruns it.

The contract terms are straightforward:

1. **Know the number.** Check the current inflation rate at least once per quarter. Know what it's doing to your money. Ignorance is not a hedge.

2. **No idle cash beyond your emergency fund.** Every dollar above 3-6 months of expenses should be in an asset that has a real chance of beating inflation. If it's sitting in a 0.5% savings account, it's dying.

3. **Think in real terms.** When you evaluate a savings rate, an investment return, a raise, or a retirement projection, subtract inflation. The nominal number is a feel-good fiction. The real number is the truth. Train yourself to ask: "What is this after inflation?"

4. **Protect against lifestyle inflation.** Use the 50% raise rule. Automate your savings increases. Make the decision before the money hits your account, not after your brain adjusts to the bigger number.

5. **Conduct an annual inflation audit.** Every year, review your cash positions, your investment allocations, your salary trajectory, and your retirement projections -- all in real, inflation-adjusted terms. Adjust the plan. This is the ongoing maintenance the contract requires.

6. **Build earning power, not just savings.** You cannot control the price of groceries. You can control the value you bring to the labor market. Skills that are in demand give you negotiating leverage. Negotiating leverage gives you raises that beat inflation. This is the long game.

This connects directly to [Module 09: The Math of Your Life](/methodology/09/). The numbers in that module need to be run in real terms. Your monthly audit needs to account for the slow bleed. If your financial plan doesn't have an inflation adjustment, it's not a plan -- it's a wish.

Inflation is the wolf you can't hear. It doesn't growl. It doesn't bare its teeth. It sits outside your door, year after year, quietly eating everything you leave unprotected. The contract is the lock on the door. But locks only work if you use them.

Do the audit. Move the money. Build the system. Your future purchasing power depends on what you do this month -- not someday, not when you get around to it. **This month.**

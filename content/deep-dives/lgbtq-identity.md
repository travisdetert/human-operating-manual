# The LGBTQ+ Contract

### Writing Your Own Terms When the Default Contract Was Never Written for You

---

## The Starting Point

Every system in this methodology -- the honest audit, the financial architecture, the physical infrastructure, the relationships module -- assumes a baseline. It assumes you can walk into a doctor's office and be treated with basic competence. It assumes your family is at least potentially a support structure. It assumes the law protects you in your workplace. It assumes your identity isn't, by itself, a financial liability.

If you're LGBTQ+, some or all of those assumptions are wrong. Not theoretically wrong. Wrong in ways that cost money, safety, health, and years of your life.

This is a module about building a functional self-management system when the default infrastructure either ignores you, wasn't built for you, or is actively hostile. The contract methodology works for LGBTQ+ people -- but like any system running on non-standard infrastructure, it needs calibration. Your identity is not the problem. The systems are the problem. Your contract accounts for both.

## The Authenticity Audit

The standard honest audit asks you to assess your finances, your health, your relationships. The LGBTQ+ authenticity audit adds a layer: where are you performing, and what is that performance costing you?

**The core question:** Where is the gap between who you are and who you present to the world?

That gap has a price. Every context where you're hiding, code-switching, or performing a version of yourself that isn't real -- that costs energy you could be spending on building the life your contract describes.

The data is stark. 40% of LGBTQ+ youth have seriously considered suicide in the past year. LGBTQ+ adults are more than twice as likely to experience poverty compared to cisgender, heterosexual adults. These aren't numbers about personal failure. They're numbers about what happens when systems don't account for you.

This audit isn't pressure to come out everywhere. Coming out is not an obligation -- it's a calculation. In some contexts, the cost of visibility is lower than the cost of hiding. In others, it's the opposite. Your contract respects both. But you cannot manage what you haven't measured.

## Family as Broken Infrastructure

The contract methodology treats family as infrastructure -- emotional stability, financial backup, safety net during crisis. For many LGBTQ+ people, that infrastructure is damaged, absent, or actively dangerous.

The numbers: 40% of homeless youth identify as LGBTQ+, despite making up roughly 7-10% of the youth population. That ratio tells you everything about how family infrastructure fails.

Family rejection exists on a spectrum:

**Full rejection.** You're cut off. No financial support, no holiday invitations, no emergency contact. This is devastating, but it's at least clear. You know what you're working with, and you can plan accordingly.

**Conditional acceptance.** "We love you, but don't bring your partner to Thanksgiving." "We accept you, but don't tell your grandmother." This is harder to navigate because it looks like support while functioning as control. You're included, but only the version of you they've approved.

**The grief nobody names.** Even when you build a stronger life, there is grief in losing unconditional family support. Other people's parents help with down payments, show up at weddings with joy instead of tension. Grieving that loss is not weakness. It's an honest audit of what you're working without.

### Chosen Family as Rebuilt Infrastructure

When biological family fails, chosen family replaces it. This isn't a consolation prize -- for many LGBTQ+ people, chosen family functions better than biological family ever did because it's built on acceptance rather than obligation.

But chosen family requires investment. Your contract should include relationship terms that build this infrastructure:

- Who are the 2-3 people you can call at 2 AM?
- Who would you list as emergency contact if not a family member?
- What community are you investing time in?
- When biological family is the threat and not the safety net, who is your safety net?

This is infrastructure work. Treat it that way in your contract.

## Financial Disparities

LGBTQ+ identity carries direct financial costs that straight, cisgender people don't face. Your financial architecture needs to account for them.

**The wage gap is real.** Gay and bisexual men earn 10-32% less than heterosexual peers. For trans individuals, earnings drop by nearly one-third after transition for trans women, and many trans people report job loss post-transition.

**Healthcare costs are higher.** HRT: $50-200/month, often out of pocket. Gender-affirming surgery: $7,000-$100,000+, inconsistent coverage. Affirming therapy: $100-250/session. PrEP: up to $2,000/month without insurance. Many LGBTQ+-specific needs are excluded, capped, or require extensive appeals.

**Discrimination persists.** Bostock v. Clayton County (2020) provides federal protection, but enforcement varies dramatically. Being fired or denied housing for being LGBTQ+ still happens under pretextual reasons.

**The emergency fund imperative.** The standard "3-6 months" may not be enough. You might need to relocate for safety, lose a job without recourse, or face uncovered medical expenses. Target 6-9 months.

## The Safety Architecture

Safety is not a given. For many LGBTQ+ people, it's a calculation running in the background every day. Can I hold my partner's hand here? Will this bathroom be safe? Does this doctor's intake form even have an option for me?

The privilege of being fully, visibly out without calculation is exactly that: a privilege. If you have it, use it well. If you don't, your contract needs a safety architecture.

### Geographic Safety Assessment

Where you live affects every other part of your contract. This isn't about blue states versus red states in some abstract political sense. It's about whether you can access healthcare, keep your job, rent an apartment, and walk down the street. Those are contract fundamentals.

**Assess your current location:**
- Legal protections for employment, housing, public accommodation
- Availability of affirming healthcare providers
- Active LGBTQ+ community and organizations
- General climate of safety for visible LGBTQ+ people
- Proximity to supportive people in your life

If your assessment is poor, relocation is a legitimate contract term. It's expensive and disruptive. It's also sometimes the single highest-leverage change you can make.

### Workplace Climate

"Technically fine" is not the same as safe. Many workplaces have non-discrimination policies on paper but cultures that punish difference in practice. Your contract should name the reality, not the policy. What is your actual, daily experience? And what would need to change -- in your approach, your boundaries, or your employment -- to make work sustainable?

### Building Safety Plans

A safety plan is not paranoia. It's infrastructure:

- **If I need to leave my housing quickly:** Where do I go? Who do I call?
- **If I face workplace discrimination:** What is documented? Who is my legal resource?
- **If I face a medical emergency and my partner isn't recognized:** Are legal documents (power of attorney, healthcare proxy) in place?
- **If I'm traveling to a less safe area:** Who knows my itinerary?

## Healthcare Navigation

Finding competent, affirming healthcare as an LGBTQ+ person is a project. It shouldn't be, but it is.

### The Trial-and-Error Tax

Every LGBTQ+ person has a version of this story: intake forms that don't have your identity as an option, half your appointment educating your provider about basic medical needs, or worse -- active hostility and refused care.

This trial-and-error costs time, money, and emotional energy. It also causes preventive care avoidance. When interacting with healthcare is consistently unpleasant or unsafe, you stop going. Screenings get skipped. Conditions that are treatable at stage one become emergencies at stage three.

Your contract term should be specific: "I will find and establish care with an affirming primary care provider by [date]." Not "I should find a doctor." A date. A commitment.

**Resources for finding affirming providers:**
- GLMA provider directory (glma.org)
- OutCare Health (outcarehealth.org)
- RAD Remedy (radremedy.org) -- specifically for trans and gender-diverse patients
- Psychology Today's therapist directory (filter by LGBTQ+ specialization)
- Word of mouth through local LGBTQ+ community organizations

### Insurance Barriers

Insurance coverage for transition-related care varies wildly. Some plans cover HRT, therapy, and surgery comprehensively. Others exclude everything with a transgender-related diagnosis code. Many fall somewhere in between -- covering hormones but not surgery, or covering surgery but requiring extensive gatekeeping.

Know your plan. Read the actual policy documents, not the summary. Compare employer plans specifically for LGBTQ+-relevant coverage. If choosing independently, this is a primary selection criterion.

### Mental Health Support

Finding a therapist who doesn't need you to educate them is worth the search. You should not be paying $150/hour to explain what non-binary means or to gently correct assumptions. An affirming therapist treats your identity as a baseline fact and works on the things you actually came to address.

If therapy is not affordable:
- Sliding-scale providers at LGBTQ+ community centers
- Open Path Collective (openpathcollective.org) -- sessions for $30-$80
- Crisis Text Line: text START to 741741
- The Trevor Project (for youth under 25): 1-866-488-7386 or text START to 678-678
- Trans Lifeline: 877-565-8860

## Scenario: Alex

Alex is a 28-year-old trans man, two years out. Graphic designer earning $48K in a mid-sized Southeast city. He left Alabama -- and his evangelical family -- eighteen months ago. His parents told him he was "choosing sin" and stopped returning calls. His younger sister texts occasionally, in secret.

Supportive partner, Marcus, together for a year. Workplace has a non-discrimination policy, but his manager consistently deadnames him in group settings -- "an honest mistake" every time. HR has been "looking into it" for four months. Monthly costs: $150 for HRT (insurance covers part), $200 for therapy (out of pocket -- in-network providers have no trans experience), saving for top surgery estimated at $8,000.

### Alex's Authenticity Audit

Alex is fully out in most contexts. The gap isn't about hiding -- it's about the cost of being visible in hostile systems. His workplace is "technically fine" but functionally hostile in small, deniable ways. Family scores a 1 on safety. Healthcare scores a 3 -- affirming therapist but mediocre PCP, and he's been avoiding his annual physical.

Biggest energy drain: the workplace. Every deadname is a paper cut. The accumulation is affecting his output, which affects his income, which affects his surgery timeline.

### Alex's Financial Architecture

```
Alex's Financial Snapshot:

Income:             $48,000/year ($4,000/month gross, ~$3,200 net)

Fixed costs:                          Variable costs:
  Rent (split w/ Marcus): $600          Groceries:        $350
  Utilities/internet:     $85           Gas:              $120
  Car payment:            $280          Everything else:  $400
  Car insurance:          $95         Total variable:     $870
  Phone:                  $65
  HRT (after insurance):  $150        Available for savings: $675
  Therapy (out of pocket):$200
  Student loans:          $180        Current savings:       $2,400
Total fixed:              $1,655      Surgery fund:          $1,800
                                      Emergency fund target: $9,900
Surgery timeline at $400/month: ~15 months
Emergency fund gap: $7,500
```

Therapy at $200/month is his single largest discretionary expense. But it's not discretionary -- it's infrastructure. The question isn't whether he can afford it. It's whether he can afford processing a family estrangement, a hostile workplace, and a medical transition without professional support. He can't.

Surgery timeline: 15 months at current savings rate. He can accelerate by finding in-network therapy (saving $200/month), negotiating a raise, or picking up freelance design work.

### Alex's Contract Terms

**Non-negotiable terms:**
1. Continue HRT and therapy without interruption
2. Document every deadnaming incident at work in writing (date, time, witness, exact language) -- this is both self-advocacy and legal preparation
3. Build emergency fund to $5,000 within 6 months

**90-day goals:**
- File a formal HR complaint with documentation (not another "informal chat")
- Find in-network affirming therapist (or appeal insurance for current one)
- Schedule annual physical with a new, affirming PCP
- Research top surgery providers and schedule a consultation

**Safety terms:**
- Healthcare proxy naming Marcus -- signed and filed
- Relocation fund stays above $2,000
- Parents' access to his location and employer information: restricted

**Relationship terms:**
- Weekly check-in with Marcus (not just logistics)
- Monthly trans support group (community is infrastructure, not optional)
- Boundary with parents: "I am available for a relationship that includes my name and my identity. Until that's possible, I am not available."

Alex doesn't need his parents' approval to execute this contract. He doesn't need his manager's cooperation to document the pattern. That's the architecture of self-management: you build it yourself, with the people who show up for you.

## Your Authenticity Contract

Here's what this comes down to.

You exist in a world that was largely designed without you in mind. Your identity is not a deficiency. It's a fact about you, the same way your height or your native language is a fact about you. But facts have consequences in systems. Your contract accounts for those consequences -- not by asking the systems to change first (though they should), but by building your own architecture within, around, and sometimes in spite of those systems.

**Your action items:**

1. **Complete the authenticity audit.** Measure the gap. Know where you're paying the energy tax and where the cost of visibility is too high. Both are real.
2. **Build your safety architecture.** Legal documents, emergency fund, geographic assessment, safety plan. Not pessimism -- infrastructure.
3. **Map your financial reality.** Include every identity-related cost. Your HRT, therapy, and surgery fund are not luxuries. They are infrastructure costs.
4. **Find your people.** Chosen family, community, affirming providers. Invest with the same intentionality you bring to financial terms.
5. **Write your terms.** Dates and numbers, not intentions. "I will find an affirming PCP by March 15" is a contract term. "I should probably find a better doctor" is a wish.
6. **Set boundaries without apology.** You do not owe anyone a performance of an identity that isn't yours. Your contract terms include who gets access to you and on what terms.

Your contract doesn't require anyone else's signature. It doesn't need your family's approval, your employer's endorsement, or society's permission. The whole point of the contract methodology is that you stop waiting for external systems to work and start building your own. For LGBTQ+ people, that principle isn't abstract. It's survival. And it's how you move from surviving to building the life you want.

Your terms. Nobody else's.

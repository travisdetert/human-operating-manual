# The Health and Money Contract

### Because Your Body Is the Asset That Funds Every Other Asset

## Medical Debt: America's Hidden Financial Crisis

Here is a number that should make you stop scrolling: **100 million Americans** carry medical debt. That's roughly one in three adults walking around with a bill from a hospital, a clinic, or a lab that they haven't been able to pay off. This is not a niche problem. This is the default condition of American healthcare finance.

And it gets worse when you look at the downstream effects.

**Medical debt is the #1 cause of personal bankruptcy in the United States.** Not credit cards. Not student loans. Not mortgages. Medical bills are cited in **66% of all personal bankruptcies**. Two-thirds. These are not reckless spenders. These are people who got sick, or whose kid got sick, or who slipped on ice and rode an ambulance they didn't call. The system is designed so that a single health event can vaporize a decade of financial discipline.

**What things actually cost:**
- Average emergency room visit: **$2,200**
- Average 3-day hospital stay: **$30,000+**
- Appendectomy: **$33,000**
- ACL surgery: **$20,000-$50,000**
- Childbirth (vaginal, no complications): **$13,000**
- Childbirth (C-section): **$22,000**
- Heart attack treatment: **$100,000+**

"But I have insurance," you say. Good. Let's talk about what insurance actually covers.

**Even with insurance, your out-of-pocket maximum exposure is significant.** In 2024, the average out-of-pocket maximum for an individual plan is **$8,700**. For a family plan: **$17,400**. That means even in the best-case scenario -- you have insurance, you stay in-network, nothing gets denied -- a serious health event can still cost you nearly $9,000 out of pocket. For a family, it's over $17,000. That's a financial emergency for most households. According to the Federal Reserve, 37% of Americans can't cover a $400 emergency expense without borrowing. We're talking about costs 20-40x that number.

**The uninsured penalty is even more brutal.** If you don't have insurance, hospitals charge you **2-4x** what they charge insurance companies for the same procedure. This is not an exaggeration. Insurance companies negotiate rates. You, as an individual, have no negotiating power. A procedure that Blue Cross pays $5,000 for gets billed to you at $15,000. The system punishes the people who can least afford it.

**Medical debt on your credit report:** Recent reforms have improved things slightly. As of 2023, paid medical debts are removed from credit reports, and unpaid debts under $500 are no longer reported. But unpaid debts above $500 still land on your credit report after 12 months, and they stay there for seven years. A single hospitalization can tank your credit score for the better part of a decade, raising your interest rates on everything else -- car loans, mortgages, credit cards. Medical debt doesn't just cost you the bill. It costs you on every other financial product you touch.

## The Disability Risk Nobody Plans For

If medical debt is the crisis nobody sees coming, disability is the crisis nobody believes will happen to them. You're wrong to think you're exempt. The math says so.

**One in four workers will experience a disability before age 67.** That's Social Security Administration data, not a scare tactic. A quarter of the working population. You probably know someone it's already happened to -- a back injury, a car accident, cancer, a mental health crisis severe enough to prevent working. And you probably thought, "That won't happen to me." Everyone thinks that. One in four of them are wrong.

**The average disability lasts 2.5 years.** Not a few weeks. Not a couple of months. Two and a half years of not being able to work, or not being able to work at your previous capacity.

Let's do the math on what that actually costs:

**Lost income at $60K/year for 2.5 years: $150,000.** That's not a medical bill. That's lost earning power. Money you would have earned, saved, invested, and compounded. Gone. And that's at a modest salary. At $85K, it's $212,500. At $100K, it's $250,000.

**Now here's what the safety net actually provides:**

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) -- which you've been paying into your entire working life -- pays an average of **$1,537 per month**. That's **$18,444 per year**. If you were earning $60,000, SSDI replaces 31% of your income. That's a **69% pay cut.** Try paying your mortgage, your car payment, your groceries, and your now-increased medical expenses on 31% of what you used to make.

And that's if you qualify. SSDI denies approximately 65% of initial applications. The average approval process takes 3-6 months for initial decisions and over a year if you need to appeal. You could be disabled, broke, AND waiting months to find out if the government will send you a check that covers a third of your old salary.

**Private long-term disability insurance** replaces approximately **60% of your income** and typically costs **1-3% of your annual salary.** At $60K, that's $600-$1,800 per year -- $50-$150/month. For $100/month, you're protecting $150,000 in potential lost income. That's a 1,250x return on your premium if you ever need it.

**Employer-provided disability insurance** is usually short-term only. It covers 3-6 months, typically at 60% of salary. After that? You're on your own. Most people don't realize their employer plan has a hard stop until they hit it.

**This is the most underinsured risk in America.** Only about 35% of private-sector workers have access to employer-provided long-term disability insurance. And among those who do have access, many don't enroll because they don't think they'll need it. One in four.

## The HSA as a Stealth Retirement Account

Most people think of a Health Savings Account as a place to stash money for copays. That is like using a Ferrari to drive to the mailbox. The HSA is the single most tax-advantaged account in the entire US tax code, and almost nobody uses it correctly.

**The basics:** To open an HSA, you need a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). In 2024, the minimum deductible for an HDHP is **$1,600 for an individual** and **$3,200 for a family.** If your employer offers an HDHP option, you likely have access to an HSA. If you're self-employed or buying on the marketplace, you can choose an HDHP and open your own HSA.

**2024 HSA contribution limits:**
- Individual: **$4,150**
- Family: **$8,300**
- Catch-up (55+): additional **$1,000**

Now here's why the HSA is the best account in the tax code. It has a **triple tax advantage** that no other account matches:

1. **Tax-deductible contributions.** Every dollar you put in reduces your taxable income. At the 22% tax bracket, a $4,150 contribution saves you $913 in federal taxes immediately.

2. **Tax-free growth.** Your money grows tax-free inside the HSA. No capital gains tax. No tax on dividends. Nothing. It compounds untouched.

3. **Tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses.** When you pull money out for medical costs, you pay zero tax. Zero.

No other account does all three. A 401(k) gives you tax-deductible contributions and tax-free growth, but you pay income tax on withdrawals. A Roth IRA gives you tax-free growth and tax-free withdrawals, but contributions aren't deductible. The HSA does everything. It is the only triple-tax-free account available to most Americans.

**And it gets better after age 65.** Once you turn 65, you can withdraw HSA funds for **any purpose** -- not just medical expenses. Non-medical withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income, just like a traditional IRA. So at worst, your HSA becomes a traditional IRA. At best, it's a completely tax-free medical fund.

**The strategy that most people miss:**

Pay your current medical expenses **out of pocket.** Don't touch your HSA for today's copays, prescriptions, or dental bills. Instead, max out your contributions, invest the money in index funds, and let it compound for decades.

Keep every medical receipt. Every single one. Because here's the rule most people don't know: **you can reimburse yourself for old medical expenses at any time.** There is no time limit. You can pay a $500 medical bill out of pocket today, keep the receipt, and reimburse yourself from your HSA twenty years from now -- after that $500 has been invested and grown tax-free for two decades.

This is not a loophole. This is how the account is designed. The IRS explicitly allows reimbursement for any qualified medical expense incurred after the HSA was established, regardless of when the reimbursement occurs.

The default HSA -- sitting in cash earning 0.01% -- is a waste of the most powerful account you have access to. Move it into a low-cost index fund and let compound interest do what it does.

The HSA is literally designed to solve the retirement healthcare funding problem. Use it.

## The Compound Cost of Skipping Preventive Care

You know that thing you've been putting off? The dentist appointment. The annual physical. The therapy session. The eye exam. The skin check. You know you should go. You tell yourself you'll get to it. You don't.

This is not just a health decision. It is a financial decision. And the math is brutal.

**The dental cascade:**

You skip your cleaning. It costs $200 and takes 45 minutes. No big deal, you'll go next time. You don't go next time either. A year passes. The plaque builds. A cavity forms. You don't feel it yet.

Another year. The cavity deepens. Now it hurts. You go in. You need a **root canal: $2,000.** But the tooth is compromised. You need a **crown: $1,500-$2,000** on top of the root canal. Total: **$3,500-$4,000.** Or the tooth is too far gone. You need an **extraction and implant: $4,000-$5,000.**

You saved $200 by skipping the cleaning. It cost you $4,000. That is a 2,000% markup for procrastination.

**The blood pressure cascade:**

You skip your annual physical. It's free under the ACA -- literally $0 copay for preventive visits. But you don't go because you're busy, or because you don't have a primary care doctor, or because you hate sitting in waiting rooms.

High blood pressure develops. You don't know because nobody checked. It runs undetected for three years. Now you need medication: **$1,200/year** for blood pressure drugs. That's $6,000 over five years. But untreated hypertension has already started damaging your arteries. Your risk of heart attack or stroke has doubled. If a cardiac event happens, treatment costs **$100,000+**, and the lost productivity, rehabilitation, and lifestyle changes that follow are incalculable.

A $0 annual physical catches the blood pressure early. Lifestyle changes -- diet, exercise, stress management -- might have prevented the medication entirely. The cascade starts with skipping something free.

**The mental health tax:**

Untreated depression costs employers an estimated **$3,400 per year per employee** in lost productivity, absenteeism, and presenteeism (showing up but not functioning). But that's the employer's number. Your number is worse.

Untreated depression costs **you** in missed promotions. In stalled career trajectories. In relationships that deteriorate because you can't show up for the people you love. In years -- actual years of your one life -- spent surviving instead of building. The financial cost of untreated depression isn't just the therapy you didn't pay for. It's the raise you didn't get, the business you didn't start, the investment you didn't make because you couldn't muster the energy to open a brokerage account.

Therapy costs $100-$250 per session. Many insurance plans cover it with a $20-$40 copay. The ROI on treating depression isn't just medical. It's existential.

**The exercise dividend:**

A gym membership costs approximately **$50/month -- $600/year.** The average annual additional healthcare cost attributable to obesity is **$1,861 per person.** That's not a one-time bill. That's every year. Over a decade, the difference between maintaining a healthy weight and not is **$18,610 in additional medical costs** alone. That doesn't include the higher life insurance premiums, the lost productivity, the reduced earning potential, or the shortened lifespan.

Regular exercise reduces your risk of heart disease by 35%, type 2 diabetes by 40%, colon cancer by 30%, and depression by 30%. No pharmaceutical product on earth has that efficacy profile. And it costs $50/month.

**Preventive care is the highest-ROI investment you can make.** Higher than the stock market. Higher than real estate. Because it protects the asset that generates all your other returns: you.

## How to Negotiate Medical Bills (The Playbook)

If you already have medical debt, or if a big bill just landed in your mailbox, this section is your playbook. Medical bills are not fixed prices. They are opening offers. And you can negotiate them.

**Step 1: Always request an itemized bill.**

The first bill you receive is usually a summary. It says something like "Hospital Services: $14,000." That tells you nothing. Call the billing department and request a **fully itemized bill** that shows every individual charge -- every medication, every supply, every hour of nursing care, every test.

Why? Because **billing errors are found 30-80% of the time** when patients request itemized bills. Duplicate charges. Charges for services that weren't provided. Charges for medications that were prescribed but not administered. A $14,000 bill might have $2,000-$5,000 in errors. You won't know unless you look.

**Step 2: Ask for the cash pay or self-pay rate.**

Hospitals have different rate structures for different payers. The rate they charge insurance companies is called the "negotiated rate." The rate they bill uninsured patients is the "chargemaster rate" -- and it's almost always the highest rate on the books.

But hospitals also have a **cash pay** or **self-pay discount** for patients who pay directly. This rate is typically **40-60% less** than the chargemaster price. You have to ask for it. They will not volunteer it. Call the billing department and say: "I'd like to know the self-pay rate for these services." Watch the number drop.

**Step 3: Ask about financial assistance programs.**

Most hospitals -- especially non-profit hospitals, which make up about 60% of US hospitals -- have **charity care** and **financial assistance programs.** If your income is below a certain threshold (often 200-400% of the federal poverty level), you may qualify for a reduction or full write-off of your bill.

Non-profit hospitals are legally required to have financial assistance policies. Ask for the application. Fill it out. You might get 50-100% of your bill eliminated.

**Step 4: Negotiate a payment plan.**

Most healthcare providers offer **0% interest payment plans.** This is standard. They would rather get paid slowly than send you to collections. Call billing, explain that you can't pay the full amount at once, and ask for a monthly payment plan. Most will set you up with payments of $50-$200/month with zero interest.

**Step 5: Never put medical debt on a credit card.**

This is critical. A hospital payment plan is typically 0% interest. A credit card is 20-28% interest. The moment you transfer medical debt to a credit card, you convert 0% debt into high-interest consumer debt. You also lose your ability to negotiate, because the hospital has been paid -- now you owe the credit card company, and they don't negotiate.

**Step 6: Know your rights under the No Surprises Act.**

As of January 2022, the **No Surprises Act** protects you from surprise out-of-network bills for emergency care, air ambulances, and certain non-emergency services at in-network facilities. If you receive emergency care at an out-of-network hospital, you can only be charged your in-network cost-sharing rate. The provider and your insurance company have to sort out the rest between themselves.

This doesn't cover everything. Elective out-of-network care is still on you. But for emergencies -- which is when you have the least ability to choose your provider -- you're protected.

**Step 7: Understand the new credit reporting rules.**

As of 2023, paid medical debts are **removed from credit reports entirely.** Unpaid medical debts under $500 are also removed. Unpaid medical debts over $500 don't appear on your credit report until **12 months** after being sent to collections, giving you a full year to negotiate, pay, or set up a payment plan before your credit takes a hit.

These changes are significant. If you have old paid medical debt on your credit report, check it -- it should be gone. If you have a bill headed to collections, you have 12 months to work it out before it affects your credit score.

**The playbook in sequence:**
1. Get the itemized bill
2. Check for errors
3. Ask for the self-pay rate
4. Apply for financial assistance
5. Negotiate the remaining balance
6. Set up a 0% payment plan
7. Never touch your credit card

Most people pay the first number they see. Don't be most people.

## Scenario: Demetrius

Demetrius is 38 years old. He earns **$72,000/year** as a logistics coordinator. His employer offers a High Deductible Health Plan, which he enrolled in because the premiums were lower. He's generally healthy -- or at least he thinks he is.

Here's what Demetrius isn't doing:

- **He's not contributing to his HSA.** His employer opened one for him when he enrolled in the HDHP. There's $0 in it. He didn't realize it was an investment account. He thought it was like a flexible spending account that would expire.

- **He hasn't been to the dentist in 3 years.** Nothing hurts, so he figures he's fine. He's paying $0 for dental care right now.

- **He hasn't had a physical in 3 years.** He's 38 and hasn't had his blood pressure, cholesterol, or blood sugar checked since he was 35. His father had a heart attack at 52.

- **He has no disability insurance beyond his employer's short-term plan.** His employer covers 60% of salary for up to 90 days. After that, nothing.

- **He carries no medical debt -- yet.** His credit score is 740. He has $4,200 in emergency savings.

Demetrius thinks he's in decent shape. Let's look at his actual risk exposure.

**Week 1: Start HSA contributions.**
Demetrius sets his HSA contribution to $346/month ($4,150/year, the individual maximum). After tax savings at the 22% bracket, the net cost to his paycheck is about $270/month. He selects a low-cost total market index fund for his HSA investments.

He books a dental cleaning ($200) and an annual physical ($0 -- covered as preventive under ACA). He calls his dentist and gets on the books for next week.

The dentist finds two cavities -- early stage. Fillings cost $350 total. If he'd waited another year, at least one would have needed a root canal ($2,000). The physical reveals his blood pressure is 138/88 -- stage 1 hypertension. His doctor recommends dietary changes, exercise, and a follow-up in three months. No medication yet. If this had gone undetected for five more years, he'd likely be on blood pressure meds for life.

Demetrius selects a policy: $120/month, 60% income replacement, 90-day elimination period, benefits until age 65.

## Your Health and Money Contract

Your body is the engine that earns everything else. Every dollar in your bank account, every investment in your portfolio, every payment on your mortgage -- all of it flows from your ability to show up, function, and produce. When that engine breaks down, the entire financial system it supports breaks down with it.

This is not a nice-to-have. This is the foundation.

Go back to **Module 03: Physical Infrastructure.** Your physical health is not separate from your financial plan. It IS your financial plan. The body that shows up to work, the brain that makes decisions, the energy that drives execution -- these are not soft benefits. They are hard assets with quantifiable value.

Go back to **Module 02: Financial Architecture.** Build your HSA into the architecture. Build disability insurance into the architecture. Build preventive care costs into your monthly baseline. These are not optional line items to be cut when the budget gets tight. They are structural supports. Remove them and the building eventually falls.

Here are the terms:

1. **Know your numbers.** Complete the health-cost audit above. Know your out-of-pocket maximum, your disability gap, your HSA balance, and your preventive care status. You cannot manage what you do not measure.

2. **Max your HSA.** If you have access to an HDHP, open and fund your HSA to the annual maximum. Invest it. Don't touch it. Let it compound for decades. This single account can solve your retirement healthcare problem entirely.

3. **Insure your income.** Get long-term disability coverage. If your employer offers it, enroll. If they don't, buy a private policy. The cost is 1-3% of your salary. The protection is 60% of your income for the duration of a disability. This is the most underinsured risk in your financial life.

4. **Schedule preventive care now.** Not next month. Now. The dental cleaning, the physical, the blood work, the screening you've been putting off. The cost of prevention is always less than the cost of treatment. Always.

5. **If you have medical debt, work the playbook.** Itemized bill. Error check. Self-pay rate. Financial assistance application. Payment plan. In that order. Don't accept the first number. Don't put it on a credit card.

6. **Protect the asset.** Move your body. Feed it real food. Sleep. Manage stress. See a therapist if you need one. This is not wellness advice. This is financial advice. A healthy body earns more, costs less, and lasts longer than a neglected one. The math is not close.

Your body is the asset that funds every other asset. Treat it like one. That's the contract.

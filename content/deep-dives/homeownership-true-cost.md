# The Homeownership Contract

### Because the Sticker Price Is the Smallest Number You'll Pay

---

## The Costs They Don't Put on the Listing

"A $350,000 home." Say it out loud. Sounds like one number. One decision. One cost. It's not. It's a cascade of costs that starts the day you make an offer and never stops until you sell -- at which point a whole new cascade begins.

The purchase price is the number on the sign. Everything below is the number on your life.

### Closing Costs: 2-5% of the Purchase Price

Before you get the keys, you write checks to people you've never met for services you didn't know existed.

**On a $350,000 home with 10% down ($315,000 loan):**

```
CLOSING COST BREAKDOWN:
Loan origination fee (1%):           $3,150
Appraisal:                           $500
Home inspection:                     $400
Title search & insurance:            $1,750
Attorney fees:                       $1,200
Escrow fees:                         $800
Recording fees:                      $250
Credit report:                       $50
Prepaid property taxes (3 months):   $963
Prepaid homeowner's insurance:       $1,350
Prepaid interest:                    $700
Survey:                              $400
──────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL CLOSING COSTS:                 $11,513
```

That's $11,513 you pay in addition to your down payment. Before you move a single box. And this is on the low end -- in high-cost markets, closing costs routinely hit $18,000-$25,000.

Your real estate agent will call these "standard." They are standard. They're also $11,513.

### PMI: The Tax on Not Being Rich Enough

Put down less than 20%? You're paying Private Mortgage Insurance. PMI protects the lender -- not you -- in case you default. You pay for their safety net.

**On a $315,000 loan (10% down on $350K):**

- PMI rate: 0.5-1.5% of the loan amount per year
- Monthly PMI at 0.8%: **$210/month**
- Time to reach 80% LTV (assuming 3.5% annual appreciation and standard amortization): roughly **6 years**
- Total PMI paid before it drops off: **$15,120**

That's $15,120 for insurance that protects someone else. And it doesn't drop off automatically -- you have to request cancellation when you hit 78% LTV, and your servicer has every incentive to "forget" to tell you.

### Property Taxes: The Rent You Pay to the Government on the House You "Own"

National average: about 1.1% of assessed value. But averages are misleading.

**Annual property taxes on a $350,000 home by state:**
- Alabama: ~$1,750
- Texas: ~$6,125 (1.75%)
- Illinois: ~$7,700 (2.2%)
- New Jersey: ~$8,225 (2.35%)
- Connecticut: ~$7,350 (2.1%)

Same home value. Property taxes range from $1,750 to $8,225. And these go up. Reassessments happen. Mill rates increase. That $6,125 in Texas becomes $7,000 in five years, then $8,200 in ten.

Nobody mentions this at the open house.

### Homeowner's Insurance: Rising Faster Than Your Income

Average annual premium: **$1,800**. But if you're in Florida, Louisiana, Texas, or California, that number is a fantasy. Homeowners in climate-exposed states are seeing 20-40% annual increases. In Florida, the average premium is now over $4,500/year and climbing. Some areas are becoming functionally uninsurable by private carriers.

And your mortgage company requires it. No insurance, no mortgage.

### HOA Fees: The Bill That Never Stops Growing

Not every home has an HOA. But if yours does:

- Typical range: **$200-$600/month**
- Annual cost: **$2,400-$7,200**
- Plus special assessments (roof replacement on the building, parking garage repair, legal fees): **$2,000-$15,000**, often with 60-90 days' notice

An HOA fee of $400/month is $4,800/year. Over a 30-year mortgage, that's **$144,000** -- assuming it never increases. It will increase. Every year. You have no control over this expense. A board you may or may not have elected decides how much you owe, and you pay it or face liens.

### Maintenance: The Cost That Eats Your Equity

The standard rule: budget **1-2% of your home's value per year** for maintenance and repairs.

On a $350,000 home, that's **$3,500-$7,000 per year** set aside. Not spent -- set aside. Because when the furnace dies in January, you're not calling a landlord. You're calling a contractor with a credit card.

**Real repair costs:**

```
MAJOR REPAIR COST RANGES:
Roof replacement:              $8,000 - $15,000
HVAC replacement:              $5,000 - $10,000
Water heater:                  $1,200 - $3,500
Foundation repair:             $5,000 - $25,000
Sewer line replacement:        $3,000 - $8,000
Electrical panel upgrade:      $1,500 - $4,000
Plumbing (repiping):           $4,000 - $15,000
Exterior paint:                $3,000 - $7,000
Windows (full house):          $8,000 - $20,000
Driveway replacement:          $3,000 - $8,000
```

Your roof lasts 20-25 years. Your HVAC lasts 15-20. Your water heater lasts 8-12. These aren't "if" expenses. They're "when" expenses on a known timeline. And they all arrive at once in the middle of whatever decade you happen to be living there.

### Utilities: The Upgrade Nobody Warned You About

Renters typically share walls, share systems, share infrastructure. Homeowners heat, cool, light, and water an entire structure.

Average utility increase from apartment to house: **20-40% higher**. On a national average of $250/month in a rental, that's an extra $50-$100/month. Modest per month. $600-$1,200 per year. $18,000-$36,000 over 30 years. And that assumes energy costs don't rise. They will.

### The True Monthly Cost

Here's what your $350,000 home actually costs every month:

```
TRUE MONTHLY COST OF A $350K HOME (10% down, 7% rate):
Mortgage (P&I):                  $2,097
Property taxes:                  $321   (national avg)
Homeowner's insurance:           $150
PMI:                             $210   (until 80% LTV)
Maintenance reserve:             $438   (1.5% of value / 12)
Utilities (above rental):       $75    (incremental cost)
──────────────────────────────────────────
MONTHLY COST:                    $3,291

Zillow listed the mortgage at $2,097.
Your actual cost is $3,291.
That's 57% more than the number on the listing.
```

Add an HOA ($400/month) and you're at $3,691. Add the closing costs amortized over the first five years ($192/month) and you're approaching $3,900.

This is the number the listing doesn't show you. This is the number the contract demands you calculate.

## The Transaction Tax of Moving

Buying a home is expensive. Selling a home might be worse.

### Getting In: $10K-$20K Just to Start

On a $350,000 purchase:
- Down payment (10%): $35,000
- Closing costs: $11,513
- Moving costs: $2,000-$5,000
- Immediate repairs and setup: $2,000-$5,000
- **Total cash to get in: $50,513-$56,513**

That's $50K+ in cash that must exist in your bank account before you turn a doorknob. And $13,500-$21,500 of it is gone forever -- it doesn't become equity. It's just the fee for the transaction.

### Getting Out: 8-10% of the Sale Price

Say your home appreciated to $400,000 over five years. You sell.

```
SELLING COST BREAKDOWN ON A $400,000 SALE:
Listing agent commission (2.5-3%):    $10,000 - $12,000
Buyer's agent commission (2.5-3%):    $10,000 - $12,000
Seller's closing costs:               $3,000 - $5,000
Pre-sale repairs/updates:             $3,000 - $8,000
Staging:                              $1,500 - $3,500
Professional photography:             $300 - $500
Transfer taxes (varies by state):     $800 - $4,000
Mortgage payoff fees:                 $200 - $500
Moving costs:                         $2,000 - $5,000
──────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL COST TO SELL:                   $30,800 - $50,500
```

On a $400,000 sale, you're paying **$32,000-$40,000** to leave. That's 8-10% of the sale price, gone before you see a dime of profit.

Your $50,000 in "appreciation" ($350K to $400K)? After selling costs, you might net $10,000-$18,000. On a home you lived in for five years. After paying $197,820 in mortgage payments during those five years.

### The Breakeven Horizon

Here's the math nobody does before they buy:

Between buying costs ($11,500+), selling costs ($32,000+), and the interest-heavy early years of your mortgage (where 75-80% of each payment goes to interest, not principal), **you typically need to own a home for 5-7 years just to break even compared to renting.**

If you move every 3-4 years? You're hemorrhaging wealth through transaction costs. Every move costs you $40,000-$60,000 in combined buying and selling friction. Do that three times in twelve years and you've burned $120,000-$180,000 on transaction costs alone. That's not building wealth. That's lighting it on fire and calling it "the American dream."

## The Opportunity Cost Nobody Calculates

Your house is not an investment. It's a place you live. And the money you put into it doesn't work for you the way money works when you invest it.

### The Down Payment Trap

You put $35,000 down on a $350,000 home. That money now sits in your house. It earns you nothing except the privilege of having a slightly lower mortgage payment and avoiding PMI sooner.

What if you'd invested that $35,000 in a total market index fund instead?

```
$35,000 INVESTED AT 8% AVERAGE ANNUAL RETURN:
After 5 years:   $51,430
After 10 years:  $75,568
After 15 years:  $111,018
After 20 years:  $163,107
After 30 years:  $352,365

$35,000 SITTING IN HOME EQUITY AT 3.5% APPRECIATION:
After 5 years:   $41,554  (house value portion)
After 10 years:  $49,334
After 15 years:  $58,574
After 20 years:  $69,543
After 30 years:  $98,066
```

After 30 years, the invested down payment is worth **$352,365**. The same money in home equity is worth **$98,066** in appreciation -- and you can't access it without selling the house or taking a loan against it.

The difference: **$254,299**. A quarter of a million dollars in opportunity cost.

### Home Appreciation: Barely Beating Inflation

The long-run average for home price appreciation is **3-4% per year** nationally. Inflation averages 2-3%. Your home's real (inflation-adjusted) return is roughly **1% per year**.

The S&P 500's long-run average return is **~10% nominal, ~7% real**. Your home is not competing with the stock market. It's barely competing with a savings account after you factor in all the costs of ownership.

"But my neighborhood went up 15% last year!" Maybe. And some neighborhoods went down 10%. Some went down 40% in 2008 and took 12 years to recover. Individual home prices are volatile, illiquid, and concentrated in a single asset. That's the opposite of every principle of sound investing.

### The Interest Bomb

This is the number that should make you sit down.

**On a $280,000 loan at 7% over 30 years:**

```
TOTAL MORTGAGE COST:
Principal borrowed:          $280,000
Monthly payment (P&I):       $1,863
Total of 360 payments:       $670,680
Total interest paid:         $390,680

You pay $390,680 in interest.
You pay for the house 2.4 times.
```

Read that again. On a $280,000 mortgage, you pay the bank **$390,680** for the privilege of borrowing their money. The total cost of your $350,000 home, after 30 years of payments, is **$705,680** (down payment + total payments). Plus property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and repairs.

At 7%, in the first year of your mortgage, **$19,562 goes to interest and only $2,794 goes to principal**. You pay $22,356 and your equity increases by $2,794. The bank takes the other $19,562. Thank you very much.

### The "Forced Savings" Argument

"But a mortgage is forced savings!" Yes. It is. It's the most expensive savings account in existence. You're paying $390,680 in interest over 30 years to "save" $280,000 in equity. A regular brokerage account charges you $0 in interest to save money. An IRA gives you tax advantages to save money. A mortgage charges you 140% of the balance in interest for the privilege.

Forced savings has value for people who truly cannot save any other way. But if you have the discipline to invest the difference between renting and owning, the math favors renting in many markets.

### The Rent vs. Buy Comparison

```
10-YEAR COMPARISON: RENTING + INVESTING vs. BUYING
(Assumptions: $350K home, 10% down, 7% rate, 3.5% appreciation,
$1,800/mo rent, 3% annual rent increases, 8% investment return)

BUYING:
Down payment + closing:              $46,513
Monthly housing cost (avg):          $3,291
Total housing cost (10 years):       $441,433
Home value after 10 years:           $493,693
Remaining mortgage balance:          $263,747
Equity:                              $229,946
Net cost of housing:                 $211,487

RENTING + INVESTING:
Monthly rent (avg over 10 years):    $2,120
Total rent paid (10 years):          $254,400
Down payment invested:               $46,513 → $100,410
Monthly savings invested ($1,171/mo avg difference): → $208,367
Total portfolio value:               $308,777
Net cost of housing:                 -$54,377 (you MADE money)
```

The renter who invests the difference has **$308,777 in liquid investments** after 10 years. The homeowner has **$229,946 in illiquid equity** locked in a house they'd need to spend $40,000+ to access by selling. The renter is ahead by $78,831 -- and their money is accessible tomorrow.

The exact numbers depend on your market, your discipline, and your rates. But the principle holds: homeownership is not the automatic wealth builder that the real estate industry tells you it is. Not when you include every cost.

## The Psychological Lock-In

Homeownership doesn't just lock up your money. It locks up your options, your identity, and your ability to make rational decisions.

### Geographic Lock-In

You get offered a job in Denver that pays $25,000 more. You own a home in Charlotte. To take the job, you need to sell the house.

Selling costs: $32,000-$40,000. Moving costs: $5,000-$10,000. Buying again in Denver (a more expensive market): another $50,000+ in cash. Total cost of accepting a $25,000 raise: **$87,000-$100,000**.

It takes 3-4 years of the higher salary just to cover the transaction costs of the move. So you don't move. You stay. You turn down the better opportunity because the house won't let you go. The house you "own" owns you.

Renters? Give notice, pack a truck, accept the job. Total cost: two months of overlapping rent and a U-Haul.

### The Sunk Cost Trap

Your home's value drops. The market shifts. You're "underwater" -- you owe more than the house is worth. Rationally, you should consider walking away, selling at a loss, or renting it out. Instead, you stay. You wait for it to "come back." You pour more money into a depreciating asset because you've already put so much in.

This is the sunk cost fallacy wearing a white picket fence. The money you've already spent is gone regardless of what you do next. But the human brain can't process that when it's your home, your neighborhood, your kitchen that you renovated.

### Lifestyle Inflation in Three Words

"The house needs..."

The house needs new cabinets. The house needs a deck. The house needs landscaping. The house needs smart home upgrades. The house needs a finished basement.

Nobody renovates a rental. But homeowners spend an average of **$12,000-$18,000 per year** on home improvements beyond basic maintenance. Over 10 years, that's $120,000-$180,000 in discretionary spending driven by ownership psychology, not need.

You didn't plan to spend $35,000 on a kitchen remodel. But you "own" the home and the kitchen is "outdated" and you'll "get it back when you sell." (You won't. Kitchen remodels recoup 54-72% of cost at resale.)

### Identity Attachment

"I'm a homeowner" is an identity in American culture. It signals stability, adulthood, financial competence. Selling -- especially at a loss, or to downsize, or to go back to renting -- feels like failure. Even when the math screams that selling is the right move.

This is the most expensive emotion in personal finance. People stay in homes they can't afford, in cities they want to leave, in properties that are draining their wealth, because selling would mean they're "not a homeowner anymore." The identity costs more than the mortgage.

### The Maintenance Stress Tax

There's no line item for the 2 AM panic when the basement floods. No accounting for the weekend spent getting quotes for a new roof. No spreadsheet captures the Sunday dread of wondering what's going to break next.

Renters call the landlord. Homeowners are the landlord. And the landlord works for free.

## When Buying Actually Wins

The contract demands honesty in both directions. Buying is not always worse than renting. Here's when the math tilts toward ownership.

**You're staying 7+ years.** Transaction costs need time to amortize. The longer you stay, the more the fixed costs of buying and selling get spread out, and the more principal you build. At 7+ years in most markets, the math starts to favor buying.

**Interest rates are below 5%.** At 4% on a $280,000 loan, total interest drops to $201,240 (vs. $390,680 at 7%). That's $189,440 less in interest. If you locked in a rate between 2020-2022, you're sitting on a historically anomalous asset: cheap debt. Don't give it up lightly.

**Your local rent-to-price ratio is high.** In markets where renting a comparable home costs more than the true monthly cost of owning, buying wins. Rent-to-price ratios above 0.7% (annual rent / home price) generally favor buying. Below 0.5%, renting is almost always cheaper.

**Tax advantages apply to you.** Mortgage interest is deductible if you itemize -- but the 2017 SALT cap ($10,000) and increased standard deduction ($29,200 for married filing jointly) mean most homeowners no longer benefit. Only about 10% of filers now itemize. If you do, and your mortgage interest plus state taxes exceed the standard deduction, there's real savings. Run the actual numbers with your tax situation.

**Emotional value is real.** Stability. A yard for your kids. A garage for your projects. Painting the walls without asking permission. These have value. They're just not financial value. The contract asks you to account for them honestly -- value them, but don't pretend they're ROI.

## Scenario: Marcus and Dana

Marcus (34) and Dana (31) earn a combined $120,000. He's a project manager ($68,000). She's a physical therapist ($52,000). They're renting a 2-bedroom apartment for $1,650/month in Raleigh, North Carolina. They're looking at a $380,000 home in a suburb with good schools.

**Their current financial picture:**

```
MARCUS AND DANA -- FINANCIAL SNAPSHOT:
Combined gross income:               $120,000/yr ($10,000/mo)
Combined take-home:                  $7,600/mo
Current rent:                        $1,650/mo
Car payments:                        $520/mo (two cars)
Student loans (Dana):                $340/mo ($28,000 remaining)
Savings:                             $42,000 (earmarked for down payment)
Retirement accounts:                 $67,000 combined
Emergency fund:                      $8,500
Credit card debt:                    $0
Monthly surplus after all expenses:  $850
```

They've been told they can "afford" a $380,000 home. The bank pre-approved them for $420,000. Let's see what "afford" actually means.

### The Purchase Cost Analysis

```
PURCHASE: $380,000 HOME
Down payment (10%):                   $38,000
Closing costs (3.3%):                 $12,540
──────────────────────────────────────────
Cash needed to close:                 $50,540

Remaining savings after purchase:     $42,000 - $38,000 = $4,000
(Closing costs from savings + gift)
Emergency fund after purchase:        $8,500
Available cash reserves:              $12,500
```

They drain their savings to $4,000 and still need help with closing costs. Their emergency fund -- $8,500 for a household with two cars, two incomes, and now a house -- covers about one HVAC replacement. Or one month of mortgage if someone loses a job.

### True Monthly Ownership Cost

```
MONTHLY OWNERSHIP COST:
Mortgage P&I ($342K loan, 7%):        $2,276
Property taxes ($3,400/yr):           $283
Homeowner's insurance:                $165
PMI (0.7%):                           $200
Maintenance reserve (1.5%):           $475
HOA:                                  $0
Utilities (est.):                     $280
──────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL MONTHLY HOUSING:                $3,679

Current rent:                         $1,650
Increase:                             $2,029/month
```

Their housing cost more than doubles. Monthly surplus drops from $850 to **negative $1,179**. They cannot afford this house at these terms without cutting $1,179/month from their current spending. That's cutting their entire discretionary budget plus most of their current savings rate.

### 5-Year Scenario: Own vs. Rent and Invest

```
SCENARIO A: BUY THE $380K HOME

Total monthly housing cost:           $3,679
Total housing paid (5 years):         $220,740
Home value after 5yr (3.5% appr.):   $451,261
Mortgage balance after 5 years:       $323,115
Equity:                               $128,146
Minus selling costs (9%):             -$40,614
Net equity after sale:                $87,532
Minus down payment + closing:         -$50,540
Net gain from ownership:              $36,992
Retirement contributions (reduced):   $0 (no surplus)
Emergency fund:                       $8,500 (never grew)

SCENARIO B: KEEP RENTING, INVEST THE DIFFERENCE

Rent (3% annual increases, avg):      $1,802/mo
Total rent (5 years):                 $108,120
Down payment invested ($38,000 @ 8%): $55,833
Monthly savings invested              $60,573
($1,877/mo avg difference @ 8%):
Total portfolio value:                $116,406
Retirement contributions maintained:  $32,400 (at current rate)
Emergency fund:                       $18,500 (grew steadily)
```

**After 5 years:**
- **Buying:** $87,532 in illiquid home equity, depleted savings, no retirement growth, thin emergency fund
- **Renting + investing:** $116,406 in liquid investments, $18,500 emergency fund, $32,400 more in retirement

The renter is ahead by **$79,874** in total financial position. And their money is liquid -- accessible without paying $40,000 in selling costs.

### The Breakeven Point

For Marcus and Dana, at these numbers, buying breaks even with renting around **year 8**. If they're certain they'll stay in Raleigh for 10+ years, buying starts to make sense. For 5 years? They'd be paying a premium for the feeling of ownership.

### Their Contract Terms

After running the numbers, Marcus and Dana write their housing decision contract:

```
MARCUS AND DANA -- HOUSING DECISION CONTRACT

Decision: WAIT 18-24 months before buying.

TERMS:
1. Continue renting at $1,650/mo
2. Save $1,200/mo toward down payment (target: $60,000 for 15% down)
3. Pay off Dana's student loans aggressively ($800/mo → gone in 30 months)
4. Grow emergency fund to $15,000
5. Do NOT look at listings until all conditions met
6. Reassess in 18 months with updated numbers

WHEN WE BUY:
- Maximum purchase price: $350,000 (not what the bank approves)
- Minimum down payment: 15%
- Monthly housing cost cap: $2,800 (including ALL costs)
- Must have 6 months expenses ($18,000) in emergency fund AFTER closing
- Must have $5,000 in maintenance reserve AFTER closing
- Must stay 7+ years minimum

WHAT WE WON'T DO:
- Use emergency fund for down payment
- Stop retirement contributions
- Buy based on pre-approval amount
- Rush because "prices are going up"
- Let parents, friends, or agents pressure the timeline
```

This is the contract working. Not saying "never buy." Saying "buy when the math works, not when the emotions scream."

## Your Housing Contract

Homeownership can be a reasonable financial decision. It can also be the most expensive mistake of your life. The difference is whether you buy based on math or based on emotion.

**Step 1: Complete the audit before you shop.** Not during. Not after you've fallen in love with the kitchen. Before. If you can't fill in every line of that audit, you're not ready to buy. You're ready to be sold to.

**Step 2: Know your breakeven horizon.** If you're not staying 7+ years, renting is almost certainly cheaper. Run the numbers for your specific market. The New York Times rent vs. buy calculator is free and brutally honest.

**Step 3: Never let the bank tell you what you can afford.** The bank approved Marcus and Dana for $420,000. Their actual comfortable maximum was $350,000. The bank doesn't care if you eat ramen for 30 years -- they get paid either way. Your pre-approval is the bank's number. Your audit is your number. Use your number.

**Step 4: Build the financial architecture first.** [Module 02](/methodology/02/) teaches you to build the system before you make the big decisions. Emergency fund. Retirement contributions. Debt under control. Then and only then do you add the largest financial commitment of your life.

**Step 5: Run the math, not the fantasy.** [Module 09](/methodology/09/) is about the math of your life. Apply it here. Total interest paid. True monthly cost. Opportunity cost. Exit costs. Every number, no exceptions.

**Step 6: Make the decision with open eyes.** If you run all the numbers and buying still makes sense -- if you're staying long enough, if the market supports it, if you have the reserves, if you've accounted for every cost on that audit -- then buy with confidence. You've earned it.

But if you're buying because "renting is throwing money away" or because your parents said you should or because it feels like what adults do -- stop. Renting is paying for shelter. Buying is paying for shelter plus interest, plus taxes, plus insurance, plus maintenance, plus transaction costs, plus opportunity cost, plus risk.

The only thing you're "throwing away" when you rent is the illusion that homeownership is automatically wealth building. The contract doesn't deal in illusions. The contract deals in math. Do the math. Then decide.

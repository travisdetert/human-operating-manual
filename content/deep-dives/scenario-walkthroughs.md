# Scenario Walkthroughs

### What 6 Sprints of Real Life Actually Looks Like

---

## Why This Module Exists

The contract methodology sounds clean on paper. In practice, it's messy. People miss terms. They have bad weeks. They breach their contracts, feel terrible, consider quitting, adjust, and keep going. That messy, imperfect, nonlinear process IS the process.

These walkthroughs follow three people through 6 full sprints (24 weeks / ~6 months). The numbers are realistic, not aspirational. The failures are as instructive as the wins.

---

## Scenario 1: Devon, 27 -- Broke, Drowning in Debt, Addicted to DoorDash

**Starting Audit:**
- Income: $3,400/month (take-home, customer service job)
- Rent + utilities: $1,350
- Car payment + insurance: $480
- Student loans (min payment): $280
- Credit card debt: $8,400 at 24% APR (min payment: $168)
- Phone: $85
- Subscriptions: $127 (Netflix, Spotify, Hulu, gym he never goes to, Xbox Game Pass, Audible, a meditation app)
- Food: Spends $180 on groceries, $620 on DoorDash/restaurants/coffee
- Other (gas, random spending, Amazon): $350
- Monthly surplus: **-$240 (going deeper into debt every month)**
- Net worth: **-$32,000**
- Exercise: None
- Sleep: 5-6 hours (phone in bed until 1-2 AM)
- Screen time: 6.5 hours/day

Devon knows he's in trouble. He's been "meaning to" budget for two years. He downloaded three budgeting apps and used none of them.

### Sprint 1: The Wake-Up

**Contract V1.0 Terms:**
1. Track every purchase in phone notes app (just record it, don't try to change yet)
2. No DoorDash on weekdays (weekends allowed)
3. Screens off by midnight (not ideal, but better than 2 AM)
4. Cancel subscriptions I haven't used in 30 days

**Week 1:** Cancelled gym ($45), Audible ($15), meditation app ($10), Hulu ($16). Saved $86/month immediately. DoorDash on Tuesday -- breach. Trigger: worked late, got home exhausted, nothing in the fridge. Countermeasure: buy frozen meals as a backup for exactly this scenario.

**Week 2:** Tracking spending is making him uncomfortable. He spent $47 on Amazon on things he can't remember. DoorDash Wednesday -- breach again. Same trigger (no food at home). Bought 10 frozen meals for $35 from grocery store.

**Week 3:** No DoorDash breaches. The frozen meals worked. Screens off at midnight most nights (missed twice -- stayed up for a game release). Realized he's spending $23/week on energy drinks.

**Week 4:** Sprint review. Tracked spending for 28 days. Subscription cuts: $86/month saved. DoorDash spending dropped from $620 to $310. Total spending reduced by roughly $400.

**Compliance: 68%.** Below target. But $400/month found. That's a start.

### Sprint 2: Tightening

**Contract V1.1 (kept terms, added one):**
5. DoorDash maximum 2x per week (down from "weekends only" which was still 4-5 times)
6. Put $200/month extra toward credit card (from the savings found)

**Week 5-6:** DoorDash limited to once per week most weeks. Started packing lunch (sandwiches -- nothing fancy). Realized sandwiches cost about $2.50. DoorDash lunches were $18-22 each.

**Week 7:** Bad week. Fight with girlfriend. Ordered DoorDash 4 times. Stayed up until 3 AM three nights. Compliance for the week: about 30%. Breach review: stress eating and revenge bedtime procrastination (he read the anti-patterns module and recognized it). Countermeasure: texted his friend Marcus and said "I need someone to text when I'm about to order delivery at 11 PM." Marcus said "bet."

**Week 8:** Sprint review. DoorDash spending: $180 (down from $620 baseline). Paid $200 extra on credit card. CC balance: $8,100 (down from $8,400). Screen time down to 5.5 hours/day. Sleep averaging 6.5 hours.

**Compliance: 72%.** Trend: up from 68%. Week 7 was rough but he recovered instead of quitting.

### Sprint 3: The Plateau

**Contract V1.2:**
- All previous terms
- Added: Walk for 20 minutes, 3 days per week (never exercised, starting small)
- Added: Energy drink limit -- 1 per day max (was doing 2-3)

**Week 9-10:** Walking happened twice per week, not three. Energy drink cut to 1/day was harder than expected -- headaches (caffeine withdrawal). Bought a coffee maker ($30) and started making coffee at home.

**Week 11-12:** Walking hitting 3x/week now. Sleep improving -- now averaging 7 hours. Screens off at midnight holding. DoorDash spending stable at $150/month. Paid another $200 to credit card.

**Sprint review:** CC balance: $7,700. Monthly surplus flipped from -$240 to +$160. He's no longer going deeper into debt. He's slowly climbing out.

**Compliance: 76%.** Trend: 68% > 72% > 76%. Steady improvement.

### Sprint 4: The Wobble

**Week 13:** Car needed $800 in repairs. Emergency. Used credit card because no emergency fund. CC balance jumped back to $8,300. Devon was furious and demoralized. "What's the point? I pay it down and life just puts it back."

**Breach review (with himself):** "The problem isn't the car repair. The problem is I have no emergency fund. Every unexpected expense goes on the card. I'm fighting the credit card balance while the system keeps reloading it."

**Contract amendment:** Redirected $100/month from extra CC payment to a starter emergency fund. Slower debt payoff, but builds a buffer.

**Week 14-16:** Grudging acceptance. Emergency fund at $100 after first month. Walking consistently 3x/week now -- it's become routine. He actually wants to do it. Sleep at 7 hours. DoorDash once per week max.

**Compliance: 71%.** Dipped due to the car crisis and emotional fallout. But he didn't quit. He amended.

### Sprint 5: The Shift

**Week 17-18:** Emergency fund at $200. Something shifts -- Devon starts feeling different. He describes it to Marcus as "I don't feel behind anymore. I feel like I'm building something."

He asks for a raise at work. Gets a 4% bump -- $136/month more. Puts $100 of it toward CC, keeps $36 as a quality-of-life buffer.

**Week 19-20:** Started watching YouTube videos about investing. Not ready yet (still has CC debt), but interested for the first time. Walking 4x/week now -- upgraded on his own without being prompted.

**Sprint review:** CC balance: $7,600. Emergency fund: $300. DoorDash spending: $100/month (was $620). Screen time: 4 hours/day (was 6.5). Sleep: 7.5 hours average.

**Compliance: 82%.** New high.

### Sprint 6: The Compound Effect

**Week 21-24:** Devon's spending is now almost on autopilot. Meal prep on Sundays (learned from YouTube -- nothing fancy, just rice, chicken, and vegetables in containers). DoorDash once per week as a treat, not a default. Coffee at home. One energy drink per day, sometimes zero.

He told his girlfriend about the contract. She thought it was weird at first, then asked if she could do her own audit. He helped her.

**Sprint 6 review:**
- CC balance: $7,100 (down $1,300 from start)
- Emergency fund: $500 (halfway to starter goal)
- Monthly surplus: +$380 (was -$240)
- Weight: down 11 lbs (from walking + not eating delivery food 5x/week)
- Screen time: 3.5 hours/day
- Sleep: 7.5 hours
- Energy level: "I didn't know I could feel this way without being on vacation"

**6-Sprint compliance trend: 68% > 72% > 76% > 71% > 82% > 85%**

Devon didn't transform his life in 6 months. He's still in debt. He still orders DoorDash. He still stays up too late sometimes. But the trajectory is unmistakable. At this rate, his credit card is paid off in 18 months. His emergency fund is fully funded in 10 months. He's sleeping more, moving more, spending less, and for the first time in his adult life, he has a system.

## Scenario 2: Priya, 41 -- Successful Career, Failing Health, Invisible to Herself

**Starting Audit:**
- Income: $105,000/year ($6,800/month take-home)
- Married, one child (9 years old)
- No debt except $210K mortgage
- 401(k): $145,000
- Savings: $8,000
- The financial picture looks fine on paper
- Weight: 195 lbs at 5'4" (BMI: 33.5 -- obese)
- Last physical: 3 years ago
- Last dental: 2 years ago
- Exercise: none
- Sleep: 5.5 hours (works until 11 PM most nights, up at 5:30 AM)
- Eats fast food for lunch daily, skips breakfast, eats a large dinner
- Drinks 2 glasses of wine most nights to "wind down"
- Screen time: 4 hours/day (mostly work email after hours + scrolling before bed)
- Stress level: 8/10 consistently
- Has been avoiding a doctor's appointment because "I know they'll tell me to lose weight and I don't want to hear it"

Priya's problem isn't money. It's that she's optimized her career at the expense of everything else, and the physical and mental toll is compounding.

### Sprint 1: Confronting the Avoidance

**Contract V1.0 Terms:**
1. Schedule and attend a physical exam (including blood work)
2. Schedule and attend a dental appointment
3. Walk for 15 minutes during lunch break, 3 days per week
4. No work email after 9 PM

**Week 1-2:** Scheduled both appointments. Walking at lunch happened twice (rained one day, had a meeting another). No work email after 9 PM -- broke it 4 out of 10 nights. "Just one quick reply" always turned into 45 minutes.

**Week 3:** Physical exam results: pre-diabetic (A1C of 6.1), high blood pressure (142/90), high cholesterol (LDL 158). Doctor recommended medication and lifestyle changes. Priya cried in the car. Then she opened her contract and wrote: "This is why I'm doing this."

**Week 4:** Dental: two cavities, early gum disease. Scheduled fillings. Started blood pressure medication.

**Compliance: 62%.** Low. But the appointments happened. That was the real win -- she broke a 3-year avoidance pattern.

### Sprint 2: The Sleep Priority

After reading Module 03, Priya realized sleep was the linchpin. At 5.5 hours, she was running her entire life -- a demanding career, parenting, health recovery -- on a brain that was functionally impaired.

**Contract V1.1:**
- All previous terms
- In bed by 10:30 PM, screens off by 10 PM (targeting 7 hours)
- Take blood pressure medication daily (non-negotiable)
- Wine limited to weekends only (was daily)

**Week 5-6:** The sleep change was brutal. She was used to the 11 PM-1 AM window as "her time" (revenge bedtime procrastination -- she recognized the anti-pattern). First week she lay in bed frustrated for 45 minutes. Second week, she started falling asleep faster. Wine on weekdays was harder to drop than expected. She journaled instead on two nights and scrolled on three.

**Week 7-8:** Waking up feeling different. "I forgot what rested felt like." Walking consistently 3x/week at lunch. Colleague noticed and started joining her. Weight: 192 (down 3 lbs without trying -- mostly from cutting weeknight wine and being less tired/hungry in the evenings).

**Compliance: 73%.** Trend: 62% > 73%. The sleep change cascaded.

### Sprint 3-4: Building Momentum

Added meal prep (husband agreed to help -- this became a couples activity). Added 2 bodyweight strength sessions per week at home after the kid's bedtime (20 minutes, YouTube video).

**Sprint 3 compliance: 78%.** Sprint 4: 74% (holidays disrupted everything for 10 days, then recovered).

A1C recheck at month 4: **5.8** (improved from 6.1). Blood pressure: **128/82** (down from 142/90). Doctor was surprised and encouraging.

### Sprint 5-6: The Identity Shift

**Sprint 5:** Priya added a contract term she never expected: "I will leave work by 5:30 PM at least 3 days per week." Her career identity was "the one who's always on." Changing that felt like a risk. But her performance didn't drop -- it improved. She was sharper, more focused, making fewer errors. The extra hours had been low-quality work disguised as dedication.

**Sprint 6 review (month 6):**
- Weight: 181 (down 14 lbs)
- A1C: 5.7 (no longer pre-diabetic)
- Blood pressure: 124/78 (normal range)
- Sleep: 7 hours average
- Exercise: 5 sessions/week (3 walks + 2 strength)
- Wine: weekends only, sometimes none
- Work email after hours: eliminated (she turned off notifications)
- Stress level: 5/10 (was 8/10)
- "I didn't realize I was disappearing. I was so focused on performing at work that I stopped existing as a person."

**6-Sprint compliance: 62% > 73% > 78% > 74% > 81% > 84%**

## Scenario 3: Marcus, 54 -- Late Start, Limited Income, Big Heart

**Starting Audit:**
- Income: $38,000/year (warehouse worker, $2,650/month take-home)
- Divorced, two adult children (limited contact)
- Rent: $1,100 (1BR apartment)
- Car: paid off (2014 Civic, 180K miles)
- Debt: $2,200 on one credit card
- Savings: $400
- Retirement: $12,000 in an old 401(k) he forgot about
- Health: 230 lbs, bad knees, high blood pressure, takes lisinopril
- Smokes half a pack per day (20 years)
- Drinks 3-4 beers most nights
- Doesn't cook -- eats fast food or gas station food for most meals
- Watches TV 4-5 hours every night
- Screen time is actually low (not a phone person -- 1.5 hours/day)
- Loneliness level: extreme. He talks to coworkers and that's it. Weekends are TV and beer alone.

Marcus isn't in crisis. He's in stagnation. He's been on autopilot for years, going through the motions, and the motions are slowly killing him.

### Sprint 1: The Minimum

Marcus doesn't do big changes. His contract is deliberately small.

**Contract V1.0:**
1. Walk for 15 minutes after work, 3 days per week (knees can handle walking)
2. Eat one home-cooked meal per week (he barely cooks -- he'll start with eggs and toast)
3. Write down what he spends each day

**Week 1-2:** Walked twice the first week (not 3). Cooked eggs on Saturday. Spent tracking felt pointless but he did it. Realized he spends $14/day on gas station food -- that's $420/month on food that's barely food.

**Week 3-4:** Walking 3x/week now. Made a stir-fry from a YouTube video. It was bad but edible and cost $6 for 3 meals. Started noticing he has more energy on walking days.

**Compliance: 71%.** Not flashy. Not a transformation story. But he moved.

### Sprint 2: The Cigarette Math

Marcus read the cost tables. Half a pack per day = $150/month = $1,800/year = $26,000 over 10 years invested. Plus the health costs. He's smoked for 20 years -- he's spent roughly $36,000 on cigarettes and is staring at COPD if he doesn't stop.

**Added term:** Reduce to 5 cigarettes per day (was ~10). Not quitting -- reducing. He's honest: he can't cold-turkey at 54 after 20 years. But he can cut in half.

**Week 5-8:** Hardest thing he's done. Used the rubber band technique (snap a rubber band on his wrist when craving hits, delay 10 minutes). Got down to 6-7 per day by end of sprint. Not 5, but a 30-35% reduction.

Beer reduced to 2 per night on his own -- he didn't even put it in the contract. He just "didn't feel like the third one" after walking.

**Compliance: 69%.** Dipped because the smoking reduction was ambitious. But the trajectory matters.

### Sprint 3: The Loneliness Problem

Marcus's weekly review kept hitting the same note: "Nothing happened this weekend." He recognized the pattern. He'd been alone for 3 years and it was calcifying.

**Added term:** Attend one social activity per week. Anything. He chose his church's Wednesday night dinner -- free meal, people his age, low-pressure.

**Week 9-12:** Went to church dinner 3 out of 4 Wednesdays. Met a guy named Earl who also works with his hands and also lives alone. They started fishing on Saturday mornings. Marcus has a friend for the first time in years.

Cooking expanded to 3-4 home meals per week. Weight: 222 (down 8 lbs). Blood pressure check at pharmacy: improved.

**Compliance: 76%.** The loneliness intervention was the keystone change.

### Sprint 4-6: The Slow Build

Marcus's progress is slower than Devon's or Priya's. His income is lower, his health baseline is worse, and he's working against 20 years of entrenched habits. But the trend is there:

**Sprint 4:** Signed up for his company's 401(k) for the first time. Contributing 3% ($95/month). His employer matches 50% up to 6%. He's leaving free money on the table but plans to increase by 1% each quarter.

**Sprint 5:** Cigarettes down to 4-5 per day. Talked to his doctor about Chantix. Doctor was supportive. Started the prescription. Cooking 5 meals per week now. Gas station meals are the exception, not the rule.

**Sprint 6 review (month 6):**
- Cigarettes: 2-3 per day (down from 10 -- Chantix is helping)
- Weight: 217 (down 13 lbs)
- Beer: 1-2 per night (down from 3-4)
- Savings: $800 (up from $400)
- 401(k): contributing 4% now (increased once)
- Social connections: church group, fishing with Earl, talks to his daughter once per month (she was surprised when he called -- they're rebuilding slowly)
- Blood pressure: managed, doctor considering reducing medication
- TV time: down to 2-3 hours (replaced by walking, cooking, and fishing)
- Energy level: "I feel like I'm waking up after a long nap"

**6-Sprint compliance: 71% > 69% > 76% > 73% > 78% > 80%**

Marcus will never be rich. He'll never run a marathon. He's not going to write a book about his journey. He's a 54-year-old warehouse worker who stopped slowly dying and started slowly building. That's the whole story. And it's enough.

## What These Scenarios Show

1. **Nobody has a clean trajectory.** Every line wobbles. Every person has bad weeks. The system works not because it prevents failure, but because it contains it.

2. **The starting point doesn't matter as much as the trend.** Devon started at -$240/month. Marcus started with $400 in savings. Priya started with pre-diabetes. All three are in measurably better positions 6 months later.

3. **The cascade is real.** One change triggers others. Walking led to better sleep. Better sleep led to less drinking. Less drinking led to less spending. Less spending led to less stress. The dominoes fall.

4. **The breach protocol is the engine.** Every scenario has multiple breaches. Not one person quit because of a breach. They named it, found the trigger, made one adjustment, and continued.

5. **Contract version matters.** Starting too ambitious leads to failure. Starting small leads to momentum. Momentum leads to upgrades. Upgrades lead to transformation. But only in that order.

6. **Six months is enough to see the compound effect, but not to be "done."** Nobody finishes. The contract evolves indefinitely. That's the point -- it's an operating system, not a program you complete.

# The Credit Score Contract

### Because a Three-Digit Number Controls More of Your Life Than It Should

You didn't sign up for this system. Nobody asked you if you wanted a secret number — calculated by companies you've never interacted with, using data you didn't know was being collected — to determine whether you can rent an apartment, buy a car, get a phone plan, or land certain jobs.

But here you are. And the system isn't going away.

So you have two options: ignore it and pay the tax that comes with a bad score, or learn how the game actually works and play it deliberately. This deep dive is the second option.

Your credit score is not a measure of your financial health. It's not a measure of your character. It's a measure of **how profitable you are to lend to** — specifically, how likely you are to pay back debt on time and in full. That's it. A person with $2 million in savings and no credit history can have a worse score than someone with $50K in debt who's been making minimum payments for a decade.

Once you understand that the score measures **predictable borrowing behavior**, not virtue, the whole system starts making sense. And once it makes sense, you can move the number where you want it.

## What the Score Actually Measures

Your FICO score — the one that actually matters for most lending decisions — is built from five factors, weighted like this:

**Payment History: 35%**
Have you paid your bills on time? This is the single biggest factor and the simplest to control. One missed payment can drop your score 60-110 points depending on how high it was. A 30-day late payment stays on your report for **seven years**. The good news: this is binary. You either pay on time or you don't. Set up autopay and this factor takes care of itself.

**Credit Utilization: 30%**
How much of your available credit are you currently using? If you have $10,000 in total credit limits and you're carrying $3,000 in balances, your utilization is 30%. Lower is better. This factor is the fastest lever you can pull — and it has no memory. We'll go deep on this one.

**Length of Credit History: 15%**
How old is your oldest account? What's the average age of all your accounts? Longer is better. This is why closing old cards is almost always a mistake, and why starting early matters. You can't hack time.

**Credit Mix: 10%**
Do you have different types of credit? Revolving credit (credit cards, lines of credit) and installment credit (auto loans, student loans, mortgages). Having both types shows you can handle different payment structures. But this is only 10% — **never take on debt you don't need just to improve your mix.**

**New Credit: 10%**
How many new accounts have you opened recently? How many hard inquiries are on your report? Opening five credit cards in two months looks desperate to the algorithm. Spacing out applications matters.

### FICO vs. VantageScore

VantageScore uses similar factors but weights them differently — and it can generate a score with as little as one month of history, while FICO needs six months. But here's what matters: **the vast majority of lenders use FICO.** When you check your score through Credit Karma, you're seeing VantageScore. When a mortgage lender pulls your credit, they're looking at FICO. The numbers can differ by 20-40 points. Don't panic about the gap — just know which one you're looking at.

### Score Ranges

| Range | Rating | What It Means |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| 800-850 | Exceptional | Best rates on everything. You're the customer lenders compete for. |
| 740-799 | Very Good | Near-best rates. Marginal difference from 800+. |
| 670-739 | Good | Approved for most products. Rates are decent but not optimal. |
| 580-669 | Fair | Approved with higher rates. Some products unavailable. |
| 300-579 | Poor | Limited options. Secured cards, subprime loans, large deposits required. |

The national average FICO score is around 715. If you're above 740, you're getting nearly the best terms available. The jump from 740 to 800 is mostly bragging rights — the rate improvements are minimal. But the jump from 620 to 740 is worth **tens of thousands of dollars.**

Read that last line again. **$99,360.** Not in some hypothetical scenario. On a completely ordinary $300K home purchase that millions of people make every year.

And mortgages are just the start.

**Auto loans:** The difference between a 620 and a 760 score on a $30,000 car loan can mean 4-8 percentage points in interest rate. On a 60-month loan, that's $3,000-$7,000 in extra interest. For the same car.

**Insurance premiums:** In most states, auto insurers use credit-based insurance scores. Drivers with poor credit pay **40-70% more** for the same coverage as drivers with excellent credit. On a $1,500/year policy, that's $600-$1,050 extra per year. Every year.

**Apartment applications:** Landlords pull credit. A low score doesn't just mean higher security deposits — it can mean outright rejection, forcing you into more expensive or less desirable housing. Some landlords require scores above 650 or 700 just to apply.

**Employment:** In some states, employers can check a modified version of your credit report (not the score itself) for certain positions, particularly in finance, government, and roles involving money handling. A report full of collections and late payments can cost you the job.

Add it all up. Over a lifetime, the difference between a poor credit score and an excellent one can easily exceed **$200,000** in extra costs. This is a game worth learning.

## The Utilization Game

Credit utilization is the fastest-moving lever in your score, and it's the one most people misunderstand.

**The basics:** Utilization is your balance divided by your credit limit, expressed as a percentage. Under 30% is considered acceptable. Under 10% is optimal. Under 1% (but not zero) is ideal for score maximization.

**But here's the part people miss: utilization is measured per card AND overall.**

Say you have two credit cards:
- Card A: $5,000 limit, $500 balance → 10% utilization
- Card B: $5,000 limit, $4,500 balance → 90% utilization
- Overall: $10,000 total limit, $5,000 total balance → 50% utilization

Your overall utilization is 50% (bad), and Card B's individual utilization is 90% (devastating). Even though Card A looks fine on its own, the scoring model sees that you've nearly maxed out one card, which is a red flag for risk.

**The statement date trick:**

Most people think: "I pay my bill on time, so my utilization should be fine." Wrong. Your card issuer reports your balance to the credit bureaus on your **statement closing date**, not your due date. If you charge $4,000 on a card with a $5,000 limit during the month and pay it off when the bill arrives, the bureaus already recorded that 80% utilization when the statement closed.

The fix: **pay down your balance before the statement closes.** If your statement closes on the 15th and your payment is due on the 10th of the next month, make a payment on the 12th or 13th to bring the balance down before it gets reported. You'll still pay in full by the due date — you're just moving the timing.

**Spreading balances across cards:**

If you spend $2,000 per month and have three cards with $5,000 limits each:
- One card with $2,000 balance: 40% utilization on that card, 13% overall
- Three cards with $667 each: 13% utilization per card, 13% overall

The second option is better because no single card shows high utilization. Is it a hassle? A little. Is it worth doing when you're trying to optimize your score before a major purchase? Absolutely.

**Utilization has no memory.**

This is the most important thing to understand about utilization. Unlike payment history, which scars your report for seven years, utilization is a **snapshot**. If your utilization was 80% last month and you pay everything down to 5% this month, your score reflects 5%. Last month's 80% is gone. It doesn't matter anymore.

This means if you're planning to apply for a mortgage in 60 days, you can dramatically improve your score by simply paying down your balances in the month before the lender pulls your credit. It's not a trick — it's how the system works.

## Hard Pulls, Soft Pulls, and What Actually Matters

People treat hard inquiries like they're radioactive. They're not. Let's clear this up.

**Hard pull (hard inquiry):** Occurs when you apply for credit — a credit card, auto loan, mortgage, personal loan. The lender checks your full credit report. Effect on your score: **3-10 points**, typically closer to 5. A hard inquiry stays on your report for two years but **stops affecting your score after 12 months.**

**Soft pull (soft inquiry):** Occurs when you check your own score, when a lender sends you a pre-approval offer, when an employer runs a background check, or when a company checks your credit for non-lending purposes. **No effect on your score. None. Zero.**

**The rate-shopping window:** The scoring models aren't stupid. They know that when you're shopping for a mortgage or auto loan, you'll have multiple lenders pull your credit. So if multiple hard inquiries for the same type of loan happen within a **14-45 day window** (depending on the FICO version), they're counted as **one single inquiry.** This means you should absolutely shop around for the best rate — just do it within a compressed timeframe.

**The bottom line on hard pulls:** If applying for a credit card that gives you a better rewards structure or a higher limit (reducing your utilization) will cost you 5 points from the hard pull but improve your utilization ratio by 10+ points — that's a trade worth making every single time. Don't avoid beneficial financial moves because you're afraid of a 5-point temporary dip.

Five points doesn't change your rate tier. Forty points does. Focus on what moves the needle.

## The Credit Building Playbook

Where you start determines what you do. Find your starting point.

### No Credit History (Starting from Zero)

You have no score because you have no credit accounts. The system doesn't know you exist.

**Step 1: Get a secured credit card.** You put down a deposit ($200-$500), and that becomes your credit limit. The Discover it Secured and Capital One Platinum Secured are solid options. You'll get approved because the bank has your deposit as collateral.

**Step 2: Use it for one small, recurring charge.** Your phone bill. A streaming subscription. Something under $30/month. That's it. Don't use it for daily spending yet.

**Step 3: Set up autopay for the full statement balance.** Not the minimum payment. The full balance. Every month. This ensures on-time payment (35% of your score) and zero utilization growth.

**Step 4: Wait.** In 6-8 months, you'll have a FICO score, typically in the 650-700 range if you've done nothing wrong. In 12 months, you'll likely qualify for an unsecured card. Your secured deposit gets refunded.

This is boring. It's supposed to be boring. Boring is how you build a foundation.

### Thin File (1-2 Accounts, Limited History)

You have a score, but it's not robust. One credit card, maybe a student loan. Score is probably in the 630-690 range.

**Add a credit-builder loan.** Companies like Self (formerly Self Lender) offer small installment loans ($25-$150/month) specifically designed to add an installment account to your report. The money goes into a savings account you get access to when the loan is paid off. You're paying interest to build credit — it's a cost, but it's small and it adds the credit mix component.

**Become an authorized user.** If a parent, partner, or trusted family member has a credit card with a long history, low utilization, and perfect payment record, ask them to add you as an authorized user. Their account history gets added to your credit report. You don't need to use the card — you don't even need to have the physical card. You just get the benefit of their account age and payment history.

**Guardrails:** Only do this with someone whose credit behavior is excellent. If they miss payments or carry high balances, their bad behavior hurts your score too. And the primary cardholder is responsible for all charges — make sure the trust goes both ways.

### Damaged Credit (Score in the 500s to Low 600s)

You have negative marks — late payments, collections, maybe a charge-off. This is the hardest starting point, but it's absolutely recoverable.

**First: Get your free credit reports from AnnualCreditReport.com.** All three bureaus. Read every line.

**Dispute errors.** Studies show that roughly 1 in 5 credit reports contain errors. Wrong account, wrong balance, wrong payment status. File disputes with the bureau that has the error. They have 30 days to investigate.

**Address collections.** If you have collections accounts, you have options:
- **Pay-for-delete:** Contact the collection agency and negotiate. Offer to pay the balance (or a settlement amount) in exchange for them removing the collection from your report entirely. Get it in writing before you pay. Not all agencies will agree, but many will.
- **If they won't delete:** Paying the collection still helps under newer FICO models (FICO 9 ignores paid collections). And it stops the bleeding — an unpaid collection gets worse over time as the creditor may sell the debt, resulting in a new collection account.

**Start the secured card strategy** from the "no credit history" section above. You need positive accounts reporting alongside the negative marks.

**The rehabilitation timeline:**

| Months 1-3 | Pull reports, dispute errors, negotiate collections, open secured card |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Months 4-6 | Consistent on-time payments reporting, utilization under 30% |
| Months 7-12 | Score improvement of 50-80 points typical. Apply for unsecured card |
| Months 13-24 | Continued on-time payments. Negative marks aging. Score improvement of 80-120 points from starting point |
| Year 3+ | Oldest negative marks approaching removal. Score entering "good" range |

Late payments fall off after 7 years. Bankruptcies after 7-10 years. But their impact on your score diminishes significantly after 2-3 years. **Time is your most powerful tool here.** You can't skip it, but you can make the waiting period productive.

### Good Credit (700+) Aiming for Excellent (750+)

You're already in decent shape. The moves here are about optimization, not repair.

**Don't close old cards.** Even if you don't use them. That old card from college is helping your average account age. If it has no annual fee, keep it open. Put a small recurring charge on it once a year so the issuer doesn't close it for inactivity.

**Keep utilization under 10%.** You've been keeping it under 30% — now tighten it. Request credit limit increases on existing cards (this is usually a soft pull if done through the card issuer's website). More limit, same spending = lower utilization.

**Request limit increases every 6-12 months.** Most issuers let you request online. If your income has increased, update that too. A $5,000 limit becoming $10,000 instantly cuts your utilization in half.

**Let time work.** The difference between a 700 and a 760 is often just time. If your oldest account is 3 years old, getting to 5-7 years makes a measurable difference in the "length of history" component. Be patient.

**The authorized user hack for building length:** If you add your child or young adult family member as an authorized user on your oldest card, they inherit that account's age on their report. A 20-year-old with a 15-year-old account on their report has a massive head start. This is one of the most powerful legal credit-building tools available.

## Common Myths Demolished

**"Carrying a balance builds credit."**

**No.** This is the most expensive myth in personal finance. Interest payments do not help your score. The scoring model looks at whether you made on-time payments and what your utilization is. Paying in full every month gives you on-time payments (good) and resets utilization to near-zero (great). Carrying a balance gives you on-time payments (good) and keeps utilization elevated (bad), **plus** costs you 18-28% APR for the privilege. Pay in full. Every month. Always.

**"Closing old cards helps simplify my finances."**

**No.** Closing a card reduces your total available credit (utilization goes up) and eventually removes a seasoned account from your report (average age goes down). If the card has no annual fee, keep it open. If it has an annual fee you don't want to pay, call the issuer and ask to downgrade to a no-fee version — you keep the account age without the cost.

**"Checking my own score hurts it."**

**No.** Checking your score through any consumer service — Credit Karma, your bank's app, AnnualCreditReport.com, or the bureaus directly — is a soft pull. It does not affect your score. Check it monthly. Know your number.

**"You need multiple types of debt to have a good score."**

**Mostly no.** Credit mix is only 10% of your score. Having a credit card with a perfect payment history will build a solid score without ever taking on installment debt. Don't finance a car you don't need "to build credit." Don't take a personal loan you don't need "for the mix." If you happen to have a student loan or auto loan, great — it helps a little. But it's never worth paying interest on debt you don't need for a 10% scoring factor.

**"There's one credit score."**

**No.** You have dozens. FICO alone has multiple versions: FICO 8 (most widely used), FICO 9, FICO 10, FICO 10T, plus industry-specific versions for auto lending and credit cards. Then there are VantageScore 3.0 and 4.0. Each uses slightly different models and can produce different numbers from the same data. The score you see on Credit Karma (VantageScore) is not the same score a mortgage lender sees (usually FICO 2, 4, or 5 depending on the bureau). Don't obsess over the exact number — focus on the factors that drive all the models in the same direction.

## Your Credit Contract

Your credit score is a tool. It's a game with rules that are publicly available, strategies that are well-documented, and outcomes that are mathematically predictable. There is no mystery here. There is no luck involved. There are behaviors and their consequences, separated by time.

**Here's what you commit to:**

**This week:** Pull your credit reports from all three bureaus. Complete the audit above. Know your numbers.

**This month:** Identify your biggest score drag — utilization, late payments, thin file, or negative marks — and start addressing it. If it's utilization, pay down balances before your next statement closes. If it's a thin file, open a secured card or credit-builder loan. If it's negative marks, file disputes on anything inaccurate.

**Ongoing:** Pay every bill on time. Every bill. Every time. Set up autopay on everything. Check your score monthly. Review your full credit report every four months (rotate between the three bureaus — Equifax in January, Experian in May, TransUnion in September). Request credit limit increases twice a year.

**Before any major purchase:** Check your score 90 days out. If it's not where you need it, you have time to optimize utilization and let recent improvements take effect. Never walk into a mortgage application or car dealership without knowing your number.

Go back to your [Honest Audit](/methodology/01-honest-audit/) and add your credit score to the picture. It's part of your financial reality. Then look at your [Habit Engineering](/methodology/04-habit-engineering/) systems — because the behaviors that build credit are exactly the kind of small, automatic, repeatable actions that compound over time.

The credit scoring system wasn't built for your benefit. It was built for lenders. But the rules are transparent, the levers are accessible, and the payoff for learning to play well is measured in tens of thousands of dollars.

That's your contract. The three-digit number doesn't own you. You manage it — deliberately, consistently, and with full understanding of what it costs you when you don't.

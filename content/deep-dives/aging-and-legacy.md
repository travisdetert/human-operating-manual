# The Long Game

### Your Contract at 50, 60, and Beyond

---

## The Math Changes. The Urgency Increases.

At 25, you have roughly 19,000 days left. At 50, you have roughly 10,000. At 65, roughly 4,400.

These are not depressing numbers. They are clarifying ones. Every year that passes increases the value of each remaining day and decreases the tolerance for wasting them.

Most personal development content is written for 25-40 year olds. This module is for people in the second half of life, and for younger people who need to understand what's coming so they can prepare for it.

---

## Health Becomes Contract Term #1

In your 20s and 30s, health is background. Your body works. You can abuse it and recover. Hangovers last a morning. Injuries heal in weeks. Weight comes off when you try.

After 50, health moves to the foreground and stays there. The consequences of decades of neglect begin to present themselves. And the window for intervention narrows.

### What Happens After 50

**Muscle loss (Sarcopenia):** You lose 3-8% of muscle mass per decade after 30, accelerating after 50. Without resistance training, a 70-year-old has 30-40% less muscle mass than they did at 30. This isn't about aesthetics -- muscle loss means:
- Reduced metabolic rate (easier weight gain)
- Reduced bone density (higher fracture risk)
- Reduced functional capacity (can't carry groceries, climb stairs, get off the floor)
- Increased fall risk (falls are the leading cause of injury death in adults 65+)

**Contract term:** "I will do resistance/strength training at least 2x per week." This is the single most important exercise modality after 50.

**Cardiovascular decline:** Heart disease is the #1 cause of death. Risk increases significantly after 50. Blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar management become critical.

**Cognitive decline:** Some cognitive slowing is normal. Dementia is not. Regular exercise, social engagement, continued learning, and sleep quality are the best-supported protective factors.

**Cancer screening:** Colonoscopy every 10 years starting at 45. Mammograms annually for women 40+. Prostate screening for men 50+. Skin checks annually. These screenings catch cancer when it's treatable. Skipping them is not saving time -- it's gambling with your life.

### The Preventive Healthcare Schedule After 50

| What | How Often | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Physical exam with blood work | Annually | Blood pressure, cholesterol, blood sugar, thyroid, kidney/liver function |
| Colonoscopy | Every 10 years (if normal) | Colon cancer: 90% survival if caught early, 14% if caught late |
| Mammogram (women) | Annually | Breast cancer early detection |
| Prostate screening (men) | Discuss with doctor annually | Prostate cancer detection |
| Bone density scan (women) | At menopause, then as advised | Osteoporosis prevention |
| Eye exam | Annually | Glaucoma, macular degeneration, diabetic changes |
| Dental | Every 6 months | Gum disease links to heart disease |
| Skin check | Annually | Melanoma detection |
| Hearing test | Every 3 years after 50 | Hearing loss links to cognitive decline |
| Flu + pneumonia vaccines | Annually / as scheduled | Immune function declines with age |
| Shingles vaccine | Once, at 50+ | Shingles is excruciating and preventable |

**Every one of these appointments is a contract term.** Not "when I get around to it." Scheduled. On the calendar. Attended.

## The Financial Pivot

### From Accumulation to Protection and Distribution

Before 50, the financial game is accumulation: earn, save, invest, grow.

After 50, the game shifts to protection and distribution: How much do I have? How long does it need to last? How do I protect it? How do I access it without penalty?

### The "Enough" Calculation

How much do you actually need to retire? The "4% rule" is the most widely used guideline:

**Take your annual retirement spending needs. Multiply by 25. That's your target nest egg.**

If you need $50,000/year in retirement: $50,000 x 25 = **$1,250,000**
If you need $40,000/year: $40,000 x 25 = **$1,000,000**
If you need $30,000/year: $30,000 x 25 = **$750,000**

The 4% rule means you withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year 1, then adjust for inflation each year after. Historically, this has a 95%+ success rate over a 30-year retirement.

**But Social Security reduces the amount you need from your portfolio:**

If Social Security provides $24,000/year and you need $50,000/year total:
- Gap to fill from portfolio: $26,000/year
- Portfolio needed: $26,000 x 25 = **$650,000**

### Social Security: When to Take It

You can start Social Security at 62, but your benefit is permanently reduced by 25-30% compared to waiting until your full retirement age (67 for most people).

If you wait until 70, your benefit increases by roughly 8% per year beyond full retirement age -- a guaranteed 24% increase over what you'd get at 67.

| Monthly Benefit | At 62 | At 67 | At 70 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Example | $1,400 | $2,000 | $2,480 |

**The math:** If your full benefit is $2,000/month and you live to 85:
- Taking at 62: 23 years x $1,400/mo = **$386,400 total**
- Taking at 67: 18 years x $2,000/mo = **$432,000 total**
- Taking at 70: 15 years x $2,480/mo = **$446,400 total**

The breakeven point between 62 and 67 is roughly age 78. If you live past 78 (average life expectancy says you probably will), waiting produces more total income.

**Contract term:** "I will run my Social Security numbers at ssa.gov and make a deliberate decision about when to claim, based on math, not emotion."

### Long-Term Care: The Conversation Nobody Wants to Have

The average cost of a nursing home in the US: **$8,000-$10,000/month.** Assisted living: **$4,500-$5,500/month.** In-home aide: **$25-$35/hour.**

Medicare does NOT cover long-term care (beyond short-term rehabilitation). Most people don't know this until they need it.

Options:
- Long-term care insurance (best purchased in your 50s -- premiums skyrocket after 60)
- Self-insuring (requires significant assets)
- Medicaid (covers long-term care but requires spending down nearly all assets first)
- Hybrid life insurance/LTC policies

This is an uncomfortable conversation. Have it anyway. The cost of not planning is catastrophic.

## Career in the Second Half

### Age Discrimination Is Real

Despite being illegal, age discrimination in hiring is well-documented. Workers over 50 take an average of 30% longer to find a new job after layoff. They receive fewer callbacks for identical resumes when age indicators are present.

**Defensive strategies:**
- Keep your skills current. The most common bias against older workers is the assumption of technical obsolescence. Disprove it
- Maintain a network -- older workers who find jobs faster do so through relationships, not applications
- Consider consulting or freelancing as a bridge or alternative to traditional employment
- Remove graduation dates from your resume (reduces age signaling)
- Focus on recent, relevant accomplishments rather than tenure

### When to Start Planning for Not Working

Most people think about retirement as a switch: working > not working. A better model is a transition:

**Phase 1 (50-55):** Full-time work. Maximize retirement contributions. Assess whether you're on track for your "enough" number. If not, this is the last window to make aggressive changes.

**Phase 2 (55-62):** Begin thinking about what you WANT your post-career life to look like. Not just "not working" -- what are you doing? Purpose matters enormously after retirement. Retirees without purpose have significantly higher rates of depression and cognitive decline.

**Phase 3 (62-67):** Transition. This might mean part-time work, consulting, a different type of work, or full retirement depending on your financial position and preferences.

**Phase 4 (67+):** Whatever you designed. Your contract doesn't end at retirement -- it evolves to focus on health, relationships, purpose, and legacy.

## The Downsizing Contract

After 50, intentional simplification is powerful. Not as deprivation -- as liberation. You've spent decades accumulating things, commitments, and obligations. Many no longer serve you.

**Physical downsizing:**
- For every room in your house: "Do I use this? Do I need this? Would I buy this today?" If no to all three, it goes
- Smaller home = lower mortgage/rent, lower utilities, lower maintenance, lower property tax
- Moving from a house to a condo can free up $50,000-$200,000+ in equity while reducing monthly costs by $500-$1,500

**Commitment downsizing:**
- Which obligations are you maintaining out of habit rather than value?
- Which relationships are you sustaining out of obligation rather than genuine connection?
- Which activities fill your calendar but not your life?

**Financial downsizing:**
- Simplify accounts -- consolidate retirement accounts, reduce the number of credit cards, streamline insurance
- Reduce recurring expenses -- the subscription audit is even more important when income is fixed

## Legacy Beyond Money

### The Ethical Will

An ethical will is not a legal document. It is a letter to your family about what you believe, what you've learned, and what you hope for them. It's the most valuable thing most people never write.

```
ETHICAL WILL PROMPTS:

- The most important thing life taught me:
  ________________________________________________

- The mistake I most want my children/family to avoid:
  ________________________________________________

- What I want them to know about money:
  ________________________________________________

- What I want them to know about relationships:
  ________________________________________________

- What I want them to know about health:
  ________________________________________________

- The values I tried to live by:
  ________________________________________________

- What I'm most proud of:
  ________________________________________________

- What I wish I'd done differently:
  ________________________________________________

- What I want them to remember about me:
  ________________________________________________
```

Write it. Update it periodically. Keep it with your legal documents. This costs nothing and is priceless.

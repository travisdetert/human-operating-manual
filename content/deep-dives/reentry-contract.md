# The Re-Entry Contract

### Rebuilding a Life When the System Is Designed to Stop You

---

## What This Module Is

This module is for people re-entering civilian life after incarceration. It is written with respect, without pity, and without pretending the path is fair.

The reality: the American system punishes people far beyond the sentence. After serving your time, you face:

- Employment discrimination (legal in most states for private employers)
- Housing discrimination (most landlords run background checks; public housing restrictions)
- Credit destruction (years of no credit activity, possible pre-existing debt)
- Loss of social networks (relationships that didn't survive the sentence)
- Professional licensing restrictions (many states bar felony convictions from dozens of professions)
- Voting restrictions (varies by state)
- Technology gap (the world changed while you were inside)
- Probation/parole conditions that restrict movement, association, and employment options
- The psychological adjustment from a controlled environment to an uncontrolled one

None of this is fair. But fair doesn't pay rent. The contract framework works within the world as it is, not as it should be.

## This Is a V0.5 Situation

Re-entry is a crisis. Not an emotional crisis (though it may be that too) -- a structural one. You are rebuilding from near-zero in a system that has placed obstacles specifically in your path.

The full "My Contract" methodology is too much for the first 90 days of re-entry. Start with Version 0.5. Stabilize. Then build.

### Re-Entry V0.5: The First 90 Days

**The only priorities:**
1. Secure housing
2. Secure income
3. Meet all probation/parole requirements
4. Don't go back

Everything else -- financial architecture, career development, relationship rebuilding, health optimization -- comes after these four are stable.

## Employment: The Hardest Part

### The Reality of Job Searching With a Record

Roughly **75% of formerly incarcerated people are still unemployed one year after release.** This is not because they don't want to work. It's because:

- Background checks eliminate them from most corporate hiring processes
- Many industries (healthcare, education, finance, government, childcare) have statutory bars on felony convictions
- Resume gaps of years are difficult to explain
- Many don't have reliable transportation, a phone, appropriate clothing for interviews, or a stable address -- all things employers assume

### What Actually Works

**1. "Ban the Box" Employers**

Many states and cities have "Ban the Box" laws that prevent employers from asking about criminal history on the initial application. The question can still come up later, but you get to the interview first. Research which employers in your area follow this practice.

Companies known for second-chance hiring:
- **Dave's Hot Chicken, Greyston Bakery** -- explicitly hire returning citizens
- **Koch Industries** -- removed criminal history from applications
- **JPMorgan Chase** -- major second-chance hiring initiative
- **Home Depot, Walmart, Target** -- case-by-case consideration
- **Most staffing/temp agencies** -- often more flexible on background checks and provide immediate income while you search for permanent work

**2. Trades and Skills-Based Work**

Many trades are accessible with a record and pay well:
- Construction (general labor to start, apprenticeship to advance)
- Welding
- Auto mechanics
- Landscaping
- Warehouse/logistics
- Trucking (CDL restrictions vary by offense -- research your specific situation)
- Food service/restaurants (more flexible on hiring)
- Moving companies
- Cleaning/janitorial services

These provide income NOW while you build toward longer-term career goals.

**3. Entrepreneurship**

No employer? Become the employer. Lawn care, cleaning, handyman services, pressure washing, junk removal -- all can be started with minimal capital and no background check. You are your own hiring manager.

**Contract term:** "I will apply for at least [X] jobs per week until employed. I will contact at least one re-entry employment program this week."

### The Interview Question

When it comes up -- and it will -- here's the framework:

1. **Be honest.** Lying and getting caught is worse than the truth.
2. **Be brief.** "I was incarcerated for [offense]. I served my time. Since then, I've [specific positive action]."
3. **Pivot to value.** "What I can bring to this role is [specific skill, work ethic, reliability]."
4. **Don't over-explain or apologize excessively.** You served your sentence. You're here to work.

## Housing: The Second Hardest Part

### The Barriers

Most private landlords run criminal background checks. Public housing has restrictions on certain offenses (especially drug and sex offenses -- check your local PHA for specifics). Even family members in public housing can face eviction for housing a returning citizen in some jurisdictions.

### Options

**Transitional housing programs:** Many cities have programs specifically for re-entry. They provide temporary housing (30 days to 2 years) while you stabilize income and credit.

Resources:
- **National Reentry Resource Center** (nationalreentryresourcecenter.org)
- **Reentry.net** -- state-by-state resource directory
- **Local PHA (Public Housing Authority)** -- for understanding your specific eligibility
- **Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, and local faith-based organizations** -- often provide transitional housing and re-entry support
- **Your PO** -- parole officers often have lists of housing options for parolees

**Roommate situations:** Renting a room in someone's home often bypasses formal background checks. Look on local community boards, Facebook Marketplace, and word of mouth.

**Halfway houses:** Not ideal long-term, but they provide a stable address, which you need for employment, benefits, and PO compliance.

**Contract term:** "I will contact [X] housing resources this week and apply for [X] programs."

## Rebuilding Credit From Zero

If you went in with bad credit, it's probably worse now (collections, unpaid debts, possibly identity theft while you were unable to monitor). If you went in with no credit, you have none.

### Step 1: Find Out Where You Stand

Request your free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com. You may be shocked by what's on it. Look for:
- Debts you didn't know about
- Accounts you didn't open (identity theft during incarceration is common)
- Collections that may be past the statute of limitations (varies by state -- these may be uncollectable)

### Step 2: Dispute Errors

If there are accounts you didn't open or debts you don't recognize, dispute them with the credit bureaus. This is free. Instructions at consumer.ftc.gov.

### Step 3: Build New Credit

**Secured credit card:** You deposit $200-$500 and that becomes your credit limit. You use it for small purchases and pay it off in full every month. After 6-12 months of on-time payments, your credit score begins to build. Banks that offer secured cards: Discover, Capital One, Chime.

**Credit-builder loans:** Available at many credit unions. You "borrow" $500-$1,000, which is held in a savings account while you make monthly payments. When the loan is paid off, you get the money. This builds credit through payment history.

**Authorized user:** If a family member with good credit adds you as an authorized user on their credit card, their positive history on that account can boost your score. You don't even need to use the card.

**Contract term:** "I will pull my credit report within 30 days of release. I will apply for a secured credit card within 60 days."

## Social Network Reconstruction

Incarceration destroys social networks. Some relationships survived. Many didn't. And some of the relationships that survived are the ones you need to leave behind -- people connected to the behavior or environment that led to incarceration.

### Who to Reconnect With

- **Family members who supported you** (if they exist and the relationship is healthy)
- **Friends who are not connected to criminal activity**
- **Mentors, faith leaders, counselors** who knew you before
- **New connections through re-entry programs, support groups, employment, and community activities**

### Who to Avoid

This is one of the hardest parts of re-entry. The people you were closest to may be people whose proximity increases your risk of recidivism. This isn't judgment -- it's risk management.

**Contract term:** "I will not spend time with [specific people or in specific places] that are connected to my previous criminal activity. This is non-negotiable. My freedom depends on it."

### Building New Connections

- **Re-entry support groups:** People who understand your situation without explanation. Many exist through nonprofits and faith communities
- **Employment connections:** Coworkers become community
- **Volunteer work:** Gives you purpose, structure, and social connections while building resume evidence of reliability
- **Community activities:** Sports leagues, faith communities, classes at community colleges -- anything that puts you in regular contact with people in a structured environment

## Probation/Parole: The Contract Within the Contract

Your PO conditions ARE contract terms. They are non-negotiable, and violating them has consequences far worse than any breach protocol in this methodology.

**Embed PO conditions into your contract:**
- Check-in schedule: when, where, always on time
- Geographic restrictions: where you can and cannot go
- Association restrictions: who you can and cannot see
- Employment requirements: documented
- Curfew (if applicable): honored every single night
- Substance restrictions: followed without exception
- Drug testing schedule: prepared for at all times

**Pro tip:** Your PO can be your ally or your obstacle. Showing up on time, being honest, being employed, and demonstrating effort goes a long way. POs see hundreds of people. The ones who are clearly trying get more flexibility and support. Bring your contract to a PO meeting. Show them you have a plan. Many will respect it.

## The Technology Gap

If you served a significant sentence, the world you left is not the world you returned to. Smartphones, apps, social media, digital banking, online applications, ride-sharing -- all of this may be new or unfamiliar.

**Priority technology skills:**
1. **Smartphone basics:** Navigation, app installation, settings, messaging
2. **Email:** Required for almost all job applications and communications
3. **Job search platforms:** Indeed, LinkedIn, your state's job board
4. **Banking apps:** Mobile check deposit, bill pay, transaction monitoring
5. **Maps/Transportation:** Google Maps, bus/transit apps, rideshare

**Where to learn:** Public libraries offer free computer classes, internet access, and sometimes even technology lending programs. Ask a librarian -- they are trained to help and they don't judge.

## A Final Word

The system failed you in many ways. It may continue to fail you. But you are not the system. You are a person with a sentence that ended, and a life that didn't.

Every day you stay out is a victory. Every job you hold is a victory. Every bill you pay is a victory. Every old associate you don't call is a victory. These are not small things. They are the hardest things.

The contract doesn't make the path fair. It makes the path visible. One term at a time. One day at a time. One sprint at a time.

You paid your debt. Now build something that's yours.

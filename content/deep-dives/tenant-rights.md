# The Tenant's Contract

### Your Lease Is a Contract — And Right Now the Landlord Is the Only One Who's Read It

---

## Why This Matters

The homeownership deep-dives on this site — [true cost of owning](/deep-dives/homeownership/), [land contracts](/deep-dives/land-contracts/) — are important. But here's the reality: **36% of American households rent.** That's 44 million households. And for people in financial crisis, rebuilding from debt, coming out of incarceration, or starting over, renting isn't a stepping stone — it's the ground you're standing on. Maybe for years. Maybe for a long time.

"Renting is throwing money away" is something homeowners say. You know what's actually throwing money away? Losing a $1,500 security deposit because you didn't know your rights. Paying rent on a unit with no heat because you didn't know your landlord was breaking the law. Getting evicted because you didn't know you had 14 days to cure.

Your lease is a legal contract. This module teaches you to read it, enforce it, and protect yourself within it.

## Your Lease: What You're Actually Signing

### Read the Lease Before You Sign It

This sounds obvious. Almost nobody does it. The landlord or property manager slides a 10-15 page document across the table, and you sign because you need a place to live and the apartment is available.

Stop. Take the lease home. Read every page. You're committing to $12,000-$30,000+ in payments over the next year. You wouldn't buy a car without reading the terms (or you shouldn't — see [Predatory Lending](/deep-dives/predatory-lending/)). Don't commit to housing without reading the terms either.

If the landlord says "it's standard" or "everyone signs the same one," that's exactly why you should read it. "Standard" means it was written to protect the landlord, not you.

### What the Lease Must Include

Every state requires certain disclosures. At minimum, your lease should specify:

- **Rent amount and due date** — when it's due, what forms of payment are accepted, where to pay
- **Lease term** — month-to-month, 6 months, 12 months. This determines how much notice either party must give to end it
- **Security deposit amount** — and the conditions for its return
- **Late fee amount and grace period** — many states cap late fees and require a grace period (typically 3-7 days)
- **Who pays utilities** — which ones, starting when
- **Maintenance responsibilities** — what the landlord maintains vs. what you maintain
- **Rules on pets, guests, alterations, subletting**
- **Lead paint disclosure** (required by federal law for housing built before 1978)
- **Move-in condition report** — documents the condition of the unit at move-in

### Clauses That Should Concern You

**Waiver of habitability.** Some leases include a clause where you agree to accept the unit "as-is" and waive the landlord's obligation to maintain it. In most states, this clause is unenforceable — you cannot waive the implied warranty of habitability. But landlords include it because tenants don't know it's unenforceable.

**Automatic renewal.** The lease converts to a new 12-month term automatically unless you give notice 60-90 days before it ends. If you miss the notice window, you're locked in for another year. Mark the notice deadline on your calendar the day you sign.

**Excessive late fees.** Some leases charge $50-$100/day in late fees. Many states cap late fees at a reasonable percentage (typically 5-10% of monthly rent). If your lease charges more than that, it may not be enforceable.

**Landlord's right to enter without notice.** Most states require 24-48 hours' notice before a landlord enters your unit, except in emergencies. If the lease says the landlord can enter "at any time," that clause conflicts with state law in most jurisdictions.

**Broad liability waiver.** "Tenant releases landlord from all liability for injury or property damage." This is often unenforceable for negligence, but its presence in the lease discourages tenants from pursuing legitimate claims.

**Non-refundable "deposits."** If it's called a deposit, it's refundable in most states. Some landlords rename deposits as "non-refundable fees" (move-in fee, cleaning fee, administrative fee). Know your state's rules on what's refundable and what isn't.

## Security Deposits: Getting Your Money Back

Security deposit disputes are the #1 landlord-tenant conflict. Nationally, landlords withhold deposits — partially or fully — in roughly 30% of move-outs. Much of that withholding is illegal but goes unchallenged because tenants don't know their rights.

### What the Law Generally Requires

State laws vary, but most states require:

**Deposit limits.** Many states cap security deposits at 1-2 months' rent. If your landlord charges 3 months' rent as a deposit in a state with a 2-month cap, the excess is illegal.

**Written accounting.** When the landlord keeps any portion of your deposit, most states require a written, itemized list of deductions within 14-30 days of move-out. If the landlord doesn't provide this within the deadline, you may be entitled to the full deposit back — even if there was legitimate damage.

**Normal wear and tear.** Landlords cannot deduct for normal wear and tear. This means:
- Faded paint from sunlight: normal wear
- Holes in the wall from hanging pictures: gray area (small nail holes are usually normal wear; large holes are damage)
- Worn carpet from walking: normal wear
- Stained carpet from a spill: damage
- Scuffed floors: normal wear
- Burned countertop: damage

**Interest on deposits.** Some states (and some cities) require landlords to hold deposits in interest-bearing accounts and pay you the interest.

### How to Protect Your Deposit

**At move-in:**

```
MOVE-IN DOCUMENTATION CHECKLIST
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

□ Walk through every room with a checklist
□ Photograph every wall, floor, ceiling, fixture
□ Photograph inside every appliance (oven, fridge,
  dishwasher, microwave)
□ Photograph any existing damage:
  □ Stains, scratches, dents, holes
  □ Cracked windows or tiles
  □ Broken fixtures or hardware
  □ Appliance condition (burns, rust, missing parts)
  □ Condition of doors and locks
  □ Condition of blinds, screens, and window coverings
□ Test every faucet, light switch, outlet, appliance
□ Note anything that doesn't work on the move-in form
□ Get the landlord to sign the move-in condition report
□ Keep your copy — do NOT give it up

DATE: ____________  PHOTOS SAVED TO: ____________

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

make your own. The photos are your evidence.
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
```

**During the lease:**
- Report maintenance issues in writing (email or text — anything with a timestamp)
- Keep copies of every communication with the landlord
- Photograph any damage that occurs and report it promptly

**At move-out:**
- Clean the unit thoroughly
- Photograph every room in the same positions as your move-in photos
- Do a walkthrough with the landlord if possible (bring your move-in photos)
- Return keys and get a receipt or written confirmation
- Provide a forwarding address in writing for the deposit return

**If the landlord wrongfully withholds:**

1. Send a written demand letter (certified mail, return receipt) citing your state's security deposit statute and requesting the full deposit
2. If no response within 14 days, file a claim in small claims court. Filing costs $30-$75. Many states award double or triple damages for wrongful withholding
3. Small claims court doesn't require a lawyer. Bring your move-in photos, your move-out photos, your lease, and the landlord's deduction list (or lack thereof)

Small claims court works. Landlords who withhold deposits illegally count on tenants not pursuing it. Most tenants don't. Be the one who does.

## Habitability: What Your Landlord Must Provide

The "implied warranty of habitability" is a legal doctrine that exists in nearly every state. It means your landlord must maintain the rental unit in a condition fit for human habitation. This isn't optional. It's not a favor. It's the law.

### What Habitability Requires

At minimum, your unit must have:

- **Functioning heat** (in cold months) and usually air conditioning (in jurisdictions that require it)
- **Hot and cold running water**
- **Working plumbing and sewage**
- **Working electrical systems**
- **Weatherproofing** — roof that doesn't leak, windows that close, doors that lock
- **Freedom from pest infestations** — roaches, mice, rats, bed bugs (responsibility varies by state, but landlords generally must address infestations)
- **Structural safety** — no collapsing ceilings, rotting floors, exposed wiring
- **Working smoke and carbon monoxide detectors**
- **Compliance with local building and housing codes**

### What to Do When the Landlord Won't Fix It

**Step 1: Put it in writing.** Send a written maintenance request (email is fine) describing the problem. Include photos. Keep a copy. This creates a paper trail.

**Step 2: Give reasonable time.** What's "reasonable" depends on the problem. No heat in January? That's a 24-hour emergency. A dripping faucet? A week is reasonable. A broken dishwasher? Two weeks.

**Step 3: Follow up in writing.** If the landlord doesn't respond or fix the issue, send a follow-up. Reference the first request and the date. "This is my second request regarding [issue], originally reported on [date]."

**Step 4: Know your remedies.** Depending on your state, you may have:

**Repair and deduct.** You hire someone to fix the problem and deduct the cost from your next rent payment. Most states limit this to a certain dollar amount (often one month's rent). You must follow specific procedures — check your state's rules.

**Rent withholding / rent escrow.** You stop paying rent (or pay it into a court escrow account) until the landlord makes repairs. This is a powerful tool but you MUST follow your state's procedures exactly. Withholding rent incorrectly gives the landlord grounds to evict you.

**Code enforcement.** Call your city or county building/housing inspector. They'll inspect the property and can order the landlord to make repairs. This is free and creates an official record.

**Constructive eviction.** If the unit is so uninhabitable that you're forced to leave (no heat, no water, severe mold), you may be able to break the lease without penalty. Document everything.

### What Retaliation Looks Like

In most states, it is illegal for a landlord to retaliate against you for exercising your legal rights. Retaliation includes:
- Raising your rent because you filed a complaint
- Refusing to renew your lease because you called code enforcement
- Shutting off utilities
- Changing the locks
- Harassing you to leave

If your landlord retaliates after you exercise a legal right, document it and contact your local legal aid office. Retaliation claims are strong claims and legal aid attorneys handle them regularly.

## Eviction: Your Rights When It Happens

Eviction is not "the landlord tells you to leave." Eviction is a legal process with specific steps, timelines, and protections. A landlord cannot change your locks, remove your belongings, shut off your utilities, or physically remove you without going through the court process. If they do any of these things, it's an illegal "self-help" eviction and you have significant legal remedies.

### The Eviction Process (General)

**Step 1: Notice.** The landlord must give you written notice. The type of notice depends on why they're evicting:
- **Pay or quit** (nonpayment of rent): typically 3-14 days to pay the overdue rent
- **Cure or quit** (lease violation): typically 14-30 days to fix the violation
- **Unconditional quit** (serious violation): no cure period, but these are rare and usually reserved for illegal activity or repeated violations
- **End of lease / no-fault**: typically 30-60 days' notice

**Step 2: Right to cure.** For nonpayment, you usually have a right to pay the overdue rent within the notice period and stop the eviction. This is called "curing the default." Pay within the notice period — and get a receipt.

**Step 3: Court filing.** If you don't cure, the landlord files an eviction lawsuit (called "unlawful detainer," "summary process," or "forcible entry and detainer" depending on your state). You'll receive a court summons.

**Step 4: Court hearing.** You have the right to appear and present your defense. Show up. Many tenants don't appear, and the landlord wins by default. If you show up, you can raise defenses:
- The landlord didn't follow proper notice procedures
- You paid the rent (bring proof)
- The eviction is retaliatory
- The unit has habitability violations
- The landlord is discriminating against you (Fair Housing Act)

**Step 5: Judgment and appeal.** If the court rules against you, you typically have a move-out deadline (often 7-14 days) and possibly an appeal window.

**Step 6: Physical removal.** Only a sheriff or constable can physically remove you. Not the landlord. Not the landlord's cousin. Not private security.

### Emergency Rental Assistance

Federal and state Emergency Rental Assistance programs have distributed billions of dollars since 2021. Funding varies by location and year — some areas have exhausted funds, others still have money available. Check:
- Your state's ERA portal
- 211.org
- Your county's community action agency
- Local nonprofits (Salvation Army, St. Vincent de Paul, Catholic Charities all offer rental assistance regardless of religion)

Even partial assistance — one month of back rent — can stop an eviction.

## Rent Increases: What's Legal and What's Negotiable

### The Rules

**During a lease term:** Your landlord generally cannot raise your rent until the lease expires. A 12-month lease at $1,200/month means $1,200/month for 12 months. Period.

**At lease renewal:** The landlord can raise rent by whatever amount they want, unless:
- You live in a rent-controlled or rent-stabilized area (select cities — New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Washington DC, and others)
- Your state has a rent increase cap (Oregon caps annual increases; California's Tenant Protection Act caps increases for qualifying properties)
- The increase is retaliatory (e.g., raised because you filed a complaint)
- The increase is discriminatory (e.g., targeted at you based on a protected class)

**With proper notice:** Most states require 30-60 days' written notice before a rent increase takes effect.

### How to Negotiate Rent

Landlords don't want vacancies. A vacant unit costs the landlord one month's rent (or more) in lost income, plus turnover costs (cleaning, painting, marketing, showing). On a $1,500/month unit, a one-month vacancy costs $1,500-$3,000. A $100/month increase that causes you to leave costs the landlord more than keeping you at the current rent.

**Your leverage points:**
- You've been a reliable tenant (on-time payments, no complaints, no damage)
- You're willing to sign a longer lease (18 months or 2 years)
- Comparable units in the area are listed at lower prices (do the research on Zillow, Apartments.com)
- The unit needs repairs that the landlord hasn't addressed
- You can pay a few months in advance

**Sample negotiation language:**

"I'd like to stay and I've been a reliable tenant for [X] years. The proposed increase to $[amount] is above what comparable units are renting for in this area — I found [X examples] at $[amount]. I'd like to propose $[your counter] per month, and I'm willing to sign a [longer term] lease to provide stability for both of us."

Put the counter-offer in writing (email is fine). Be factual, not emotional. The landlord will either accept, counter, or decline. If they decline, you decide whether the increase is worth staying or whether moving makes more financial sense.

## Renters Insurance: The $15/Month You're Skipping

Roughly 55% of renters don't have renters insurance. Here's what that means: if your apartment floods, burns, or is burglarized, you lose everything and have no recourse. The landlord's insurance covers the building — not your belongings, not your liability, not your temporary housing costs.

### What Renters Insurance Covers

**Personal property.** Everything you own inside the unit — furniture, electronics, clothes, kitchen items. If it's stolen, damaged in a fire, or destroyed by a covered event, the policy pays to replace it.

**Liability.** If someone is injured in your unit and sues you, renters insurance covers legal defense and damages. If your dog bites a guest, if a visitor slips on your wet floor, if your kid breaks a neighbor's window — liability coverage pays.

**Additional living expenses.** If your unit becomes uninhabitable (fire, flood, major damage), the policy pays for temporary housing (hotel, short-term rental) and additional food costs.

**Off-premises coverage.** Your laptop stolen from your car? Your bike stolen from the rack at work? Most renters policies cover personal property off-premises too.

### What It Costs

──────────────────────────────────────────────────

Liability coverage:     $100,000-$300,000
Deductible:             $500-$1,000
Monthly premium:        $12-$25

Walk through your apartment right now. Add up what it would cost to replace everything — bed, couch, TV, clothes, dishes, pots, your phone, your computer, your shoes, your coat. For most people, the answer is $10,000-$30,000. Renters insurance covers that for $15/month.

**How to get it:** Any major insurer (State Farm, Allstate, Progressive, GEICO, Lemonade) offers renters policies online. Get quotes from 3 providers — they take 5 minutes each. If you already have car insurance, bundling renters insurance with the same company usually drops the cost by 10-15%.

## When to Break a Lease (and How)

Breaking a lease has consequences. Understanding those consequences lets you make a rational decision instead of a panicked one.

### Legal Reasons to Break a Lease

In most states, you can break a lease without penalty for:
- **Active military duty** (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act — federal law)
- **Domestic violence** (many states allow victims to break leases with documentation)
- **Uninhabitable conditions** that the landlord has refused to fix after proper notice
- **Landlord harassment or illegal entry**
- **Landlord retaliation** for exercising legal rights

### Financial Consequences of Breaking

If you don't have a legal reason, breaking a lease typically means:
- **Early termination fee.** Many leases specify a fee (often 1-2 months' rent). Pay it and you're done.
- **Remaining rent obligation.** Without an early termination clause, you may owe rent for the remaining lease term. However, in most states, the landlord has a **duty to mitigate** — they must make reasonable efforts to re-rent the unit. You owe rent only until a new tenant moves in.
- **Security deposit forfeiture.** The landlord may apply your deposit to unpaid rent or the early termination fee.

### The Math of Breaking vs. Staying

Sometimes breaking a lease is the right financial decision:

```
BREAK THE LEASE vs. STAY — $1,500/MONTH RENT
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
6 months remaining on lease
Early termination fee: $3,000 (2 months)

SCENARIO: Found a $1,100/month apartment

Cost to break:            $3,000
Monthly savings:          $400
Breakeven:                7.5 months
Savings over 12 months:   $1,800 net

VERDICT: If you're staying in the new place 8+
months, breaking the lease saves money.
──────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

Do the math. Don't let the emotional weight of "breaking a lease" prevent you from making the financially rational choice.

## Housing Assistance Programs

If you're struggling to afford housing, these programs exist and you should use them.

### Section 8 / Housing Choice Vouchers

The largest federal rental assistance program. You pay 30% of your adjusted gross income toward rent; the voucher covers the rest. Eligibility is based on income (generally under 50% of area median income).

**The reality:** Waitlists are long. In many cities, 2-5 years. In some cities, the waitlist is closed entirely. Apply anyway. Apply in every jurisdiction where you could live. The waitlist moves — slowly, but it moves.

**How to apply:** Contact your local Public Housing Authority (PHA). Find yours at hud.gov.

### Other Programs

- **Public housing.** Government-owned rental units at reduced rates. Same income limits as Section 8, similar waitlists. Apply through your local PHA.
- **LIHEAP.** Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program. Helps pay heating and cooling bills. Apply through your state.
- **HUD housing counseling.** Free counseling for renters facing eviction, looking for affordable housing, or transitioning from homelessness. Call (800) 569-4287.
- **Local rapid rehousing programs.** For people experiencing or at risk of homelessness. Short-term rental assistance and case management. Contact 211.

## The Bottom Line

Renting isn't failure. Renting isn't temporary. For millions of people, renting is their housing situation for years or decades, and there's nothing wrong with that. What's wrong is renting without understanding your rights — paying for housing you're legally owed repairs on, losing deposits you're legally entitled to, and accepting evictions you could legally fight.

Your landlord has a property manager, a standard lease, and a lawyer on retainer. Your side of this contract has been underrepresented for your entire renting life. That changes when you read the lease, document everything, know the law, and enforce your rights.

The contract you sign with your landlord governs where you sleep, where your kids sleep, and where you keep everything you own. Read it like it matters. Because it does.

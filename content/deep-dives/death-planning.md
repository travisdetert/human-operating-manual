# The Death Contract

### The Legal, Financial, and Logistical Mechanics of Dying

---

## You Are Going to Die. Plan Accordingly.

This is the module nobody wants to read. That's exactly why you need to read it.

You will die. The people you love will die. Statistically, someone you know will die this year. When that happens — when it happens to *you* or to someone whose affairs you'll need to handle — the absence of a plan will cost your survivors thousands of dollars, hundreds of hours, and an incalculable amount of stress during the worst period of their lives.

Here are the numbers your avoidance is betting against:

**60% of American adults have no will.** Not "haven't updated their will." Have no will at all. Among adults under 35, that number is 75-80%. Among parents with minor children — the people who arguably need a will most urgently — roughly 35% have nothing in writing.

**About 150,000 Americans between age 25 and 44 die every year.** Car accidents. Cancer. Heart attacks. Overdoses. Suicide. Strokes. Violence. You don't get to schedule your death, and you don't get to plan *after* it happens. The 25-year-old who dies in a car accident on I-95 doesn't get a do-over because they thought estate planning was something you did at 65.

**What happens when you die without a plan?** The state decides everything. Every state has intestacy laws — pre-written rules about who gets your stuff when you didn't bother to say. These laws are blunt instruments. They follow bloodlines and marriage certificates. They don't know about:

- Your partner of 12 years you never married
- Your estranged parent you haven't spoken to in a decade
- Your stepchildren you raised since they were three
- Your best friend who was supposed to get the car
- The sibling who would be a terrible guardian for your kids

Without a will, your unmarried partner gets nothing. Your estranged parent might inherit everything. Your stepchildren have no legal claim. Your wishes are irrelevant because you never wrote them down.

**The cost of dying without a plan** runs between $5,000 and $15,000+ in probate court costs, attorney fees, and administrative expenses — before anyone inherits a dime. With a contested estate, that number can exceed $50,000 easily and drag on for years. Meanwhile, a basic will costs $200-$800 if you do it yourself, or $1,000-$3,000 with an attorney for a complete estate planning package.

You're not planning for your death. You're planning for the people who survive it. Every hour you spend on this saves them ten hours of confusion, conflict, and bureaucracy during the worst week of their lives.

This is not a retirement activity. This is a right-now activity.

## The Essential Documents: Your Legal Operating System for Death

There are six documents that form a complete death plan. You don't need all of them — but you need to understand all of them to know which ones you need. Each one does a different job, and none of them can substitute for the others.

Think of these as an operating system. Your will is the core program. The trust is an optional upgrade. The powers of attorney are the emergency protocols. The advance directive is the shutdown procedure. And the HIPAA authorization is the access key that lets anyone run the system at all.

Without this operating system, your family will be locked out of your own life.

### Last Will and Testament

**What it does:** Specifies who gets your stuff, names a guardian for minor children, and appoints an executor (the person who manages the whole process).

**Who needs it:** Everyone over 18. No exceptions. If you own anything, owe anything, or have anyone who depends on you — you need a will. If you have minor children, a will is the *only* legal document where you can name their guardian. Without it, a judge decides. A judge who has never met your family.

**What it covers:**
- Distribution of assets not covered by beneficiary designations (more on this in Section 3)
- Naming an executor/personal representative
- Naming guardians for minor children
- Specific bequests (the watch goes to your brother, the books go to your best friend)
- Instructions for pets
- Disinheriting anyone you want to exclude (yes, you have to explicitly state this in many states)

**What it doesn't cover:**
- Anything with a named beneficiary (retirement accounts, life insurance, POD/TOD accounts)
- Assets held in a trust
- Jointly owned property with right of survivorship
- What happens if you're incapacitated but alive

**Cost with an attorney:** $300-$1,000 for a simple will. More if your situation is complex (blended families, business ownership, significant assets).

**Cost DIY:** $0-$200. Services like FreeWill (literally free for basic wills), Nolo's Quicken WillMaker ($100), or Trust & Will ($159+) produce legally valid documents in most states. Many state bar associations offer free will-drafting clinics.

**Without it:** The state's intestacy laws decide everything. Your executor is appointed by a judge (who might pick someone you'd hate). If you have minor children, a court decides who raises them. Your assets go through probate, which is public — meaning anyone can see what you had and who got it.

**The bottom line:** A will is the bare minimum. It's the "I did something" of death planning. It's not perfect — it still goes through probate, it's a public document, and it can be contested — but it's infinitely better than nothing.

### Revocable Living Trust

**What it does:** Holds your assets in a legal container that passes them to your beneficiaries without going through probate. You control everything while you're alive (you're the trustee). When you die, your successor trustee distributes assets according to the trust document. No court involvement. No public record. No waiting.

**Who needs it:** Not everyone. Despite what estate planning attorneys will tell you (they charge $1,500-$5,000 to set one up), a trust is most valuable if you:

- Own real estate, especially in multiple states (each state requires a separate probate proceeding — a trust avoids all of them)
- Have assets above your state's small estate threshold (varies: $25,000 in some states, $150,000+ in others)
- Want privacy (probate is public; trusts are not)
- Want to control distribution timing (e.g., "my kids get their inheritance at 25, not 18")
- Have a blended family where distribution could get contested
- Own a business

**Who probably doesn't need one:**
- Young people with minimal assets and no real estate
- People whose assets are mostly in retirement accounts and life insurance (these pass by beneficiary designation anyway)
- People in states with simplified probate (Texas, for example, has relatively cheap and fast probate)

**Critical warning — the "unfunded trust" trap:** A trust only works if you actually transfer your assets into it. This is called "funding the trust." You have to re-title your bank accounts, your brokerage accounts, your real estate — everything — into the name of the trust. An estimated 50-70% of people who create trusts never fully fund them, which means their assets go through probate anyway, and they paid $1,500-$5,000 for a fancy paperweight.

**Cost with an attorney:** $1,500-$5,000 for the trust document plus a pour-over will (a backup will that catches anything not in the trust).

**Cost DIY:** $200-$500 through online services. Be very careful here — trust funding is the hard part, and DIY services vary widely in how well they walk you through it.

**Without it:** Your assets go through probate. Depending on your state, this takes 6-18 months and costs 2-5% of the estate in fees. That's $5,000-$25,000 on a $500,000 estate.

### Durable Power of Attorney (Financial)

**What it does:** Names someone to manage your finances if you become incapacitated — if you're in a coma, have a stroke, develop dementia, or are otherwise unable to pay bills, manage investments, file taxes, or access accounts.

**Who needs it:** Everyone over 18. This is not optional. Without it, your family will need to go to court to get a conservatorship or guardianship over your finances. That process costs $2,000-$10,000, takes weeks to months, and requires ongoing court reporting. Meanwhile, your bills don't pay themselves and your mortgage doesn't pause.

**What your agent can do (you define this):**
- Pay bills and manage bank accounts
- File tax returns
- Manage investments
- Sell or manage property
- Handle insurance claims
- Make gifts (if you authorize it)
- Manage business affairs

**"Durable" vs. "Springing":** A durable power of attorney takes effect immediately when signed (though your agent only uses it when needed — you still control your finances while you're capable). A springing power of attorney only activates when you're declared incapacitated by a doctor. Springing sounds better in theory but creates delays in practice — banks and financial institutions often refuse to honor them until they get medical documentation, which can take days or weeks. Most attorneys recommend durable.

**Cost with an attorney:** $150-$500. Often included in an estate planning package.

**Cost DIY:** $0-$50. Many states provide free statutory forms. Your state bar association website likely has one.

**Without it:** Court-supervised conservatorship. $2,000-$10,000 to establish, ongoing reporting requirements, and a judge making decisions about your money.

### Healthcare Power of Attorney (Healthcare Proxy)

**What it does:** Names someone to make medical decisions for you if you can't make them yourself. This is different from a living will (which states your wishes in advance) — this is a person who can respond to situations you didn't anticipate.

**Who needs it:** Everyone over 18. Yes, even healthy 22-year-olds. Especially healthy 22-year-olds. If you're in a car accident at 23 and can't speak for yourself, your parents no longer have automatic medical decision-making authority once you turn 18. Without this document, they might be locked out of your medical decisions entirely while a court sorts it out.

**Choose someone who will:**
- Actually follow your wishes, even if they disagree
- Stay calm in a crisis
- Be available (geographically and emotionally)
- Advocate forcefully with doctors if needed
- Handle family disagreements about your care

**Don't automatically choose your spouse or parent.** Choose the person who will be the best advocate when you can't speak. Sometimes that's a sibling, a friend, or an adult child. Choose the person who will execute your wishes, not the person whose feelings would be hurt if you didn't choose them.

**Cost with an attorney:** $0-$150. Usually included in an estate planning package.

**Cost DIY:** $0. Every state has a free statutory form. Search "[your state] healthcare power of attorney form."

**Without it:** Your state's surrogate decision-making hierarchy kicks in — typically spouse, then adult children, then parents, then siblings. If those people disagree (and they often do, under stress), a court decides. See: Terri Schiavo, a 15-year legal battle that cost millions and tore a family apart because there was no clear healthcare proxy.

### Advance Directive / Living Will

**What it does:** States your wishes for end-of-life medical care in advance — specifically, what you want when you're terminally ill, permanently unconscious, or in a condition with no chance of recovery.

**What you specify:**
- Mechanical ventilation (breathing machine): yes, no, or trial period
- Artificial nutrition and hydration (feeding tubes): yes or no
- CPR if your heart stops: yes or no
- Dialysis: yes or no
- Antibiotics for infection: yes or no
- Pain management: always yes — specify that you want full pain management even if it hastens death
- Organ donation: yes or no, and which organs

**Why you need both a living will AND a healthcare proxy:** The living will covers the scenarios you anticipated. The healthcare proxy covers everything else. Your living will might say "no ventilator if I'm in a persistent vegetative state." But what about a temporary coma with a 40% chance of recovery? Your healthcare proxy makes that call. The two documents work together.

**Cost:** $0. Free forms available in every state. Five Wishes (fivewishes.org) is a widely recognized document accepted in most states that walks you through the decisions in plain English.

**Without it:** Your family guesses. And then argues about what you would have wanted. Under the worst possible emotional conditions. While you're dying.

### HIPAA Authorization

**What it does:** Gives named individuals the legal right to access your medical information. Without this, HIPAA privacy laws can prevent your family from getting information about your condition, talking to your doctors, or accessing your medical records — even in an emergency.

**Who needs it:** Everyone. This is a one-page form that takes five minutes to complete.

**Who to name:** Your healthcare proxy, your spouse/partner, your adult children, and anyone else who might need to communicate with your medical team.

**Cost:** $0. Your doctor's office has the form. You can also download templates online.

**Without it:** Your loved ones show up at the hospital and the staff says "we can't tell you anything." It happens constantly.

### The Complete Package: What It Costs

Here's the reality of what a complete death plan costs:

| Component | Attorney | DIY | Without It |

| Revocable Living Trust | $1,500-$5,000 | $200-$500 | $5,000-$25,000 probate |
| Durable Power of Attorney | $150-$500 | $0-$50 | $2,000-$10,000 conservatorship |
| Healthcare Power of Attorney | $0-$150 | $0 | Family conflict + court costs |
| Advance Directive / Living Will | $0-$100 | $0 | Family guesses while you die |
| HIPAA Authorization | $0 | $0 | Locked out of medical info |
| **Complete package** | **$1,500-$3,000** | **$200-$800** | **$10,000-$50,000+** |

The DIY column is not a compromise. For most people with straightforward situations — single, married without kids, or married with kids and no blended family — a DIY estate plan is perfectly adequate. The online tools have gotten genuinely good. Use an attorney if you have a complex situation: significant assets, business ownership, blended family, property in multiple states, or a beneficiary with special needs.

The "Without It" column is the cost of avoidance. That's what your family pays because you didn't spend a Saturday afternoon filling out forms.

## Beneficiary Designations: The Documents That Override Your Will

This is the section where most people's understanding of estate planning breaks down — and it's the section that causes the most damage when you get it wrong.

**Beneficiary designations override your will.** Read that again. It doesn't matter what your will says. If your 401(k) beneficiary form names your ex-spouse from 2008, your ex-spouse gets your 401(k). Not your current spouse. Not your children. Your ex. The will is irrelevant. The beneficiary form wins.

This isn't a technicality. This is established law. In *Hillman v. Maretta* (2013), the Supreme Court upheld that a life insurance policy paid to a deceased federal employee's ex-wife — even though the divorce decree said the ex-wife would not receive the benefit and the deceased had remarried. The beneficiary designation hadn't been updated. The ex-wife got $124,000. The widow got nothing.

### Which Accounts Have Beneficiary Designations

These accounts pass directly to named beneficiaries, bypassing your will and probate entirely:

- **401(k), 403(b), and other employer retirement plans** — Federal law (ERISA) requires your spouse to be the primary beneficiary unless they sign a waiver. Even if you name someone else. Even if you're separated. Until the divorce is final and the beneficiary is changed, your spouse has a legal claim.
- **Traditional and Roth IRAs** — No spousal requirement under federal law (unlike 401(k)s), but state law may create one. Name who you want, but verify.
- **Life insurance policies** — Paid directly to the named beneficiary. No probate, no will, no waiting. Usually within 30-60 days.
- **Payable-on-death (POD) bank accounts** — You add a POD designation to any bank account. When you die, the beneficiary walks into the bank with a death certificate and walks out with the money.
- **Transfer-on-death (TOD) brokerage accounts** — Same concept for investment accounts.
- **TOD real estate deeds** — Available in about 30 states. You record a TOD deed, and your property transfers automatically at death without probate.
- **Pension and annuity plans**
- **Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)** — If your spouse is the beneficiary, the HSA becomes theirs. If anyone else is the beneficiary, the entire balance is taxable income to them in the year of your death.

### Common Beneficiary Disasters

**The ex-spouse problem:** You got divorced in 2015. You updated your will. You forgot to update your 401(k) beneficiary form — the one you filled out when you started the job in 2011 and named your then-spouse. You die. Your ex gets the 401(k). Your current partner, your children, your will — none of it matters. This happens thousands of times a year.

**The dead beneficiary problem:** You named your mother as your life insurance beneficiary 20 years ago. Your mother died 5 years ago. You never updated the form. Now the benefit either goes to a contingent beneficiary (if you named one) or into your estate (where it goes through probate and may not end up where you wanted).

**The minor child problem:** You name your 8-year-old as a life insurance beneficiary. You die. A minor child cannot receive life insurance proceeds directly. The court appoints a financial guardian (who may or may not be the person you would have chosen) and the money sits in a court-supervised account with restrictions until the child turns 18 — at which point they get a lump sum. An 18-year-old. With a lump sum. This is almost never what parents want. Name a trust as the beneficiary instead, or use a custodial designation (UTMA/UGMA).

**The per stirpes vs. per capita confusion:** These are the two ways to distribute assets when a beneficiary dies before you.

- **Per stirpes** ("by the roots"): If a beneficiary dies before you, their share goes to their children (your grandchildren). You name your three kids. One dies. That child's share goes to their kids — your grandchildren. The inheritance follows the family tree.
- **Per capita** ("by the head"): If a beneficiary dies before you, their share is split among the surviving beneficiaries. You name your three kids. One dies. The other two split everything equally. Your deceased child's children get nothing.

Most people want per stirpes and don't know to specify it. Check every beneficiary designation form you have.

## Probate: What It Actually Is and How to Avoid It

Probate sounds scary because the estate planning industry *wants* it to sound scary. It's how they sell trusts. But you should understand what it actually is, what it actually costs, and then decide for yourself whether it's worth avoiding.

**Probate is the legal process of:**
1. Validating that a will is legitimate
2. Appointing an executor to manage the estate
3. Notifying creditors so they can file claims against the estate
4. Paying the deceased's debts and taxes
5. Distributing remaining assets to beneficiaries
6. Court oversight of the entire process

**That's it.** It's not inherently terrible. In many states, it's a relatively streamlined process — especially for smaller estates. The problem is when it's *not* streamlined.

### What Probate Actually Costs

**Time:** The national average is about 16 months from filing to final distribution. Simple estates in efficient states can clear in 6-9 months. Complex or contested estates can take 3-5 years.

**Money:** Attorney fees typically run 2-5% of the estate's value. Court filing fees range from $50 to $1,200 depending on the state and estate size. Executor fees (if the executor charges, which they're entitled to) are another 1-3%.

On a $500,000 estate, that's roughly:
- Attorney fees: $10,000-$25,000
- Court costs: $200-$1,200
- Executor fees: $5,000-$15,000
- Total: $15,200-$41,200

**Privacy:** Probate is a public court proceeding. The will, the asset inventory, the debts, the beneficiaries — all of it becomes public record. Anyone can look it up. This is why celebrities use trusts — it's not about the money; it's about keeping their financial lives out of the tabloids.

### Which Assets Go Through Probate

**Goes through probate:**
- Assets owned solely in the deceased's name with no beneficiary designation
- Real estate titled in the deceased's name alone
- Personal property (cars, jewelry, furniture)
- Bank accounts without POD designations
- Investment accounts without TOD designations

**Skips probate:**
- Assets with named beneficiaries (retirement accounts, life insurance, POD/TOD accounts)
- Jointly owned property with right of survivorship
- Assets held in a trust
- Community property with right of survivorship (in community property states)
- Assets below the state's small estate threshold (you can use a simplified affidavit process instead)

### How to Avoid Probate (If You Want To)

You don't have to avoid probate. For small estates in efficient states, probate is a minor inconvenience, not a catastrophe. But if you want to avoid it:

**1. Beneficiary designations on everything possible.** POD on bank accounts. TOD on brokerage accounts. Named beneficiaries on retirement accounts and insurance. This is free and takes an afternoon.

**2. Joint ownership with right of survivorship.** For married couples, this is often the simplest approach for real estate and bank accounts. When one spouse dies, the other automatically owns the full asset. No probate. Caution: adding a non-spouse as a joint owner creates gift tax implications and gives them immediate access to the account.

**3. Transfer-on-death deeds for real estate.** Available in about 30 states (check yours). You file a TOD deed with the county recorder. When you die, the property transfers automatically to the named beneficiary. You keep full control while alive. No probate for the property.

**4. A revocable living trust.** The most comprehensive probate avoidance tool. Assets in the trust skip probate entirely. But remember — you have to actually fund the trust by re-titling assets into it. An unfunded trust doesn't avoid anything.

**5. Small estate procedures.** Most states have a simplified process for estates below a certain value ($25,000-$150,000+ depending on the state). Your heirs file an affidavit instead of going through full probate. Check your state's threshold.

**State variation matters.** Probate in California is notoriously expensive (attorney and executor fees are set by statute as a percentage of the estate). Probate in Texas is relatively cheap and efficient. Probate in Wisconsin uses a simplified process for most estates. Your state's probate process should inform your planning decisions. Look up "[your state] probate process" before deciding whether a trust is worth the cost.

## Life Insurance in the Death Planning Context

This isn't the insurance module. The [Insurance deep dive](/deep-dives/insurance/) covers the full landscape — health, disability, auto, home, umbrella, and life. Read that for the comprehensive framework on what insurance you need, what you're overpaying for, and the term-vs-whole-life debate.

This section covers life insurance specifically as a death planning tool: how much you need, when you don't need it, and how the claims process actually works.

### How Much Coverage Do You Need?

The insurance industry loves rules of thumb — "10 times your salary" is the most common. It's also nearly useless. Your actual need depends on what your death would cost the people who depend on you financially.

Use the DIME method:

- **D — Debt:** Total all debts that need to be paid off at your death: mortgage, car loans, student loans, credit cards, personal loans. This is the lump sum needed to clear the slate.
- **I — Income:** How many years of income replacement does your family need? If you earn $80,000 and want to replace income for 15 years, that's $1.2 million (simplified — inflation and investment returns complicate this, but it's a starting point).
- **M — Mortgage:** If your mortgage isn't included in the debt total above, add it here. Do you want to pay off the house entirely so your family has no housing payment? Or is the income replacement amount enough to cover the mortgage payment over time?
- **E — Education:** If you have kids, estimate college costs. Current average: $25,000-$55,000 per year depending on in-state vs. private. Multiply by 4. Per kid.

**Example:** A 35-year-old with $250,000 mortgage, $30,000 in other debt, two kids, and a $90,000 salary:
- Debt: $280,000
- Income replacement (15 years): $1,350,000
- Education (2 kids × $120,000): $240,000
- **Total need: ~$1,870,000** → Round to $2 million in coverage

### What Term Life Insurance Costs

Term life insurance is cheap. Shockingly cheap relative to the coverage it provides. Here's what a 20-year, $500,000 term policy costs for a healthy non-smoker:

| Age at Purchase | Monthly Premium | Annual Premium | Total Over 20 Years |
|---|---|---|---|
| 25 | $18-$25 | $216-$300 | $4,320-$6,000 |
| 30 | $20-$28 | $240-$336 | $4,800-$6,720 |
| 35 | $25-$35 | $300-$420 | $6,000-$8,400 |
| 40 | $35-$50 | $420-$600 | $8,400-$12,000 |
| 45 | $55-$80 | $660-$960 | $13,200-$19,200 |
| 50 | $90-$130 | $1,080-$1,560 | $21,600-$31,200 |

A 35-year-old can get $500,000 of coverage for the cost of a streaming subscription. Two streaming subscriptions. And yet millions of people with dependents have no life insurance at all.

### When You DON'T Need Life Insurance

Life insurance is an income-replacement tool. If nobody depends on your income, you probably don't need it:

- **Single with no dependents.** If nobody relies on your income to live, you don't need life insurance. Maybe a small policy ($10,000-$25,000) to cover funeral costs if you don't want that burden on your family. But a full life insurance policy? No.
- **Retired with sufficient assets.** If your surviving spouse has enough retirement savings, Social Security, and pensions to live comfortably, life insurance is a luxury you can drop.
- **Kids are financially independent.** Once your kids are grown and self-supporting, the income replacement need disappears.
- **Enough savings to self-insure.** If your net worth is high enough that your family is financially secure regardless, life insurance is redundant.

Don't let an insurance salesperson tell you that you need whole life insurance "as an investment" or "for the tax benefits." You don't. The [Insurance module](/deep-dives/insurance/) explains why in detail.

### How Life Insurance Claims Work

This matters because you might be the one filing a claim, not the one who died.

**Step 1:** Get the death certificate. You'll need multiple certified copies — at least 10-15. The funeral home typically handles this.

**Step 2:** Find the policy. Check the deceased's files, email, bank statements for premium payments, employer benefits portal, and with any financial advisor. If you can't find the policy, contact the state's unclaimed life insurance registry or use the NAIC Life Insurance Policy Locator (free).

**Step 3:** Call the insurance company. They'll send you a claim form (or you'll complete it online). You'll need the policy number, certified death certificate, and claimant information.

**Step 4:** Submit the claim. Most claims are paid within 30-60 days. Life insurance proceeds are generally income tax-free to the beneficiary.

**Common claim problems:**
- **Contestability period:** If the insured died within the first two years of the policy, the insurer can investigate and potentially deny the claim if there were material misrepresentations on the application (undisclosed health conditions, smoking status, etc.).
- **Suicide clause:** Most policies exclude suicide within the first two years. After that, suicide is covered.
- **Lapsed policy:** If premiums weren't paid, the policy may have lapsed. Check for any grace period.
- **Missing beneficiary:** If the beneficiary can't be found, proceeds may go to the estate or escheat to the state.
- **Multiple claimants:** If there's a dispute about who the beneficiary is (e.g., ex-spouse vs. current spouse), the insurer may file an interpleader action — essentially asking the court to decide. This delays payment by months or years.

## The Funeral Industry: What Death Actually Costs

The funeral industry is a $20 billion-a-year business in the United States. It relies on one core dynamic: people making expensive decisions during the worst emotional moments of their lives, with no time to comparison shop and massive social pressure to "do the right thing" by the dead.

The industry has consolidated significantly — three corporations now control a large share of funeral homes across the country — and markup margins on caskets can exceed 300-500%. You are the customer at your most vulnerable, and the industry is structured to capitalize on that vulnerability.

This isn't to say funeral directors are bad people. Many are compassionate professionals providing a necessary service. But you need to understand the economics of death *before* you're sitting in an arrangement room, grieving, while someone slides a price list across the table.

| Obituary | $200-$1,000+ | Newspaper obituaries charge by the line/word |

That's $10,000-$20,000 for a "standard" funeral and burial. And families are expected to make these decisions within 24-72 hours of a death, often while in shock.

### The FTC Funeral Rule: Your Rights

The Federal Trade Commission's Funeral Rule is your most important protection. It requires:

1. **Itemized price list.** Funeral homes must give you a written, itemized General Price List (GPL) before showing you any merchandise or discussing arrangements. If they don't offer it, ask for it. If they refuse, walk out and report them to the FTC.

2. **You can decline services.** You are not required to buy embalming (in most cases), a casket for cremation, a viewing, or any package you don't want. The funeral home must offer services *a la carte*.

3. **You can bring your own casket.** This is the big one. You can buy a casket from Costco ($950-$1,500), Amazon, Walmart, or any third-party retailer and have it delivered to the funeral home. The funeral home **cannot charge a handling fee** for using a casket you purchased elsewhere. A casket that costs $2,500-$10,000 at the funeral home costs $950-$2,500 online. Same product, fraction of the price.

4. **You can use an alternative container.** For cremation, you do not need a casket. A simple wooden or cardboard container is sufficient and costs $50-$200.

5. **You can ask for prices by phone.** The Funeral Rule requires funeral homes to provide price information over the phone. You don't have to walk in. Call three funeral homes, get their GPLs, and compare.

### Cost Reduction: Alternatives That Work

**Direct cremation: $1,000-$3,000.** No viewing, no embalming, no ceremony at the funeral home. The body goes directly from the place of death to the crematory. You receive the cremated remains. You can hold a memorial service wherever you want — at home, at a park, at a community center — on your own timeline, without the funeral home's per-hour facility charges. This is what about 60% of Americans now choose, up from about 25% in 2000.

**Green/natural burial: $2,000-$5,000.** No embalming, no vault, biodegradable casket or shroud, in a certified green cemetery. The body returns to the earth. Costs are lower because you're eliminating the most expensive components (embalming, casket, vault). Green cemeteries are growing rapidly — the Green Burial Council certifies locations.

**Body donation to science: $0 (usually).** Medical schools and research institutions accept whole-body donations. They typically cover transportation costs. After the research period (usually 1-3 years), cremated remains are returned to the family. You do need to register in advance. Not all donations are accepted (certain conditions or causes of death may disqualify).

**Home funerals: $200-$1,000.** Legal in most states. The family washes and prepares the body, holds the vigil at home, and arranges transportation to the cemetery or crematory. It requires research and preparation but is legal and was the norm for most of human history.

### Pre-Planning vs. Pre-Paying

**Pre-planning: Yes.** Writing down your wishes — cremation vs. burial, type of service, who speaks, what music plays — takes an enormous burden off your family. They don't have to guess. They don't have to argue. They follow your plan. Do this as part of your death file (Section 10).

**Pre-paying: Extreme caution.** Pre-paid funeral plans lock in prices, but they come with significant risks:
- The funeral home might go out of business before you die
- Moving to a different state makes the plan complicated or useless
- Some plans are non-refundable or charge heavy cancellation penalties
- The money is often held in a trust or insurance product with unfavorable terms
- If you've overpaid relative to what your family would actually choose, you've wasted money

If you want to set aside money for funeral costs, a dedicated POD savings account ($10,000-$15,000) gives your family the same financial security without locking you into a specific funeral home or plan. They can use the money however they choose.

## Your Digital Estate

You have a digital life. When you die, it doesn't disappear — it just becomes inaccessible to everyone who needs to manage it.

The average person has over 100 online accounts. Email, social media, banking, subscriptions, cloud storage, shopping accounts, utility logins, streaming services, government portals, medical portals, work tools. Each one is locked behind a password. Many are locked behind two-factor authentication tied to a phone your family may not be able to unlock.

Then there's the money: cryptocurrency wallets where the keys die with you (an estimated $140 billion in Bitcoin is permanently inaccessible due to lost keys), PayPal balances, Venmo balances, online bank accounts, investment platforms, digital storefronts. And the recurring charges: subscriptions that keep billing a dead person's credit card for months because nobody knows the login.

This is a solvable problem. It requires one tool and one document.

### The One Tool: A Password Manager

If you do nothing else in this section, start using a password manager (Bitwarden is free and open-source; 1Password is excellent for families at $5/month). Store every login, every password, every account in one place, protected by one master password.

Then give your master password to one trusted person. Or put it in your death file (Section 10). Or use the password manager's emergency access feature — most good ones have it.

That's it. One master password unlocks your entire digital life. Without it, your survivors will spend weeks contacting companies, providing death certificates, and navigating bureaucratic processes to close accounts one by one. With it, they can handle everything in an afternoon.

### Social Media Legacy Settings

The major platforms have built-in tools for handling accounts after death. Set these up now:

**Facebook/Meta:** Settings → Memorialization Settings. You can designate a "Legacy Contact" who can manage your memorialized profile (pin posts, respond to friend requests, update profile photo). You can also choose to have your account permanently deleted after death.

**Google (Gmail, YouTube, Drive, Photos):** Google Inactive Account Manager. Set a timeout period (3-18 months of inactivity), and Google will either notify your designated contacts and share specified data, or delete the account. You choose.

**Apple:** Settings → [Your Name] → Legacy Contact. Your legacy contacts can access your iCloud data, photos, messages, and more after your death using a special access key and your death certificate.

**Instagram:** Can be memorialized (by a verified family member contacting Instagram) or removed. No pre-death settings available.

**Twitter/X:** Family members can request account deactivation by contacting support with a death certificate.

## The Survivor Checklist: What to Do When Someone Dies

When someone dies, you will not be in a state to think clearly. You'll be in shock, grief, exhaustion, or all three. This checklist exists so you don't have to think. You just follow it.

Print this. Put it in your death file. Tell your family where it is. When the moment comes, this is what you open.

### First 72 Hours

**Immediately:**
- [ ] Call 911 if the death is unexpected or occurs at home. They will send EMTs and, if necessary, the coroner.
- [ ] If the death occurs in a hospital or hospice, the staff will handle initial procedures and paperwork.
- [ ] Contact immediate family members. Delegate — assign one person to be the caller so you're not making 20 phone calls while in shock.
- [ ] Secure the deceased's home if they lived alone. Lock up. Check for pets that need care.

**Within 24 hours:**
- [ ] Choose a funeral home. If the deceased pre-planned, contact that funeral home. If not, call at least two and ask for their General Price List before making a decision.
- [ ] Locate the will, trust documents, and any pre-planned funeral arrangements. Check the deceased's files, safe deposit box, attorney, or death file.
- [ ] Order death certificates — at least 10-15 certified copies. The funeral home handles this. Each copy costs $10-$25 depending on the state.

**Within 72 hours:**
- [ ] Notify the deceased's employer and ask about any death benefits, final paycheck, life insurance through work, or pension survivor benefits.
- [ ] Notify life insurance companies. You'll need the policy number and a death certificate. Claims are usually filed by phone or online.
- [ ] If the deceased was the primary account holder for shared bills (mortgage, utilities, car payment), make sure payments continue. Late payments and shutoffs don't wait for grief.

### First Week

- [ ] File for Social Security survivor benefits if applicable: call 1-800-772-1213 or visit your local SSA office. A surviving spouse can receive survivor benefits as early as age 60 (50 if disabled). Dependent children under 18 also qualify.
- [ ] Contact the deceased's bank(s). You'll need a death certificate and proof of your authority (as executor, joint account holder, or POD beneficiary). Lock down accounts to prevent unauthorized access.
- [ ] Contact the deceased's financial advisor, accountant, or attorney if they had one.
- [ ] Start a log of every expense, every call, every decision. You'll need this for the estate and potentially for tax purposes.
- [ ] Forward the deceased's mail to the executor or a family member who can monitor it. Bills, statements, and notices will continue arriving.

### First Month

- [ ] File life insurance claims. You'll need: certified death certificate, policy information, claim form, and claimant identification.
- [ ] Begin the probate process if necessary — file the will with the probate court, apply for letters testamentary (which give the executor legal authority to act).
- [ ] Notify credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) to flag the deceased's credit file. This helps prevent identity theft of the dead, which is a real and growing problem.
- [ ] Cancel or transfer accounts: credit cards, subscriptions, memberships, utilities, phone, internet, streaming services. Some will require a death certificate; some just need a phone call.
- [ ] File for any veteran's benefits if the deceased was a veteran: burial benefits ($2,000-$3,000), headstone/marker (free from the VA), burial in a national cemetery (free).
- [ ] Check for any unclaimed assets: old bank accounts, pension benefits, life insurance. Use your state's unclaimed property website and the NAIC Policy Locator.

### First Six Months

- [ ] File the deceased's final federal and state tax returns (due April 15 of the year following death). The executor is responsible for this.
- [ ] File an estate tax return if the estate exceeds the federal exemption ($13.61 million per individual in 2024). Most estates don't owe federal estate tax, but some states have lower thresholds.
- [ ] Distribute assets according to the will or trust (after debts and taxes are settled).
- [ ] Close the estate with the probate court once all assets are distributed and all claims are settled.
- [ ] Monitor the deceased's credit reports for 12+ months. Identity thieves target the recently deceased — they apply for credit cards, loans, and even tax refunds using the dead person's Social Security number.

### What It Costs the Survivor

Your own out-of-pocket costs as a survivor, before any inheritance:

| Expense | Typical Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Funeral and burial | $10,000-$20,000 | Or $1,000-$3,000 for direct cremation |
| Death certificates (10-15 copies) | $100-$375 | $10-$25 each, varies by state |
| Probate attorney fees | $2,000-$15,000+ | If probate is required |
| Court filing fees | $50-$1,200 | Varies by state and estate size |
| Final medical bills | $1,000-$10,000+ | Often arriving for months after death |
| Travel for out-of-town family | $500-$3,000 | Flights, hotels, missed work |
| Time off work (unpaid) | $0-$5,000+ | Most employers offer 3-5 days bereavement |
| **Total survivor costs** | **$5,000-$25,000+** | Before any estate debts |

These costs hit immediately — often before any life insurance is paid, any accounts are unfrozen, or any inheritance is distributed. The survivor fronts the money. This is why your emergency fund (Module 02 — Financial Architecture) matters even when the emergency is someone else's death.

## When Someone Dies Without a Plan

Intestacy is the legal term for dying without a valid will. Every state has intestacy laws — default rules that dictate who gets what. These laws are based on bloodlines and legal marriage. They do not account for relationships, intentions, promises, or fairness.

Here's how intestacy generally works:

**Married, no kids:** Surviving spouse gets everything (in most states). Some states give a portion to the deceased's parents.

**Married with kids (all children are of this marriage):** Surviving spouse gets everything, or spouse gets a large share and children split the rest (varies by state).

**Married with kids from a prior relationship:** This is where it gets ugly. In many states, the surviving spouse gets only a portion (often one-third to one-half), and the deceased's children from the prior relationship get the rest. The surviving spouse may not be able to keep the house.

**Unmarried with kids:** Children inherit everything. The partner gets nothing.

**Unmarried, no kids:** Parents inherit. If parents are deceased, siblings. Then nieces and nephews. Then more distant relatives. If no relatives can be found, the state takes everything (escheat).

### Four Nightmare Scenarios

These are composites based on real outcomes. The dollar amounts are realistic for these situations.

**Scenario 1: The Unmarried Couple**

Marcus and David have been together for 14 years. They own a house together — but only Marcus is on the deed because he had the better credit score when they bought it. Marcus dies without a will.

Under intestacy, Marcus's assets — including the house — go to his parents. His parents, who never fully accepted David, get the house. David, who paid half the mortgage for 12 years, has no legal claim. He gets 30 days' notice to vacate.

**Cost of no plan:** David loses his home (value: $380,000) and 12 years of mortgage payments (~$120,000). A will naming David would have cost $200-$500.

**Scenario 2: The Forgotten Beneficiary**

Rachel divorced James in 2017. The divorce decree specified that James would remove Rachel from all financial accounts. James updated his will to leave everything to his new partner, Michelle. He forgot to update his 401(k) beneficiary form, which still named Rachel.

James dies in 2022. His 401(k) balance: $340,000. Rachel gets all of it. Michelle gets nothing from the 401(k) — despite five years together, despite the will, despite the divorce decree. The beneficiary designation supersedes everything.

**Cost of no plan:** Michelle loses $340,000. A 10-minute beneficiary update would have prevented this entirely.

**Scenario 3: The Single Parent**

Keisha is a single mother of two children, ages 6 and 9. She has no will and no named guardian for her kids. Keisha dies in a car accident.

A court must now decide who raises her children. Her mother petitions. Her ex (the children's father, who hasn't been involved in years) also petitions — he has legal rights as the biological parent. The court battle takes 8 months. During that time, the children are in temporary foster care. The father wins because biology trumps everything else in most courts, absent documented abuse or neglect.

**Cost of no plan:** Eight months of instability for two children, $15,000-$30,000 in legal fees for the family, and the children end up with a parent who wasn't raising them. A will naming a guardian — with a letter explaining why — would have cost $200-$500 and given the court clear guidance.

**Scenario 4: The Blended Family**

Tom and Linda each have two children from prior marriages. They've been married for 10 years. Tom dies without a will or trust. Their home is in Tom's name only (he owned it before the marriage).

Under their state's intestacy law, Linda gets one-third of Tom's estate. Tom's two biological children split the other two-thirds. Linda cannot keep the house — Tom's children (who are now adults and want their inheritance) force a sale. Linda's children — Tom's stepchildren whom he raised for a decade — inherit nothing, because stepchildren have no legal standing under intestacy law.

**Cost of no plan:** Linda loses her home. Her children lose their inheritance. The family fractures over money. A trust or will with clear instructions would have prevented all of it.

Every one of these scenarios is a direct result of someone deciding they'd "get to it later." Later never came.

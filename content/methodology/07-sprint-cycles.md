# Module 07: Sprint Cycles for Life

### How to Execute in Structured Time Blocks That Actually Work

---

## Why 4-Week Cycles

The 4-week sprint is the operating rhythm of "My Contract." Not 1 week (too short to see meaningful results). Not 90 days (too long to maintain urgency and too slow to course-correct). Four weeks is the sweet spot, and here's why:

**Billing alignment.** Most bills, subscriptions, and pay cycles are monthly. A 4-week sprint aligns with your financial reality, making budgeting and tracking natural.

**Habit formation research.** The popular "21 days to form a habit" is a myth (it comes from a misreading of a 1960s plastic surgery study). Actual research (Phillippa Lally, University College London, 2009) shows habit formation takes **18 to 254 days**, with a median of **66 days.** A 4-week sprint covers the early critical period and, across two sprints, approaches the median.

**Motivation curves.** Motivation follows a predictable arc: high at the beginning (novelty), dipping in weeks 2-3 (reality), and recovering in week 4 (finish-line effect). Four weeks allows you to experience and learn from the full cycle. Shorter periods mask the crucial "week 2-3 dip" where most people quit.

**Review cadence.** Monthly reviews are manageable. Weekly reviews within the month keep you on track. More frequent reviews become noise; less frequent reviews allow too much drift.

## Sprint Planning (Day 1)

Sprint planning takes 30 minutes. Do it on the first day of your sprint -- same day each month. This is not optional. It is contract infrastructure.

### Step 1: Review the Previous Sprint (10 minutes)

If this is your first sprint, skip this. Otherwise:

```
PREVIOUS SPRINT REVIEW:

What I committed to:
1. _________________________ Result: [ ] Met [ ] Partially [ ] Missed
2. _________________________ Result: [ ] Met [ ] Partially [ ] Missed
3. _________________________ Result: [ ] Met [ ] Partially [ ] Missed

Overall Compliance Rate: ____%

What worked well this sprint:
_________________________________________________

What didn't work and why:
_________________________________________________

Key insight from this sprint:
_________________________________________________
```

Be honest. "Partially met" is not "met." If the term was "exercise 4 days per week" and you exercised 2 days per week, that's "missed," not "partially met." Partial credit feels kind, but it corrupts your data.

### Step 2: Set Sprint Commitments (15 minutes)

Choose 3-5 specific, measurable targets for this sprint. These should flow directly from your contract terms.

**The #1 mistake: overcommitment.** Your ambition will always exceed your capacity in the planning phase. You're planning when you feel good, motivated, and clear-headed. You'll be executing when you're tired, stressed, and managing a hundred things you didn't anticipate.

**The scope rule:** Take what you think you can do. Cut it by 30%. That's your sprint. If you crush it, add more next sprint. If you barely make it, you scoped correctly. If you fail, you still overscoped.

```
SPRINT __ COMMITMENTS:

1. _________________________
   Metric: _________________
   Contract term it supports: _________

2. _________________________
   Metric: _________________
   Contract term it supports: _________

3. _________________________
   Metric: _________________
   Contract term it supports: _________

4. _________________________  (optional)
   Metric: _________________

5. _________________________  (optional)
   Metric: _________________
```

### Step 3: Define "Done" (5 minutes)

For each commitment, define exactly what "done" means. This prevents the fuzzy "I kind of did it" at sprint review.

Examples:
- "Exercise 4x/week" -- DONE means: 16 or more exercise sessions logged in 28 days, each at least 20 minutes
- "Save $200/month" -- DONE means: $200 transferred to savings account, confirmed with bank balance
- "Read daily" -- DONE means: read for at least 15 minutes, 24 or more days out of 28
- "No fast food on weekdays" -- DONE means: zero fast food purchases Monday-Friday for all 4 weeks (20 weekdays, zero violations)

## The Weekly Check-In

Every week, same day, same time. 15 minutes. This is the pulse check that keeps you on track between sprint planning and sprint review.

```
WEEKLY CHECK-IN -- Week __ of Sprint __

Date: ____________

CONTRACT COMPLIANCE:
  Term 1: _____________ [ ] On track  [ ] Off track
  Term 2: _____________ [ ] On track  [ ] Off track
  Term 3: _____________ [ ] On track  [ ] Off track

SPRINT COMMITMENT PROGRESS:
  Commitment 1: [ ] On track  [ ] Behind  [ ] At risk
  Commitment 2: [ ] On track  [ ] Behind  [ ] At risk
  Commitment 3: [ ] On track  [ ] Behind  [ ] At risk

FINANCIAL SNAPSHOT:
  Spent this week: $________
  Budget remaining: $________
  On track? [ ] Yes [ ] No

PHYSICAL SNAPSHOT:
  Exercise days this week: ____
  Sleep avg: ____ hrs
  Energy avg: ____/10

ONE THING TO ADJUST THIS WEEK:
  _________________________________________________

ANYTHING I'M AVOIDING:
  _________________________________________________
```

The weekly check-in should take 15 minutes or less. If it takes longer, you're either overthinking it or you have too many terms/commitments.

## The Sprint Retrospective

At the end of each sprint, before planning the next one, do a retrospective. This is borrowed from Agile software development, but stripped of the corporate theater.

The retrospective asks three questions:

### 1. What Went Well?

Not just "I hit my targets." Why did they work? What systems, environmental factors, or mindset shifts made compliance possible?

Examples:
- "Meal prepping on Sundays eliminated my weekday fast food spending -- the friction engineering actually worked"
- "The 24-hour purchase rule stopped 3 impulse buys totaling $180"
- "Exercising first thing in the morning meant it happened before life got in the way"

### 2. What Didn't Go Well?

Specific failures with specific causes. Not "I need to try harder." That's not a cause.

Examples:
- "I missed exercise on Wednesdays consistently -- my Wednesday schedule has back-to-back meetings from 8-5 with no break"
- "I overspent in week 3 because of a birthday dinner I didn't budget for"
- "I broke my phone screen time limit 12 out of 28 days -- the limit is too easy to override with one tap"

### 3. What Will I Change?

One concrete adjustment for each failure. Not more effort. A system change.

Examples:
- "I'll move my Wednesday workout to a 15-minute bodyweight routine at home before my 8 AM meeting"
- "I'll add a $100/month 'social events' line item to my budget so planned events don't blow the whole budget"
- "I'll delete Instagram entirely instead of relying on screen time limits I can override"

## Sprint Themes

An alternative approach to balanced sprints is themed sprints -- focusing each sprint heavily on one domain:

### Financial Sprint
- Primary focus: Financial audit completion, budget creation, subscription cancellation, debt payoff planning
- Other domains on maintenance mode (existing terms continue, but no new commitments added)
- Useful when: Financial instability is the most urgent problem

### Health Sprint
- Primary focus: Exercise habit installation, sleep schedule fix, meal prep system, overdue medical appointments
- Other domains on maintenance
- Useful when: Physical health is undermining everything else

### Career Sprint
- Primary focus: Skill development, resume update, networking, negotiation preparation, side project launch
- Useful when: Income is the bottleneck

### Relationship Sprint
- Primary focus: Hard conversations, boundary setting, reconnecting with people, couples financial review
- Useful when: Isolation or relationship conflict is the biggest drain

### Pros of Themed Sprints:
- Concentrated energy produces faster, more visible results in one area
- Reduces decision fatigue (fewer competing priorities)
- Creates momentum that can carry into the next sprint

### Cons of Themed Sprints:
- Other areas may slide during the focused sprint
- Can feel unbalanced
- Some people need variety to stay engaged

**Recommendation:** Start with themed sprints for the first 2-3 cycles (address the most urgent domain first), then shift to balanced sprints once the foundation is stable.

## What to Do When a Sprint Fails

A failed sprint is not a failed contract. It's diagnostic information. Here's the decision tree:

### Was the compliance rate above 60%?

**Yes (60-74%):** You overcommitted slightly. Reduce the number of commitments or the intensity of terms by one notch. Keep the same structure. Run the next sprint.

**No (below 60%):** Something systemic is wrong. Ask:

### Was the failure across all terms or concentrated?

**Concentrated in one area:** That specific term is either too ambitious, poorly structured, or masking a deeper issue. Revise or replace that term. Keep the rest.

**Across all terms:** Your entire contract is scoped beyond your current capacity. This is not a motivation problem -- it's a design problem. Strip down to 2 terms. Just 2. Run a recovery sprint. Rebuild from there.

### Are you dealing with a life disruption?

**Yes (job loss, health issue, relationship crisis, family emergency):** Downgrade to a crisis contract (see V0.5 in the expanded modules). Stabilize first. Sprints resume when the crisis passes.

**No:** Then the sprint failure is a calibration issue. You need to understand whether:
- The terms were unrealistic for your current life (reduce scope)
- You have an unaddressed obstacle (addiction, mental health, toxic relationship) that needs direct attention before the contract can succeed
- You're not actually committed to the contract (this is valid -- not everyone is ready. But be honest about it rather than pretending to try)

## Tracking Velocity Over Time

Track your compliance rate across sprints. The trend is the signal:

```
Sprint 1: ██████░░░░  62%  -- Baseline. Expected to be rough.
Sprint 2: ████████░░  78%  -- Adjustment worked. Improvement.
Sprint 3: ███████░░░  72%  -- Slight dip. Normal variation.
Sprint 4: █████████░  85%  -- New groove. Terms feeling automatic.
Sprint 5: █████████░  88%  -- Contract upgrade candidate.
Sprint 6: ██████░░░░  63%  -- Life event. Downgrade triggered.
Sprint 7: ████████░░  75%  -- Recovery sprint. Rebuilding.
Sprint 8: █████████░  84%  -- Back on track.
```

**What to look for:**
- **Upward trend over 3+ sprints:** The system is working. Consider upgrading the contract
- **Flat at 75-85%:** You're in a steady state. This is sustainable. Consider whether you want to challenge yourself with a contract upgrade or maintain
- **Flat below 65%:** The contract is wrong for your current life. Redesign, don't just "try harder"
- **Single-sprint dip:** Normal. Life happens. Don't panic. Investigate, adjust, continue
- **Multi-sprint decline:** Something has changed -- externally or internally. Conduct a full contract review. You may need to downgrade or address a root cause

## A Complete Sprint Example

### Sprint 4 -- Maria (from Module 06)

**Sprint Planning:**
```
Previous Sprint 3 Review:
  1. Track all spending: MET (28/28 days tracked)
  2. Walk 3x/week at lunch: PARTIALLY MET (9/12 target walks -- missed 3 due to rain)
  3. Weekly money check-in with spouse: MET (4/4 weeks)
  Compliance: 78%

Sprint 4 Commitments:
  1. Continue daily spend tracking
     Metric: 28/28 days
  2. Walk 3x/week (rain plan: walk in mall or do YouTube workout at home)
     Metric: 12/12 sessions
  3. Weekly money check-in with spouse
     Metric: 4/4 weeks
  4. NEW: Pack lunch at least 3 workdays/week
     Metric: 12/16 workdays with packed lunch
```

**Week 2 Check-In:**
```
  Spending tracking: On track (14/14 days)
  Walking: On track (6/6 sessions so far, used rain backup once)
  Money check-in: On track (2/2 weeks)
  Packed lunches: Behind (4/8 -- only doing 2/week so far)
  Adjustment: Will prep 3 lunches on Sunday instead of trying to make them morning-of
```

**Sprint 4 Review:**
```
  1. Spend tracking: MET (28/28)
  2. Walking: MET (12/12 -- rain backup used 3 times)
  3. Money check-in: MET (4/4)
  4. Packed lunch: PARTIALLY MET (10/16 -- improved after week 2 adjustment but still short)
  Compliance: 83%

  Retrospective:
  - What worked: Rain backup plan for walking was essential. Sunday meal prep improved lunch packing.
  - What didn't: Lunch packing still missed on Mondays when Sunday prep didn't happen
  - Change: Will prep on Saturday OR Sunday (flexibility) and always make enough for Monday-Wednesday minimum

  Contract update: Ready for V2.0? Compliance trending up (62% > 78% > 78% > 83%). Yes -- add one term next sprint.
```

# Module 06: The Contract

### Writing, Signing, and Living by a Binding Agreement With Yourself

---

## Why the Contract Structure Works

This isn't just a metaphor. The contract structure leverages specific psychological mechanisms:

### Implementation Intentions

Research by Peter Gollwitzer shows that people who form "implementation intentions" -- specific if-then plans ("If it is 7 AM on a weekday, then I will put on my running shoes and walk for 20 minutes") -- are **2-3x more likely** to follow through than people who simply set goals ("I want to exercise more").

Contract terms ARE implementation intentions. They specify the what, when, where, and how. They remove ambiguity, and ambiguity is where good intentions go to die.

### Commitment Devices

A commitment device is a choice you make now that constrains your future behavior. Odysseus tied himself to the mast so he couldn't respond to the Sirens. He didn't trust his future willpower -- he designed a system that made the wrong choice impossible.

Your contract is a commitment device. By defining terms in advance -- when you're thinking clearly and rationally -- you create a framework that guides behavior when you're tired, emotional, or depleted. The decision was already made. You just execute.

### Cognitive Consistency

Humans have a deep need to act consistently with their stated commitments. This is why public commitments are more powerful than private ones, and why written commitments are more powerful than verbal ones. Once you've signed a document saying "I will save $200 per month," your brain experiences discomfort when you violate that statement. This is productive discomfort -- it creates a natural accountability pressure.

## Good Terms vs. Bad Terms

The difference between a contract that works and one that doesn't is almost always in how the terms are written.

### Bad Terms (Vague, Unmeasurable, Aspirational)

- "I will eat healthier"
- "I will exercise more"
- "I will save money"
- "I will be more productive"
- "I will spend less time on my phone"
- "I will drink less"

These are wishes, not terms. You cannot comply with "more" or "healthier" because there's no definition of compliance. There's no way to know if you succeeded or failed on any given day. This ambiguity lets you rationalize anything.

### Good Terms (Specific, Measurable, Binary)

- "I will eat home-cooked meals at least 5 days per week and track every meal in [app/notebook]"
- "I will walk for 25 minutes on Monday, Wednesday, Friday, and Saturday mornings before 8 AM"
- "I will auto-transfer $200 to savings on the 1st and 15th of each month"
- "I will complete my three highest-priority work tasks before checking social media"
- "I will set a 30-minute daily screen time limit on Instagram and TikTok using Screen Time"
- "I will not drink alcohol Sunday through Thursday. On Friday and Saturday, maximum 3 drinks"

**Each of these is binary.** At the end of the day, you can answer: did I comply? Yes or no. There's no wiggle room. No "kind of." No "I meant to." You did it or you didn't. That clarity is the point.

### The Specificity Test

For every contract term, ask:
1. **Can I answer yes/no to this today?** If not, it's too vague
2. **Would a stranger understand exactly what's required?** If not, it's too ambiguous
3. **Do I know exactly when this happens?** If not, add a time/trigger
4. **Do I know what "done" looks like?** If not, define the finish line
5. **Is this something I can actually do consistently starting tomorrow?** If not, scale it down

## The Version System

Your contract is a living document. It starts simple and grows as you prove you can sustain your commitments.

### Version 1.0 (Starter Contract)

**Scope:** 3-5 non-negotiable terms. These should be things you can do starting tomorrow that are slightly challenging but not overwhelming.

**Duration:** Run V1.0 for at least 8 weeks (2 sprints) before upgrading.

**Criteria for advancing to V2.0:**
- Compliance rate above 75% for two consecutive sprints
- All terms feel sustainable (not heroic effort, but routine)
- You have the capacity to add more without compromising existing terms

### Version 2.0 (Intermediate Contract)

**Scope:** Original terms plus 2-3 additional terms. These should address the next-priority items from your audit.

**Duration:** Run V2.0 for at least 8 weeks before considering V3.0.

### Version 3.0+ (Advanced Contract)

**Scope:** Comprehensive terms across all life domains. This is the fully operational contract, but it's built on a foundation of proven compliance, not good intentions.

### Version Downgrades

Life happens. Job loss. Health crisis. Breakup. New baby. When your capacity is reduced, you don't abandon the contract -- you downgrade it.

**How to downgrade:**
1. Acknowledge that your capacity has changed (not permanently, but right now)
2. Identify the 2-3 most critical terms -- the ones that maintain basic stability
3. Suspend other terms explicitly (write it down: "Terms 2.4, 2.5, and 2.6 are suspended until [condition]")
4. Operate on the reduced contract without guilt. You're not failing. You're adapting.
5. When capacity returns, upgrade back, one term at a time

Downgrading is not failure. It is the system working as designed. A system that can't handle adversity isn't a system -- it's a fantasy.

## The Breach Protocol (Expanded)

You will break your contract terms. This is guaranteed. The question is: what happens next?

Most people, when they slip up, do one of two things:
1. **Guilt spiral:** "I'm such a failure, I can't do anything right, why do I even try" > abandon the entire contract
2. **Denial:** "It doesn't count because [excuse]" > continue as if it didn't happen, learn nothing

Both are useless. The breach protocol replaces them with a diagnostic process:

### Step 1: Name the Breach
State specifically what happened. "I committed to not eating out on weekdays. I got Chipotle on Wednesday."

Not "I kind of slipped up." Be specific.

### Step 2: Identify the Trigger
What specifically preceded the breach? Not "I was stressed." More specific than that.

"I had a bad meeting at 2 PM, didn't eat lunch, was starving by 5 PM, and drove past Chipotle on the way home. I was hungry, tired, and emotionally depleted."

### Step 3: Identify the System Failure
The trigger is the surface. The system failure is the root cause. In the example above:

- "I didn't eat lunch" -- why? Because I didn't prep food the night before
- "I was emotionally depleted" -- my meeting schedule on Wednesday leaves no recovery time
- "I drove past Chipotle" -- my commute route puts temptation in my path

### Step 4: Design One Countermeasure
Not five countermeasures. One. The one most likely to prevent the exact scenario from recurring:

"I will always have a backup lunch option at my desk (protein bars, canned soup, nuts) so that skipping meal prep doesn't result in hunger-driven impulse eating."

### Step 5: Log and Move Forward
Write the breach and countermeasure in your weekly review. Then continue operating under your contract terms. The breach is a data point, not a verdict. You are an engineer reviewing a system failure, not a criminal on trial.

## Contract Amendments

Sometimes circumstances change mid-sprint: you get sick, your work schedule changes, a family situation arises. The contract needs to flex.

**Amendment protocol:**
1. Write down what changed and why the current term is no longer feasible
2. Write the modified term
3. The modification must still be specific and measurable
4. Note the amendment in your review log with the date and reason
5. The amendment takes effect immediately -- don't wait for a new sprint

Example:
- Original term: "I will exercise 4 days per week"
- Situation: Sprained ankle
- Amendment: "I will do seated upper body exercises 3 days per week until cleared for full activity"

This is not cheating. This is responsible contract management. A good contract adapts to reality. A brittle contract breaks and gets abandoned.

## Three Example Contracts

### Example 1: Alex, 23, Just Out of College

**Situation:** $28,000 in student loans, working first full-time job at $42,000/year, living with roommates, no savings, eating out constantly, spending aimlessly.

```
MY CONTRACT -- VERSION 1.0
Name: Alex
Date: [today]
Next Review: [4 weeks from today]

NET WORTH: -$27,200
MONTHLY SURPLUS: -$180 (spending more than earning)
BIGGEST STRENGTH: Tech-savvy, good at learning
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: No financial awareness, impulse spending
AVOIDING: Looking at student loan balance, budgeting

NON-NEGOTIABLE TERMS:
1. I will track every dollar I spend using [app] every day
2. I will bring lunch to work at least 3 days per week
3. I will be in bed with screens off by 11:30 PM on work nights
4. I will not make any purchase over $30 without waiting 24 hours

SPRINT 1 TARGET:
1. Achieve a positive monthly cash flow (spend less than I earn)
   Metric: Monthly spending < monthly take-home pay

WEEKLY REVIEW: Sundays at 10 AM
```

### Example 2: Maria, 38, Married, Two Kids

**Situation:** Dual income household ($95K combined), $12K credit card debt, $180K mortgage, both parents exhausted, no exercise, marriage under strain from financial stress. One kid in daycare ($1,400/month), one in elementary school.

```
MY CONTRACT -- VERSION 1.0
Name: Maria
Date: [today]
Next Review: [4 weeks from today]

NET WORTH: -$42,000 (mortgage + CC debt - home equity - small 401k)
MONTHLY SURPLUS: $120 (barely positive)
BIGGEST STRENGTH: Organized at work, good under pressure
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: Avoids financial conversations with spouse, stress-eats
AVOIDING: The full debt picture, a real budget with spouse, doctor visit

NON-NEGOTIABLE TERMS:
1. I will track all spending (mine, not trying to control spouse's yet)
2. I will walk for 20 minutes during lunch break on M/W/F
3. I will have one 15-minute money check-in with my spouse per week
   (Sunday evenings after kids are in bed)
4. I will schedule my overdue physical exam within 2 weeks

SPRINT 1 TARGETS:
1. Complete the financial audit with real numbers
   Metric: Full audit document filled out with 3-month averages
2. Identify $200/month in cuttable expenses
   Metric: List of specific cuts totaling $200+

WEEKLY REVIEW: Sunday at 9 PM (after kids' bedtime)
```

### Example 3: James, 52, Starting Over

**Situation:** Recently divorced, moved to apartment, splitting retirement assets. Income: $68,000. Lost half of 401K in divorce ($85K remaining). No other savings. Drinking increased over the last 2 years. 40 lbs overweight. Estranged from adult children. Feels like it's "too late."

```
MY CONTRACT -- VERSION 1.0
Name: James
Date: [today]
Next Review: [4 weeks from today]

NET WORTH: $52,000 (401k minus small debts)
MONTHLY SURPLUS: $400 (reduced expenses post-divorce)
BIGGEST STRENGTH: Work ethic, technical skills, stable income
BIGGEST WEAKNESS: Isolation, using alcohol to cope, health neglect
AVOIDING: Confronting alcohol use honestly, reaching out to kids,
          looking at retirement math

NON-NEGOTIABLE TERMS:
1. I will not drink Sunday through Thursday
2. I will walk for 30 minutes every day (no exceptions, rain or shine)
3. I will cook dinner at home at least 5 nights per week
4. I will attend at least one social activity per week (doesn't matter
   what -- meetup, church, class, volunteer, anything with other humans)

SPRINT 1 TARGETS:
1. Schedule a full physical including blood work
   Metric: Appointment scheduled AND attended
2. Research therapists who take my insurance, schedule first session
   Metric: First session attended
3. Text or call each of my kids at least once this sprint
   Metric: Contact initiated (response is not in my control)

WEEKLY REVIEW: Saturday at 9 AM
```

## Sign It

Print your contract. Or write it by hand. Sign it. Put the date on it. Put it somewhere you'll see it daily -- not in a drawer, not in a file on your computer. On your bathroom mirror. On your refrigerator. On your desk.

This is not a goal list you wrote in a journal. This is a binding agreement with the only person responsible for your life.

Live by it. Review it. Amend it when necessary. Upgrade it when you're ready. But honor it.

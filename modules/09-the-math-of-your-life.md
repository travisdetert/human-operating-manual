## The Hourly Value of Your Life

### Basic Calculation

```
Your annual income (after tax):               $________
Standard work hours per year: 2,080
Basic hourly rate:                             $________
```

### Real Hourly Rate (Adjusted)

Your paycheck doesn't account for all the time you give to work. Include:

```
Average hours at work per day:                 ________
Commute time (round trip):                   + ________
Getting ready for work:                      + ________
Decompression after work:                    + ________
Work done at home (email, prep, thinking):   + ________
TOTAL daily hours given to work:               ________

Total weekly hours for work:                   ________  (x5)
Total annual hours for work:                   ________  (x50)

REAL hourly rate:
  Annual income / actual annual hours:         $________
```

**Example:** Sarah makes $60,000/year after tax.
- Standard calculation: $60,000 / 2,080 = $28.85/hour
- Real calculation: She works 9 hours, commutes 1 hour round trip, spends 30 min getting ready, 30 min decompressing, and 30 min on work email at night = 11.5 hours/day for work
- Real weekly hours: 57.5
- Real annual hours: 2,875
- **Real hourly rate: $20.87/hour**

That's a 28% reduction. Sarah thinks she earns $28.85/hour. She actually earns $20.87 when you account for all the time work consumes.

### Purchases Translated to Life-Hours

Now apply your real hourly rate to your spending:

| Purchase | Cost | Life-Hours Traded (at $20.87/hr) |
|---|---|---|
| Morning coffee + pastry | $9.50 | 0.45 hours (27 minutes of your life) |
| Lunch out | $15 | 0.72 hours (43 minutes) |
| New shoes (impulse) | $120 | 5.7 hours |
| Bar tab (Friday night) | $65 | 3.1 hours |
| Monthly car payment | $450 | 21.6 hours |
| Weekend getaway | $800 | 38.3 hours (nearly a full work week of your life) |
| iPhone upgrade | $1,200 | 57.5 hours |
| Annual unused subscriptions | $2,400 | 115 hours (nearly 3 full work weeks) |

**The reframe:** Every purchase is a trade -- your life-hours for a thing. The question isn't "can I afford it?" The question is "is this worth [X] hours of my life?"

Some things are worth it. A weekend with family? Probably worth 38 hours. Unused subscriptions? 115 hours of your life for nothing.

## The Math of Debt

### How Minimum Payments Actually Work (Detailed)

Let's trace a $5,000 credit card balance at 24% APR with minimum payments (2% of balance or $25, whichever is greater):

| Month | Balance | Min Payment | Interest That Month | Goes to Principal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $5,000 | $100.00 | $100.00 | $0.00 |
| 6 | $4,867 | $97.34 | $97.34 | $0.00 |
| 12 | $4,698 | $93.96 | $93.96 | $0.00 |
| 24 | $4,344 | $86.88 | $86.88 | $0.00 |
| 60 | $3,416 | $68.32 | $68.32 | $0.00 |
| 120 | $1,990 | $39.80 | $39.80 | ... |

In the early months, **virtually 100% of your minimum payment goes to interest.** You're paying $100/month and the balance barely moves. After 5 years of payments totaling $4,500, you still owe $3,416. After 10 years and $6,500 in payments, you still owe nearly $2,000.

**Total time to pay off: approximately 27 years.**
**Total paid: approximately $12,700.**
**Interest paid: $7,700 on a $5,000 balance.**

This is not an anomaly. This is how credit card math works. By design.

### The Debt Payoff Accelerator

What happens when you pay more than the minimum:

| Monthly Payment | Time to Pay Off $5,000 at 24% | Total Interest Paid |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum (~$100) | 27+ years | $7,700 |
| $150 | 4 years, 8 months | $3,400 |
| $200 | 2 years, 10 months | $2,200 |
| $300 | 1 year, 9 months | $1,300 |
| $500 | 11 months | $700 |

**Going from $100/month to $200/month -- an extra $100 -- saves you $5,500 in interest and 24 years of payments.** That extra $100/month is worth $5,500. Where else can you get a guaranteed 55x return?

### The Debt Avalanche vs. Snowball: Real Numbers

**Scenario:** You have three debts:
- Credit Card A: $3,000 at 26% APR, min $60
- Credit Card B: $7,000 at 22% APR, min $140
- Student Loan: $15,000 at 6% APR, min $167
- Total minimum payments: $367/month
- Extra money available: $200/month
- Total allocated: $567/month

**Avalanche (highest rate first):**
Extra $200 goes to Card A (26%) while minimums on everything else.
- Card A paid off: Month 12
- Card B paid off: Month 27
- Student Loan paid off: Month 48
- Total interest paid: **$6,800**

**Snowball (smallest balance first):**
Extra $200 goes to Card A ($3,000 smallest) while minimums on everything else.
- Card A paid off: Month 12 (same -- it's both smallest AND highest rate here)
- Card B paid off: Month 29
- Student Loan paid off: Month 49
- Total interest paid: **$7,100**

**Difference: $300** in this scenario. In many real-world cases, the difference is $500-$2,000. Not trivial, but not life-changing. **The best method is the one you'll stick with.**

## The Math of Raising Your Income

### The Compounding Value of a Raise

A $5,000 raise is not worth $5,000. It compounds:

| Year | Annual Salary (3% raises on $50K base) | Annual Salary (3% raises on $55K base) | Annual Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $50,000 | $55,000 | $5,000 |
| 5 | $56,275 | $61,903 | $5,628 |
| 10 | $65,240 | $71,764 | $6,524 |
| 15 | $75,629 | $83,192 | $7,563 |
| 20 | $87,675 | $96,442 | $8,767 |
| 25 | $101,640 | $111,804 | $10,164 |
| 30 | $117,839 | $129,623 | $11,784 |

**Cumulative extra earnings over 30 years: approximately $230,000.**

And that's just salary. It doesn't include the higher 401(k) match (usually a percentage of salary), the higher bonus (if percentage-based), or the higher Social Security benefits.

A single $5,000 salary negotiation is worth a quarter million dollars over a career. This is why Module 11 (Career & Skill Capital) emphasizes negotiation. One awkward 15-minute conversation = $230,000.

| Investment | Typical Cost | Typical Income Increase | Payback Period |
|---|---|---|---|

| Coding bootcamp | $10,000-$20,000 | $20,000-$50,000/year (career change) | 6-12 months |

### The Cost of Staying in a Dead-End Job

If your income is stagnant -- no raises, no advancement, no skill development -- the cost isn't just the missing raises. It's:

- **Inflation erosion:** At 3% inflation, your $50,000 salary is worth $44,632 in 3 years, $37,129 in 10 years. You're getting a pay CUT every year you don't get a raise
- **Skill depreciation:** Skills that aren't developed atrophy and become less marketable
- **Opportunity cost:** Every year in a dead-end role is a year not spent building skill capital in a growing field
- **Psychological cost:** Stagnation erodes self-worth, motivation, and mental health

## The Math of Health

### Lifetime Cost of Preventable Conditions

| Condition | Lifetime Additional Medical Cost | Lost Earnings | Total Lifetime Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Smoking (lifetime) | $200,000+ (medical) | $150,000+ (premature death, disability) | $350,000+ (plus $100,000+ in purchases) |
| Obesity (BMI 30+) | $150,000-$250,000 | $50,000-$100,000 (lower wages, missed work) | $200,000-$350,000 |
| Type 2 Diabetes | $250,000-$400,000 (lifetime medical) | $100,000+ | $350,000-$500,000 |
| Sedentary lifestyle | $100,000-$200,000 (associated conditions) | $50,000+ | $150,000-$250,000 |
| Alcohol use disorder | $150,000-$300,000 (medical + legal) | $100,000-$200,000 | $250,000-$500,000 |
| Untreated hypertension | $100,000-$300,000 (heart disease, stroke) | $50,000-$200,000 | $150,000-$500,000 |

### The Financial Value of Each Healthy Year

Research estimates the economic value of one additional year of healthy life at $100,000-$150,000 (combining earning capacity, medical cost avoidance, and quality of life). By this metric:

- Quitting smoking (adds ~10 years): $1,000,000-$1,500,000 in value
- Reaching healthy weight (adds ~5-8 years): $500,000-$1,200,000
- Regular exercise (adds ~3-5 years): $300,000-$750,000
- Moderate alcohol (vs. heavy, adds ~5-10 years): $500,000-$1,500,000

**Exercise is the single cheapest, most effective health intervention available.** It costs nothing (walking is free) and returns hundreds of thousands in lifetime value.

## The Bottom Line

The math doesn't change because you don't look at it. Your debt accrues interest whether you check the statement or not. Your time runs out whether you track it or not. Your health declines whether you monitor it or not.

But the math also works in your favor the moment you start paying attention. $200/month becomes $352,000. One salary negotiation becomes $230,000. Quitting smoking becomes $450,000 in purchases and medical costs. 20 minutes of daily walking becomes years of additional healthy life.

**Small, consistent, mathematically-informed decisions compound into transformative outcomes.** That's not motivation. That's arithmetic.

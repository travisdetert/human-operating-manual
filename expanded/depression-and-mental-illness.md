# The Depression Contract

### Running an Operating System When the Power Keeps Going Out

---

## What Depression Actually Is

Depression is not sadness. Sadness is a normal emotion with a cause and a resolution. Depression is a neurological condition that distorts perception, depletes motivation, impairs cognition, and convinces you -- with absolute certainty -- that nothing will ever get better.

That certainty is a lie. It is a symptom, not a fact. But it doesn't feel like a symptom. It feels like the truth. That's what makes depression so dangerous and so difficult to fight -- you're trying to fix a problem using a brain that the problem has compromised.

### What's Happening in the Brain

In plain language:

**Neurotransmitter disruption:** Serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine -- the chemicals responsible for mood regulation, motivation, energy, and pleasure -- are either underproduced, poorly received, or metabolized too quickly. The result: things that used to feel good don't. Things that used to feel manageable feel impossible. Things that used to matter don't.

**Prefrontal cortex suppression:** The part of your brain responsible for planning, decision-making, and future-orientation goes quiet. This is why depressed people can't "just plan ahead" or "think positive." The planning hardware is running at reduced capacity.

**Amygdala hyperactivation:** The threat-detection center is overactive. Everything feels dangerous, overwhelming, or threatening. Getting out of bed isn't lazy -- it's a brain that's screaming that the world is hostile and the best survival strategy is to stay still.

**Hippocampus changes:** Memory and learning are impaired. You forget appointments, lose track of conversations, can't retain what you read. This isn't stupidity. It's a brain under siege redirecting resources to survival.

**Inflammation:** Mounting research links depression to chronic neuroinflammation. The brain is physically inflamed, which impairs function across all systems. This is why depression is exhausting -- your body is fighting an internal inflammatory response 24/7.

**This is not a character flaw. This is a medical condition.** Telling a depressed person to "just be positive" is like telling a person with a broken leg to "just walk it off." The hardware is damaged. It needs treatment, not motivation.

## How Depression Sabotages the Contract

Depression attacks the contract at every level:

| Contract Component | How Depression Attacks It |
|---|---|
| **The Honest Audit** | Depression says: "Why bother looking? You already know it's bad. Looking will make it worse. You can't fix it anyway." |
| **Financial terms** | Executive function impairment makes tracking, planning, and managing money feel impossible. Impulse spending or complete financial neglect (not opening bills, not checking accounts) |
| **Physical terms** | Getting out of bed is the obstacle. Exercise feels absurd. Cooking feels like climbing Everest. Sleep is either 14 hours or 3 hours with nothing in between |
| **Habit engineering** | Dopamine depletion means the reward system doesn't fire. You complete the habit and feel... nothing. No satisfaction. No win. Just empty completion. So why bother? |
| **Sprint cycles** | Planning 4 weeks ahead requires a prefrontal cortex that's online. Depression takes it offline. The future doesn't feel real -- only the current moment of suffering feels real |
| **Weekly review** | Looking at your compliance rate when depressed can trigger a shame spiral. "See? I'm failing at this too." The review becomes evidence against yourself |
| **Breach protocol** | Every breach feels like proof of worthlessness rather than a diagnostic data point. The inner critic (not the inner auditor) takes over completely |

### The Withdrawal Loop

Depression creates a self-reinforcing cycle:

```
Depression reduces energy and motivation
  └─> You stop doing things (exercise, socializing, working, self-care)
       └─> Isolation and inactivity worsen depression
            └─> Even less energy and motivation
                 └─> You stop doing more things
                      └─> Depression deepens further
                           └─> Eventually you're barely functioning
                                └─> Now the climb back is steeper
                                     └─> Depression says: "See? It's hopeless"
```

**Every intervention in this module is designed to interrupt this loop.** Not to cure depression -- that requires professional treatment. But to prevent the spiral from reaching the bottom.

## The Depression Contract: A Modified Framework

When you're depressed, the standard contract is too much. The V0.5 Crisis Contract is the starting point. But depression has specific characteristics that require specific modifications.

### Principle 1: The Bar Is on the Floor -- And That's Correct

Your contract terms during a depressive episode should be embarrassingly simple to a healthy person. That's by design. You are not a healthy person right now. You are a person running on 30% power, and your terms should reflect that.

**Examples of appropriately-sized depression contract terms:**

- "I will get out of bed and move to another room by [time]" (not "I will be productive")
- "I will eat one real meal today" (not "I will meal prep and eat clean")
- "I will go outside for 5 minutes" (not "I will walk for 30 minutes")
- "I will shower today" (not "I will maintain a full hygiene routine")
- "I will reply to one text message" (not "I will socialize and maintain relationships")
- "I will take my medication" (not negotiable, ever)
- "I will attend my therapy appointment" (not negotiable, ever)

**If you met these terms today, you succeeded.** Not partially. Not "it's a start." You succeeded. Depression told you nothing was possible, and you did something. That is a win.

### Principle 2: Medication and Therapy Are Term #1

If you are experiencing clinical depression, the contract's first non-negotiable term is **pursuing and maintaining professional treatment.** This is not optional. This is not "when I feel up to it." This is the foundation.

**Medication:**

Antidepressants work. Not for everyone, and not always the first one you try. But across hundreds of clinical trials, they are demonstrably more effective than placebo for moderate to severe depression.

What to know:
- **They take 2-6 weeks to reach full effect.** This is agonizing when you're suffering now, but the neurochemistry needs time to recalibrate
- **Side effects are common in the first 1-2 weeks** and usually diminish. If they don't, tell your doctor -- there are many options to try
- **You may need to try 2-3 medications** before finding the right one. This is normal, not a sign that "nothing works for you"
- **Do not stop suddenly.** Discontinuation must be tapered under medical supervision. Abruptly stopping SSRIs/SNRIs can cause withdrawal symptoms
- **Taking medication is not weakness.** Your brain has a chemical imbalance. The medication corrects it. A diabetic isn't "weak" for taking insulin. You are not "weak" for taking sertraline

Common types:
- **SSRIs** (sertraline/Zoloft, fluoxetine/Prozac, escitalopram/Lexapro): Most commonly prescribed, fewest side effects for most people
- **SNRIs** (venlafaxine/Effexor, duloxetine/Cymbalta): Also addresses norepinephrine; sometimes better for depression with fatigue or pain
- **Bupropion/Wellbutrin:** Different mechanism; often used when SSRIs cause sexual side effects or weight gain. Also helps with focus and energy
- **Atypicals and augmentation:** Your doctor may combine medications or use newer options based on your specific presentation

**Contract term:** "I will take [medication] every day at [time]. I will keep all psychiatrist/prescriber appointments. I will report side effects within 48 hours rather than stopping on my own."

**Therapy:**

For depression specifically:
- **CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy):** Identifies and restructures distorted thinking patterns. Depression lies -- CBT teaches you to catch the lies. Research shows CBT is as effective as medication for mild-to-moderate depression, and the combination of CBT + medication is more effective than either alone
- **Behavioral Activation:** A component of CBT that specifically targets the withdrawal loop. Instead of waiting to "feel like" doing things, you schedule small activities and do them regardless of mood. This interrupts the inactivity-depression cycle
- **IPT (Interpersonal Therapy):** Focuses on relationship patterns and social functioning. Effective for depression triggered by grief, role transitions, or interpersonal conflict
- **Psychodynamic Therapy:** Explores deeper patterns and origins. Useful for chronic, recurring depression with roots in early experiences

**Contract term:** "I will attend therapy [weekly/biweekly]. If I can't afford it, I will contact [EAP / Open Path / community mental health center / sliding scale provider] within one week."

### Principle 3: Movement Is Medicine (Literally)

Exercise is one of the most evidence-backed interventions for depression. A 2023 meta-analysis in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found that exercise was **1.5x more effective than medication or therapy alone** for reducing depression symptoms.

But when you're depressed, "exercise" sounds impossible. So don't call it exercise:

**The 5-Minute Rule:** "I will put on shoes and go outside for 5 minutes." That's the term. Not 30 minutes. Not a jog. Five minutes of standing outside, walking to the mailbox, walking to the end of the block. If you do more, great. If you do 5 minutes and come back inside, you met the term.

**Why 5 minutes works:** It circumvents the activation energy problem. Starting is the barrier, not continuing. Most people who make it outside for 5 minutes end up walking for 10-15. And even 5 minutes of sunlight and movement produces measurable changes in cortisol, serotonin, and endorphin levels.

**If you can't get outside:** Stand up. Walk to the kitchen. Do 5 wall push-ups. Stretch for 3 minutes. Any movement breaks the stasis. Depression wants you horizontal and still. Vertical and moving -- even briefly -- is resistance.

### Principle 4: Eat Something Real

Depression disrupts appetite in both directions -- you either can't eat at all or you eat everything in sight (typically high-sugar, high-carb comfort foods that provide a brief dopamine spike followed by a crash that worsens depression).

The nutrition contract term during depression is not about macros, meal prep, or optimization. It's about basic fuel:

**"I will eat at least one meal today that contains protein and a vegetable."**

That's it. Eggs and spinach. A can of soup with some chicken. A peanut butter sandwich and an apple. Yogurt and a banana. Something that gives your brain actual building blocks for neurotransmitter production (protein provides tryptophan, the precursor to serotonin) instead of just sugar.

If all you can manage is cereal, eat cereal. Eating something is better than eating nothing. But when you can manage it, one real meal makes a biochemical difference.

### Principle 5: Connection Is an Antidepressant

Depression lies about relationships: "Nobody wants to hear from you." "You're a burden." "They're just being polite." "They'd be better off without you."

These are symptoms. Not facts.

Social isolation is both a symptom and a cause of depression. Breaking isolation -- even in small ways -- disrupts the cycle:

**Contract terms for connection:**
- "I will reply to one text message today" (not initiate a conversation -- just reply to one)
- "I will attend [one standing commitment] this week, even if I don't want to" (therapy counts, a support group counts, a family dinner counts)
- "I will tell one person how I'm actually doing" (not "I'm fine" -- the real answer)
- "I will not cancel plans more than once per week due to depression" (this one is hard but important -- canceling becomes its own habit)

**Who to tell:**
Not everyone needs to know you're depressed. But at least one person should -- a therapist, a close friend, a family member, a crisis counselor. Depression feeds on secrecy and isolation. Saying "I'm struggling" out loud, to another human, reduces its power.

### Principle 6: Sleep Regulation (Not Just "More Sleep")

Depression distorts sleep in two ways:

**Hypersomnia (too much):** Sleeping 10-14 hours and still feeling exhausted. The sleep is low-quality and fragmented. Your body stays in bed because the brain has nowhere it wants to go. This feels like tiredness but is actually anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure or motivation).

**Insomnia (too little):** Can't fall asleep because your mind is racing with anxious or hopeless thoughts. Or you fall asleep but wake at 3 AM with dread and can't return to sleep. Sleep deprivation then worsens depression symptoms the next day.

**Contract terms:**
- "I will set an alarm and get out of bed by [time] regardless of how I feel" (for hypersomnia -- staying in bed past a certain point is the depression, not rest)
- "I will not nap after 2 PM" (late naps destroy nighttime sleep)
- "Screens off by [time]. If I can't sleep, I will listen to a podcast or audiobook rather than scrolling" (for insomnia -- scrolling activates the brain; passive audio allows wind-down)

## Anxiety: Depression's Co-Pilot

Anxiety and depression co-occur in roughly **60% of cases.** They are frequently experienced simultaneously or in alternation: depressive episodes where you can't get out of bed, then anxious episodes where you can't stop the racing thoughts.

### How Anxiety Interacts With the Contract

**Analysis paralysis:** You can't choose contract terms because every option feels wrong. What if you pick the wrong ones? What if you fail? What if this makes things worse?

**Response:** Pick anything. Literally anything from the starter list. The contract is versioned -- you can change it in 4 weeks. A imperfect contract executed is infinitely better than a perfect contract imagined.

**Catastrophic thinking:** "If I look at my bank account, I'll see how bad it is, and then I'll panic, and then I won't be able to function." Your brain presents a cascade of worst-case scenarios that prevent you from taking the first step.

**Response:** Set a specific time, a specific place, and a specific duration. "I will look at my bank balance on Saturday at 10 AM for 5 minutes. I will write down the number. Then I will close the app and go for a walk." Containing the anxiety by containing the activity.

**Avoidance:** Anxiety and avoidance are partners. You avoid checking your health, your finances, your obligations -- not because you're lazy but because approaching them triggers a physiological panic response (elevated heart rate, tight chest, racing thoughts).

**Response:** The Avoidance Audit (Module 05) is especially critical for anxiety. Name what you're avoiding. Approach ONE item. Set the smallest possible first step. Do it with someone if possible (a friend sitting next to you while you make the phone call, a therapist helping you write the email).

### Anxiety-Specific Contract Terms

- "When I feel anxious about a task, I will set a timer for 5 minutes and do only 5 minutes of it"
- "I will practice one grounding exercise when anxiety spikes" (5-4-3-2-1: name 5 things you see, 4 you hear, 3 you feel, 2 you smell, 1 you taste)
- "I will not make decisions while anxious. I will wait until the spike passes, then decide"
- "I will limit news/social media to [X] minutes per day" (anxiety feeds on information overload)
- "I will tell my therapist about the specific things I'm avoiding, even if talking about them feels hard"

## Seasonal Depression (SAD)

If your depression follows a seasonal pattern -- typically worsening in fall/winter and improving in spring/summer -- you may have Seasonal Affective Disorder. This is a physiological response to reduced sunlight, which affects melatonin and serotonin production.

**Specific interventions:**
- **Light therapy:** A 10,000 lux light box, used for 20-30 minutes each morning, is the primary treatment. These cost $30-$80 and have strong clinical evidence. Use it within the first hour of waking.
- **Vitamin D:** Most people in northern latitudes are deficient in winter. 2,000-5,000 IU/day is commonly recommended (check with your doctor). Deficiency is linked to depression symptoms.
- **Front-load outdoor time:** Get outside in the first 2 hours of daylight, even if it's cold, even if it's cloudy. Natural light, even on overcast days, is 5-10x brighter than indoor lighting.
- **Anticipate it:** If you know your depression follows a pattern, prepare before it hits. Increase therapy frequency in October. Start the light box before symptoms arrive. Adjust contract terms preemptively rather than reactively.

**Contract term:** "I will use my light box every morning October through March for 20 minutes."

## Grief-Related Depression

Grief and depression overlap but aren't identical. Grief is a normal, healthy response to loss that typically comes in waves -- you feel devastated, then okay, then devastated again. Depression is a persistent, unrelenting state.

Grief can trigger depression. When it does, the grief doesn't need to be "fixed" (it needs to be felt), but the depression does need treatment.

**Key differences:**
- **Grief:** Sadness comes in waves, often triggered by reminders. You can still experience moments of joy or connection. You know why you're sad.
- **Depression:** Sadness is constant and pervasive. Joy is absent or inaccessible. You may not be able to identify why you feel this way.
- **Complicated grief:** When grief doesn't follow the expected trajectory -- when it intensifies rather than gradually becoming manageable, when it prevents basic functioning for months, when it triggers persistent suicidal thoughts -- it has become a clinical condition requiring professional help.

**Contract approach for grief:** See the Grief Contract in the Crisis module. Key terms: allow yourself to grieve, maintain minimum physical function, don't make major decisions, accept help, seek professional support if grief becomes debilitating.

## Bipolar Disorder: The Oscillating OS

Bipolar disorder adds a dimension the standard contract doesn't account for: mania (or hypomania). During manic episodes, you feel invincible, need little sleep, have racing thoughts, make impulsive decisions, and may take on enormous commitments. During depressive episodes, everything in this module applies.

**The bipolar-specific danger:** During mania, you might write a V5.0 contract with 15 terms, quit your job to start a business, spend $3,000 you don't have, and feel absolutely certain it's all brilliant. Then the depressive phase hits and you're dealing with the wreckage.

**Bipolar contract modifications:**

- **Medication is the entire foundation.** Mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate, lamotrigine) and/or atypical antipsychotics are not optional. They are the kernel of the OS. Without them, nothing is stable
- **The contract has two versions:** One for baseline (stable mood) and one for episode management (both manic and depressive). Both are written during a stable period, not during an episode
- **Mania safeguards:** "During elevated mood, I will not make financial decisions over $[X], start new projects, quit my job, or make relationship changes without discussing with [therapist/trusted person] first and waiting 72 hours"
- **Early warning system:** Know your mania and depression warning signs. Write them down. Share them with someone who sees you regularly. Catching an episode early allows for medication adjustment before full-blown crisis
- **Financial protection:** Consider giving a trusted person co-signing authority on accounts, or setting daily spending limits that you cannot change without a waiting period. This is not paternalistic -- it is self-aware engineering

## When Someone You Love Is Depressed

If you're reading this because someone in your life has depression, here's what helps and what doesn't:

**What helps:**
- Showing up. Not with advice. Just being there.
- Specific offers, not open-ended ones. "I'm bringing you dinner at 6" is better than "Let me know if you need anything" (they won't let you know -- depression prevents asking for help)
- Maintaining contact even when they don't respond. Text anyway. They read it even when they can't reply
- Going with them to appointments if they ask
- Not taking their withdrawal personally. It's the depression, not you
- Saying: "I'm not going anywhere" and meaning it

**What doesn't help:**
- "Just think positive" / "Choose happiness" / "Other people have it worse"
- Tough love. Depression is not a motivation problem
- Trying to fix them. You're not their therapist
- Taking over their life. They need agency, even when exercising it is hard
- Getting frustrated when they "don't get better fast enough." Depression doesn't follow your timeline
- Ignoring talk of suicide or self-harm. Always take it seriously. Always.

## The Hardest Truth and the Most Important One

Depression tells you it will always be this way. It tells you this is permanent. It tells you nothing will help. It tells you you're broken beyond repair.

**Depression is lying.**

The evidence: the majority of people with depression improve with treatment. Not all at once. Not in a straight line. But measurably, documentably, verifiably. The brain that depression has compromised is also the brain that can heal, adapt, and rebuild -- with the right support, the right treatment, and the right structure.

The contract can't cure depression. But it can give you one thing depression tries to take: **the belief that what you do matters.**

It does. Even when you can't feel it. Especially when you can't feel it.

Take your medication. Go to your appointment. Get out of bed. Eat something real. Step outside for 5 minutes.

That's today's contract. Tomorrow, you do it again.
